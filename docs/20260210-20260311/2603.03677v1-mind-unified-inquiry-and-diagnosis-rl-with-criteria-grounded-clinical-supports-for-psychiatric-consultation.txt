## Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) have advanced medical dialogue systems, yet psychiatric consultation poses substantially higher de-mands due to subjective ambiguity and comorbidity complexity: an agent must continuously extract psychopathological cues from in-complete and inconsistent patient reports in multi-turn interactions and perform rigorous differential diagnostic reasoning. However, existing methods face two fundamental challenges. First, without criteria-grounded clinical supports, they are prone to unsupported clinical assertions when symptoms are atypical or underspecified. Second, in multi-turn interactions, they struggle to mitigate inquiry drift (off-topic or low-yield questioning) and optimize questioning strategies. To address these challenges, we propose MIND, a unified inquiry–diagnosis reinforcement learning framework for psychi-atric consultation. Specifically, we build a Criteria-Grounded Psy-chiatric Reasoning Bank (PRB) that summarizes dialogue context into clinical retrieval states, retrieves semantically similar refer-ence consultations, and distills reusable criteria-grounded clinical supports to guide criteria-aligned inquiry and reasoning. Building on this foundation, MIND enforces explicit clinical reasoning with rubric-based process rewards to provide fine-grained supervision over intermediate decision steps, and incorporates a value-aware trajectory rectification mechanism to jointly improve information acquisition and diagnostic decision-making across turns. Exten-sive experiments demonstrate that MIND consistently outperforms strong baselines in diagnostic accuracy, empathetic interaction quality, interpretability, and generalization.

## 1 Introduction

Effective agents must map free-form descriptions to structured signals, justify discriminative questioning, mitigate inquiry drift, and maintain focus on informative cues across multi-turn interactions. Prior research enhances LLM consultation via optimized ques-tioning and diagnosis. Many treat consultation as a sequential deci-sion process to reduce uncertainty [ 51 , 65 ]; for instance, Dr.APP [ 65 ]utilizes guideline-informed, uncertainty-aware follow-up selection. Despite progress, LLM-based psychiatric consultation faces two fundamental challenges. First, models produce untraceable asser-tions under atypical symptoms. By imitating clinician style [ 29 , 45 ]rather than anchoring reasoning to diagnostic thresholds and exclu-sion rules [ 21 , 33 , 52 ], systems often ignore key criteria (e.g., func-tional impairment in Figure 5(a)), causing confident but unverifiable attribution errors. Second, models struggle to sustain diagnostic focus and optimize multi-turn questioning. Amidst noisy signals and sparse feedback, agents often prioritize generic support over informative probing [ 13 , 29 ]. As Figure 5(b) illustrates, failing to distinguish symptoms like "flight of ideas" from "work stress" leads to inquiry drift, inefficient information acquisition, and diminished diagnostic reliability. Our motivation stems from two observations: First, providing LLMs with task-relevant knowledge improves medical performance and reduces hallucinations [ 9, 49 , 54 , 57 ]. We formulate psychiatric consultation as criteria-grounded reasoning, introducing retrieval to provide decision cues for subsequent diagnostic steps. As shown in Figure 2(a), whereas generic models often overlook key thresh-olds (e.g., functional impairment) in ambiguous reports, MIND re-trieves supports from the PRB to prompt criterion-targeted follow-ups (e.g., symptom duration), converting intuition into criteria-aligned reasoning. Second, sparse outcome-level feedback in multi-turn dialogues hinders credit assignment and causes inquiry drift. We incorporate value-aware trajectory rectification to handle in-complete or inconsistent narratives. As illustrated in Figure 2(b), while generic systems often derail when symptoms are conflated (e.g., "flight of ideas" vs. "work stress"), MIND employs rubric-based process rewards and trajectory rectification to prioritize discrimi-native questioning (e.g., "most impairing symptom"), thereby en-hancing diagnostic progress. To address these challenges, we propose MIND, an evidence-grounded and process-supervised unified inquiry–diagnosis rein-forcement learning framework for psychiatric consultation. First, MIND constructs a Criteria-Grounded Psychiatric Reasoning Bank (PRB). It summarizes multi-turn dialogue context into a clinical re-trieval state, retrieves semantically similar reference consultations, and distills criteria-grounded supports (in the form of clinical ratio-nales) to guide criteria-aligned inquiry and differential diagnosis under incomplete and inconsistent patient reports. Second, to im-prove decision-making in multi-turn interactions, MIND introduces rubric-based process supervision over intermediate reasoning and decision steps. By enforcing explicit reasoning traces (e.g., symptom analysis and differential diagnosis) and applying rubric-based pro-cess rewards, the framework provides fine-grained guidance at each turn. Furthermore, we incorporate a value-aware trajectory recti-fication mechanism that detects low-utility turns and adaptively applies self-retry or a PRB-guided fallback strategy to mitigate in-quiry drift, thereby jointly improving information acquisition and diagnostic decision-making. This paper makes four major contributions: (1) We introduce MIND, an evidence-grounded reinforcement learning framework for multi-turn psychiatric consultation that jointly optimizes in-quiry policies and diagnostic decisions via retrieval augmentation and mixed rewards, improving diagnostic accuracy and overall performance. (2) We propose a Criteria-Grounded Psychiatric Rea-soning Bank (PRB). By summarizing dialogue context into clinical retrieval states and retrieving criteria-grounded supports (in the form of clinical rationales), we facilitate criteria-aligned inquiry and differential diagnosis. (3) We design a process-supervised RL framework that couples explicit reasoning traces with rubric-based process rewards, and further integrates a value-aware trajectory rec-tification mechanism with self-retry and a PRB-guided fallback strat-egy to mitigate inquiry drift and improve information acquisition in challenging multi-turn interactions. (4) Extensive evaluation on psychiatric consultation benchmarks shows that MIND consistently outperforms strong baselines in diagnostic accuracy, empathetic interaction quality, and reasoning interpretability, demonstrating its effectiveness for complex, multi-turn clinical dialogues.

## 2.2 LLMs for Medical Decision-Making 

LLMs demonstrate strong potential in history taking, diagnosis, and treatment planning [ 16 , 18 , 20 , 35 , 37 , 64 ], with reliability enhanced through prompt engineering and domain adaptation [ 27 , 42 , 47 , 62 ]. Research has shifted from passive QA toward proactive, multi-turn consultation and sequential decision-making [ 31 , 43 ], utilizing multi-agent collaboration and specialized evaluation [ 28 , 59 ]. Con-currently, RL optimizes interaction strategies and reasoning using clinically motivated rewards and verification signals [ 3, 22 , 34 , 36 ]. Building on these, we target psychiatric consultation via grounded reasoning and stable multi-turn optimization. 

## 2.3 RAG for Medical Decision-Making 

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) improves medical LLM fac-tuality and reliability by grounding outputs in clinical guidelines and references [ 2, 15 , 25 ]. While RAG surfaces cues for differential diagnosis and inquiry to reduce hallucinations, direct retrieval from psychiatric narratives is often unreliable due to their metaphorical and underspecified nature. To address this, we propose using struc-tured intermediate representations for controlled signal injection. Specifically, we leverage criteria-grounded supports as decision cues for criteria-aligned reasoning and questioning, complement-ing existing psychiatric consultation frameworks [43, 58].

## 4 Methodology 

This section presents MIND for multi-turn psychiatric consulta-tion, as shown in Figure 3. We model unified inquiry–diagnosis as a task-oriented multi-turn dialogue problem. To minimize un-supported clinical assertions, we construct a Criteria-Grounded Psychiatric Reasoning Bank (PRB) organized by standardized criteria. Each PRB entry consolidates multi-source knowledge (e.g., guidelines, textbooks) into reusable support notes specifying infor-mation gaps, threshold/exclusion checks, and question rationales. At each turn, MIND distills the dialogue history into a clinical retrieval state to fetch semantically similar PRB contexts, inject-ing associated supports as inquiry hints. Conditioned on these hints, the agent generates explicit reasoning—mapping "known information–key gap–rationale"—before responding. We further implement rubric-based process supervision , utilizing an LLM judge to provide dense rewards for symptom analysis and diagnos-tic logic. Finally, a value-aware trajectory rectification mecha-nism monitors turn-level utility, triggering self-retry or PRB-guided Conference acronym ’XX, June 03–05, 2018, Woodstock, NY Guoyi Li et al. 

fallback to enhance efficiency and stability. In summary, MIND in-tegrates criteria-grounded retrieval, explicit reasoning with process supervision, and value-aware rectification.

This framework transforms noisy narra-tives into a standardized retrieval interface and converts clinical checks into RL-optimizable turn-level process signals.

## 4.3 Value-Aware Trajectory Rectification 

To mitigate inquiry loops and off-topic drift in noisy psychiatric consultations, we introduce value-aware trajectory rectification. This mechanism triggers interventions based on rule-checked util-ity signals (e.g., duplication, formatting, budget), which serve as penalty signals to stabilize training and ensure dialogue auditability. 

4.3.1 Trajectory Correction. When an action exhibits low utility, the system triggers self-retry under stricter constraints. Persistent failures invoke PRB-guided fallback, utilizing SCID-5-style refer-ence inquiries (e.g., severity, risk) from clinical priors. ## 4.5 Patient Simulation 

During training, we reconstruct structured patient profiles from the original psychiatric consultation records and employ a patient simulator to produce turn-level patient responses conditioned on (i) the profile and (ii) the dialogue history, following common practice in multi-turn medical consultation learning [ 17 , 46 , 58 ]. Formally, given the current dialogue context 𝐻 𝑡 and the clinician action ˜𝑎 𝑡 , the simulator generates the next patient utterance 𝑢 𝑡 +1 and updates the state to 𝐻 𝑡 +1, which is then used by the doctor policy for subsequent decisions. To support robust optimization and generalization under simu-lator shift, we instantiate two complementary simulators: PsySim-Std and PsySim-Adapt . PsySim-Std prioritizes faithful and stable responses by strictly adhering to the reconstructed profile and pre-viously stated information, reducing stochastic artifacts in training trajectories. PsySim-Adapt introduces controlled narrative varia-tion (e.g., paraphrasing style and mild ambiguity) while preserving profile-consistency and label-consistency, thereby better approxi-mating realistic psychiatric narratives and stress-testing the doc-tor’s inquiry policy. We impose lightweight constraints to ensure clinical plausibility and to prevent degenerate behaviors, including: (a) consistency with the profile and dialogue memory, and (b) avoidance of repetitive or templated turns. When an inquired item is not explicitly annotated in the profile, the simulator performs weak inference using the profile label and clinical priors, then writes the inferred status into the dialogue memory to enforce cross-turn consistency, mitigating uncertainty bias from unrecorded symptoms. This simulator consti-tutes the environment of our GRPO optimization: transitions and rewards are computed on simulated interactions, and all actions, simulator responses, and turn-level rewards are logged into training trajectories. Finally, we calibrate the simulator toward real-patient-like be-haviors and evaluate it under a multi-dimensional protocol inspired by [ 24 ]. Specifically, we assess Information Control (IC), Response Completeness (RC), Factual Conflict (FC), and Human-likeness (HL), where the scoring is verified by both an LLM judge and domain experts. Results in Table 1 demonstrate that our patient simulation achieves strong overall quality under these criteria. 

## 5 Experiments 

Datasets. To evaluate MIND, we utilize 1,000 de-identified EMRs with structured psychiatric descriptions (complaints, symptoms, severity, and risks). Cases are categorized into Depression, Anx-iety, Mixed Anxiety–Depression, and Other, aligned with ICD-10 [ 32 ] (distribution: 335/200/265/200). Following MDD-5k pro-tocols, we reconstruct patient profiles, simulate multi-turn con-sultations, and ensure split isolation (Table 6). Given the Chinese-centric clinical resources, we release Chinese data and models at https://anonymous.4open.science/status/MIND-83DF. 

Baselines. We compare against inference-only models: GPT-4o [ 14 ], DeepSeek-V3 [ 26 ], Qwen3 (4B, 8B, 32B) [ 56 ], Baichuan-M2 [ 10 ], DDT [ 58 ], and MRD-RAG [ 38 ]. Fine-tuned baselines in-clude Qwen3-4B/8B †, DoctorAgent-RL [ 46 ], and DDO [ 17 ]. † de-notes the use of our SFT warm-start data for controlled comparison. 

Experimental Setup. To optimize multi-turn performance, our model utilizes Qwen3 backbones warm-started via Kimi-K2 dis-tilled SFT (DeepSeek-R1-style), followed by GRPO on 8 ×A100 GPUs. Training rewards prioritize (i) information gain and (ii) clinical rea-soning alignment. During inference, MIND performs turn-level support injection and employs a fallback mechanism to correct low-yield behaviors. The patient is simulated by a fixed Qwen3 (temper-ature = 0) capped at 𝐿 = 10 turns. We report diagnostic accuracy and macro-F1 across two simulators (PsySim-Std and PsySim-Adapt, Table 6) to assess generalization. Support faithfulness is evaluated via DeepSeek-V3 across factual consistency, clinical grounding, and context-fidelity. Appendix B provides implementation details.

Ablation Study. Table 4 reports ablations of MIND and con-trolled PRB-effectiveness comparisons. Adding PRB to strong back-bones (Qwen3-32B and Baichuan-M2) consistently improves Sup-port, Acc, and F1 under both patient simulators, indicating that criteria-grounded PRB supports better anchor multi-turn decisions and reduce unsupported clinical additions. For MIND, removing any key element degrades performance with clear and differenti-ated effects. Disabling PRB yields a moderate but consistent drop in F1 (about 5–6%) and lowers Support, suggesting that turn-wise criteria supports are important for grounding clinical claims and preventing over-interpretation when narratives are incomplete or ambiguous. Removing thinking supervision causes the largest degradation (about 12–14% in F1) together with a notable Support decline, implying that fine-grained process signals are crucial for maintaining clinically decisive checks and stable category bound-aries under simulator shift. Disabling fallback leads to a smaller yet non-trivial decrease (about 3% in F1), mainly reflecting reduced robustness in long-horizon trajectories due to more repetitions and occasional off-track questions. Among optimization components, the SFT warm-start remains critical for stabilizing the two-stage turn structure and preventing early format/behavior drift, while the InfoGain and reasoning rewards further improve information acquisition and clinically aligned decision making. Overall, the full MIND achieves the best results across all three metrics under both simulators.

## 6 Conclusion 

We present MIND, a support-grounded multi-turn psychiatric con-sultation framework that unifies proactive inquiry and diagnosis under criteria-guided supervision. MIND aligns each turn with clin-ically decisive supports and optimizes multi-objective rewards to encourage high-yield questioning, stable reasoning, and natural, empathetic interactions. A lightweight fallback mechanism further corrects low-yield behaviors and improves trajectory reliability. Ex-tensive experiments under two patient simulators show that MIND consistently improves diagnostic accuracy and support faithfulness over strong inference-only, RAG-based, and RL-based baselines, demonstrating stronger overall effectiveness and more dependable medical decision-making. ## A Ethical Considerations 

Large language models may generate fluent but incorrect or un-supported clinical content, especially in high-stakes settings such as psychiatric consultation. Our work is intended for academic research on decision-making and training signals in diagnostic dia-logue. It does not provide medical advice and should not be used as a stand-alone system for real-world clinical decision-making. In practice, diagnosis and treatment must be determined by quali-fied clinicians based on comprehensive assessment and appropriate clinical procedures. To reduce potential misuse, we focus on criteria-grounded sup-ports as decision cues rather than clinical evidence, and we evaluate our method primarily in a controlled simulation environment. The patient simulator and reconstructed profiles are used for research and benchmarking only, and do not represent real patients. Any deployment would require rigorous prospective validation, human oversight, safety monitoring, and compliance with applicable regu-lations and institutional review processes.

Baselines. We organize baselines into four categories.

Medical LLMs (inference-only). We include Qwen3-4B [ 56 ], Qwen3-8B, Qwen3-32B, and Baichuan-M2 [ 10 ] as representative open medical-capable LLMs, evaluated directly under the same psychi-atric consultation protocol to measure domain adaptation ability without additional tuning. 

Retrieval-augmented consultation baselines. DDT [ 58 ] is a guide-line or criteria-tree grounded dialogue baseline that asks questions following structured diagnostic checkpoints, while MRD-RAG [ 38 ]augments multi-turn consultation with retrieved supports to reduce unsupported clinical claims and improve evidence-conditioned de-cisions. 

Fine-tuned and RL-based consultation models. Qwen3-4B † and Qwen3-8B † are instruction-tuned with the same warm-start data as our method for a controlled comparison. DoctorAgent-RL [ 46 ] and DDO [ 17 ] optimize multi-turn inquiry policies with reinforcement learning-style objectives to improve question selection and final diagnosis over multiple rounds. Finally, we report ours (MIND-4B/MIND-8B). “†” denotes using the same instruction-tuning data as our su-pervised warm-start for a controlled comparison. All results are obtained under the most appropriate experimental settings for each method. 

Experimental Setup. Our framework follows a multi-turn doctor– patient dialogue paradigm. The doctor agent is built on Qwen3 backbones and warm-started with supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on our consultation instruction data, yielding MIND-4B and MIND-8B. For SFT, we distill a Kimi-K2 teacher in a DeepSeek-R1-style man-ner to obtain high-quality multi-turn supervision. We then further optimize the doctor agent with GRPO on 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs, where the learning signals are composed of (i) an information-gain reward that encourages high-yield inquiry and (ii) a reasoning-alignment reward that favors clinically decisive checks at each turn. During consultation, MIND performs turn-level retrieval and injec-tion of criteria-grounded supports to anchor the next inquiry step; a lightweight fallback mechanism is enabled to correct low-yield behaviors (e.g., repetitions, format drift, or off-track questions) by re-issuing the turn with support-guided prompts. The patient agent is simulated by a fixed Qwen3 model with deterministic decoding (temperature = 0).
