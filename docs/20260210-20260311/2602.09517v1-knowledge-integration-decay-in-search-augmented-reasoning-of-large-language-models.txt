# Abstract 

Modern Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in com-plex tasks by employing search-augmented rea-soning to incorporate external knowledge into long chains of thought. However, we identify a critical yet underexplored bottleneck in this paradigm, termed Knowledge Integration Decay (KID). Specifically, we observe that as the length of reasoning generated before search grows, mod-els increasingly fail to integrate retrieved ev-idence into subsequent reasoning steps, limit-ing performance even when relevant informa-tion is available. To address this, we propose Self-Anchored Knowledge Encoding (SAKE), a training-free inference-time strategy designed to stabilize knowledge utilization. By anchoring re-trieved knowledge at both the beginning and end of the reasoning process, SAKE prevents it from being overshadowed by prior context, thereby pre-serving its semantic integrity. Extensive experi-ments on multi-hop QA and complex reasoning benchmarks demonstrate that SAKE significantly mitigates KID and improves performance, offer-ing a lightweight yet effective solution for knowl-edge integration in agentic LLMs.

# 2. Related Works 

The fusion of Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022; Kojima et al., 2022) and test-time scaling (Snell et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025) has established inference-time com-pute as a critical scaling dimension alongside parameter growth (Kaplan et al., 2020). This shift has given rise to Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) (Xu et al., 2025; Jaech et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2025; Team, 2024), which leverage extended inference capabilities to exhibit System 2 reason-ing behaviors, such as planning and self-correction (Muen-nighoff et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025d). Consequently, these models have achieved state-of-the-art proficiency in com-plex reasoning domains (Yang et al., 2025; He et al., 2025). 2Knowledge Integration Decay in Search-Augmented Reasoning of LLMs 

To move beyond reliance on fixed end, static parametric knowledge, recent efforts have sought to improve LLM reliability by integrating external knowledge sources via Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)(Huang et al., 2025; Shao et al., 2023; Asai et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2025; Yu et al., 2024). Building upon this foundation, search-augmented rea-soning has emerged as a more advanced framework. This approach utilizes test-time scaling to perform deep reason-ing interleaved with iterative search (Li et al., 2025b; Jin et al., 2025; Song et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025c; Hu et al., 2025), allowing models to dynamically acquire information as needed. Furthermore, this paradigm has naturally evolved into modern agentic LLM systems, which employ a diverse set of tools to solve complex, long-horizon, and real-world tasks (Mialon et al., 2024; Phan et al., 2025; Wei et al., 2025). By leveraging search, planning, and tool-use capabilities, re-cent agentic systems have demonstrated robust performance across a wide range of challenging scenarios (LLC, 2024; OpenAI, 2025; Zheng et al., 2025; Shi et al., 2025; Mei et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025a). Despite the advancements in these works, they largely share a common structural pattern: reasoning is performed first, followed by search, and the retrieved results are subse-quently appended to the ongoing reasoning context. In this work, we identify a fundamental limitation inherent to this sequential reasoning-search-reasoning structure and pro-pose an effective methodology to address it. Our approach complements and strengthens existing research on search-augmented and agentic LLMs, offering a principled direc-tion for building more reliable and robust reasoning systems.

3.2. Empirical Observation 

We investigate the following research question: 

RQ: Can an LLM consistently maintain its ability to un-derstand and utilize retrieved search results throughout a prolonged reasoning process? 

To answer this question, we empirically study the effect of 

pre-search reasoning length on the performance of search-augmented reasoning models. Specifically, our goal is to examine whether increasing the volume of reasoning gen-erated before the search degrades the model’s ability to effectively incorporate external knowledge, even when the retrieved information is sufficient to answer the question. 

Experimental Setup We conduct experiments on the 2WikiMultihopQA benchmark under a controlled, ideal-ized search setting. Specifically, we consider a scenario in which the model first performs a sequence of reasoning steps, invokes the search tool once, and then derives the final answer based on the retrieved evidence. The search-3Knowledge Integration Decay in Search-Augmented Reasoning of LLMs

Following prior work (Li et al., 2025b), we refer to this framework as Search-o1 throughout this paper. The prompt template for Search-o1 is provided in Appendix E. We evaluate three representative open-source large rea-soning models operating under the Search-o1 frame-work: Qwen3-4B-Thinking , Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking ,and QwQ-32B (Team, 2024; Yang et al., 2025). After the search is triggered, we ensure that the search result d1 includes all gold supporting documents for the question. This eliminates retrieval quality as a confounding factor, allowing us to directly measure how effectively the model integrates external knowledge into its reasoning process. Performance is measured by the word-level F1 score between predictions and ground truth. Additional details on setup are provided in Appendix B. 

Observation Figure 2 illustrates the performance trend as the length of pre-search reasoning increases. Across all Search-o1 baseline models, we observe a consistent degra-dation in F1 scores as the amount of reasoning performed before search increases. Notably, this degradation occurs de-spite all relevant documents being explicitly included in the search results, indicating that the performance drop cannot be attributed to retrieval failure. Instead, the results suggest that the model increasingly fails to effectively incorporate external knowledge into its sub-sequent reasoning as the prior reasoning context becomes longer. This reveals a systematic phenomenon, which we term Knowledge Integration Decay ( KID ). We hypothesize that this occurs because extensive earlier reasoning traces r1 tend to dominate the model’s atten-tion during the encoding of d1, thereby biasing its inter-nal representations. As a result, newly retrieved evidence is overshadowed and consequently underutilized—or even ignored—during final answer generation. This motivates the mechanistic analysis in the following section, where we examine how attention dynamics contribute to KID.

# 4. Method 

4.1. Approach of Unbiased Knowledge Encoding 

Building on the empirical observations and mechanistic analysis presented in Section 3, we identify the primary driver of Knowledge Integration Decay (KID) as the exces-sive interference from the reasoning trace ( rt) during the encoding of newly retrieved knowledge ( dt). To address this issue, we introduce unbiased knowledge encoding as a core design principle. This objective is moti-vated by a simple intuition: when humans consult an external document while solving a problem, they do not distort the factual content based on their partial reasoning states. In-stead, they first comprehend the information as-is and only then determine how it should influence their subsequent reasoning steps. In contrast, search-augmented LLMs often conflate these two stages, allowing intermediate reasoning states to interfere with and potentially distort the encoding of external evidence. Formally, we aim to prevent the conditioned representa-tion gθ (dt | rt) from deviating significantly from its intrin-sic encoding gθ (dt) due to interference from the reasoning trace. Importantly, unbiased encoding does not imply that 

gθ (dt | rt) must be identical to gθ (dt) in isolation. While the reasoning context should not override or distort the fac-tual content of dt, it still plays a necessary role in shaping how that knowledge is applied. That is, the reasoning trace should provide functional contextualization without compro-mising the semantic integrity of the retrieved information. This allows the encoded knowledge to serve not merely as an isolated fact, but as a context-aware component that effectively supports the next reasoning step rt+1 .Accordingly, our objective is not to enforce strict condi-tional independence between dt and rt, but rather to sup-press harmful interference while preserving beneficial inter-actions. We recognize that rt contains intermediate abstrac-tions essential for problem-solving. However, its influence should be limited to guiding the usage of knowledge, not al-tering its meaning. This principle is summarized as follows: 1. Semantic Integrity: External knowledge should be en-coded in a manner that preserves its original semantics, ensuring it is not distorted by biases accumulated in prior reasoning steps. 2. Reasoning-Aligned Contextualization: The encoding process should remain aware of the reasoning context, enabling the knowledge to be effectively integrated into the ongoing problem-solving trajectory. In the following section, we propose a concrete mechanism that operationalizes this principle by explicitly balancing semantic integrity and reasoning contextualization during the knowledge encoding process. 

4.2. SAKE: Self-Anchored Knowledge Encoding 

To mitigate KID while preserving both semantic integrity and reasoning contextualization, we propose Self-Anchored 

Knowledge Encoding ( SAKE ). SAKE introduces a dual-positioning strategy for external knowledge, in which search results are integrated into the input sequence in two comple-mentary roles: (1) a cumulative knowledge stack that serves as a semantic anchor, and (2) a reasoning-aligned context that facilitates stepwise knowledge utilization. 4.2.1. C UMULATIVE KNOWLEDGE STACK AS A 

SEMANTIC ANCHOR 

The first component of SAKE is a cumulative knowledge stack placed at the very beginning of the reasoning trace. At reasoning step t + 1 , the model is provided with a stack of all previously searched documents, organized in reverse chronological order: 

St = [ dt, d t−1, . . . , d 1]. (6) Here, the most recently searched information is placed at the front of the stack, while earlier documents appear later in the sequence. By positioning St before any generated reasoning tokens ( r1, . . . , r t), the model encodes external knowledge in a context, free from interference by prior rea-soning. Furthermore, placing the most recent search result (dt) at the very beginning ensures that it is processed with minimal exposure to preceding content under the causal attention mechanism. As a result, the document containing the latest searched information is encoded with controlled input sequence con-struction, preventing speculative reasoning traces from dis-torting its semantics. # 6. Discussion 

6.1. Is Performance Gain of SAKE Truly due to Mitigating KID? 

A critical question regarding SAKE is whether its perfor-mance gains stem from the structural mitigation of KID or simply from the redundancy of having the retrieved content appear twice in the context, as some prior works have sug-gested regarding repetition (Springer et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2025). To disentangle these factors, we construct a control variant where the retrieved document is simply duplicated immediately following the reasoning step ( [. . . ; rt; dt; dt])instead of adopting the dual-placement structure of SAKE. This setting, denoted as “Repeat” in Table 3, isolates the effect of repetition from SAKE. This variant yields little to no improvement over the Search-o1 baseline and, in some cases, even degrades performance. In contrast, SAKE con-sistently achieves substantial gains. These results indicate that the effectiveness of SAKE cannot be attributed to sim-ple repetition. Instead, the gains arise from its ability to mitigate KID by anchoring the knowledge in a position free from interference, thereby ensuring robust encoding.

However, it consistently underperforms the full SAKE method. This result highlights that semantic integrity alone is insufficient. Effective search-augmented reasoning requires not only the preservation of factual content but also its proper contextualization within the ongoing reasoning trace to guide the specific problem-solving path. 

6.3. Why Resolving KID Is Essential for Scaling Search-Augmented Reasoning 

Finally, we investigate a fundamental implication for the field: does enhancing search quality automatically lead to better reasoning? This suggests that current models face a “utilization bottleneck”, meaning that even when perfect information is retrieved, they struggle to use it effectively due to KID. # 7. Conclusion 

In this work, we identify Knowledge Integration Decay (KID ) as a fundamental yet previously overlooked bottle-neck in search-augmented reasoning. We show that as pre-search reasoning extends, a model’s ability to effectively incorporate newly retrieved evidence degrades. Through mechanistic analyses, we demonstrate that this failure arises from attention-level interference during knowledge encod-ing, where the accumulated state of prior reasoning over-shadows the objective representation of external knowledge. To address this issue, we propose Self-Anchored Knowledge 

Encoding ( SAKE ), a simple yet effective inference-time strategy. SAKE is designed to balance two essential require-ments: preserving the semantic integrity of retrieved knowl-edge and maintaining the necessary reasoning contextualiza-tion. Extensive experiments across multi-hop QA and com-plex reasoning benchmarks confirm that SAKE consistently improves both performance and robustness—particularly as reasoning depth increases—without requiring any additional training or architectural modifications. 

Limitations While this work provides a rigorous analysis of KID and a robust mitigation strategy, we acknowledge a practical trade-off in context management, as our dual-placement strategy inherently increases the input length with each search step. Furthermore, since SAKE operates solely at inference time, it leaves open the possibility for funda-mental training-time solutions. Future work may build upon these findings to explore adaptive context management tech-niques, integrate KID-aware objectives into reinforcement learning for search policies, and extend these principles to broader applications in autonomous agent systems.

# B. Experimental Details 

B.1. Dataset Processing Data filtering. To ensure that performance improvements reflect enhanced knowledge integration rather than shortcut reasoning from parametric knowledge, we apply a filtering procedure to all evaluation datasets except GAIA. Specifically, for each benchmark, we first identify hard samples that cannot be correctly solved by the model using direct reasoning alone, without access to external knowledge. For each query in the validation set, we sample 8 independent responses from the model under a no-retrieval setting. If any of these responses achieves a word-level F1 score greater than 0.5 against the ground-truth answer (for HotpotQA, 2WikiMultiHopQA, and MuSiQue), or is judged as Correct by an LLM-based evaluator (for FRAMES and GAIA), the corresponding sample is considered solvable via parametric knowledge and is excluded from evaluation. This filtering process ensures that the resulting evaluation set consists solely of samples that genuinely require external knowledge search. For HotpotQA, 2WikiMultiHopQA, and MuSiQue, we additionally balance the evaluation set to maintain an equal number of samples across query types. B.2. Evaluation Metrics 

We evaluate model performance using two complementary metrics: word-level F1 score and LLM-as-a-Judge accuracy, depending on the characteristics of each benchmark. For HotpotQA, 2WikiMultiHopQA, and MuSiQue, the ground-truth answers are provided as short, span-based responses derived from the gold documents. Accordingly, we adopt the word-level F1 score between the model-generated answer and the ground-truth answer as the evaluation metric, following standard practice in multi-hop question answering. In contrast, FRAMES and GAIA often involve long-form or compositional answers, where semantically correct responses may not exactly match the reference text. Moreover, these benchmarks frequently require reasoning over implicit or multi-step evidence, making exact string matching unreliable. To account for this, we employ an LLM-based evaluation protocol, using Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct as the judge model to assess answer correctness. The judge determines whether the model output is semantically consistent with the ground-truth answer, following the evaluation setup described in prior work. 

B.3. Implementation Search environment. For HotpotQA, 2WikiMultiHopQA, and MuSiQue, we construct the search corpus by aggregating all document chunks provided in the validation split of each benchmark. This results in 66,635 documents for HotpotQA, 56,687 for 2WikiMultiHopQA, and 101,962 for MuSiQue. For FRAMES, each sample is associated with a relevant Wikipedia page rather than pre-segmented document chunks. Accordingly, we extract the main textual content of each page up to a maximum length of 8,000 characters and segment it into paragraph-level chunks. This procedure yields a total of 25,106 paragraphs. Because these paragraphs are generally longer than the document chunks used in other benchmarks, the effective amount of information retrieved per search step is larger for FRAMES. All corpora are encoded using Qwen3-Embedding-0.6B to construct an embedding-based retrieval index. During search-augmented reasoning, the model retrieves the top-5 most relevant documents at each search step and is allowed to perform up to 10 search iterations. For the Standard RAG baseline, the model retrieves the top-10 documents only once at the beginning of inference. The cumulative knowledge stack proposed in SAKE is characterized by three key properties: (1) retrieved knowledge is accumulated across reasoning steps, (2) the stack is ordered as [dt, . . . , d 1], and (3) each retrieved document is preserved in its original form without compression or truncation. To understand the contribution of each design choice, we conduct an ablation study by modifying these components and evaluating their impact on performance. Specifically, we consider three variants. (i) Flash Stack places only the most recent retrieved document at the beginning of 17 Knowledge Integration Decay in Search-Augmented Reasoning of LLMs 

the reasoning context, without accumulating past knowledge. (ii) Reversed Stack arranges retrieved documents in the order 

[d1, . . . , d t], reversing the proposed stacking order. (iii) Brief Stack includes only a truncated subset of the retrieved content instead of the full documents. Table 5 summarizes the results. The Flash Stack exhibits slight performance gains on simpler 2-hop queries but shows a consistent degradation as the task complexity increases to 3-hop and 4-hop settings. This behavior suggests that, while recent evidence may suffice for shallow reasoning, complex multi-hop queries require effective utilization of earlier retrieved knowledge. Since Flash Stack does not preserve prior evidence in the knowledge stack, it fails to support such reasoning. The Reversed Stack leads to notable performance drops across most settings, except for 3-hop queries. This observation aligns with our analysis in Section 4: when the stack is ordered as [d1, . . . , d t], the encoding of the current document is more strongly influenced by earlier retrieved content, increasing interference and degrading knowledge integration. The Brief Stack shows moderate improvements for 2-hop and 3-hop queries but suffers a significant performance drop in the 4-hop setting. This indicates that as reasoning complexity increases, retaining comprehensive external knowledge becomes increasingly important, and aggressive truncation hinders effective multi-step reasoning. Overall, these results demonstrate that the proposed knowledge stack provides the most stable and robust performance across varying reasoning depths. This ablation study further supports the design choice of SAKE and highlights the importance of structured context management for reliable knowledge integration in agentic reasoning.
