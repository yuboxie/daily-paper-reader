Abstract 

As mental health issues continue to rise globally, there is an increasing demand for accessible and scalable therapeutic solutions. Many individuals currently seek support from Large Language Models (LLMs), even though these models have not been validated for use in counseling services. In this paper, we evaluate LLMs’ ability to emulate professional therapists practicing Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT). Using anonymized, transcribed role-play sessions between licensed therapists and clients, we compare two approaches: (1) a generation-only method and (2) a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) approach using CBT guidelines. We evaluate both proprietary and open-source models for linguistic quality, semantic coherence, and therapeutic fidelity using standard natural language generation (NLG) metrics, natural language inference (NLI), and automated scoring for skills assessment. Our results indicate that while LLMs can generate CBT-like dialogues, they are limited in their ability to convey empathy and maintain consistency. 

Keywords: Cognitive Behavioral Therapy, LLMs, Mental Health 

# 1. Introduction and Related Work 

Mental health disorders are increasingly prevalent, highlighting a significant need for accessible dig-ital interventions to compensate for the shortage of psychologists and counselors capable of provid-ing therapeutic support. 1 As individuals become more inclined to express their concerns through online tools rather than engaging in face-to-face therapy (Lucas et al., 2014; Haensch, 2025), there is growing interest in the potential of generative AI chatbots for facilitating the delivering of therapy. Al-though recent studies have reported a positive shift in attitudes towards using Large Language Mod-els (LLMs) for mental health support (Haensch, 2025), many of these models were not specifically designed for therapeutic applications 2 and have not been validated for offering counseling services. The development of dialog systems for emotional support has also gained attention, with researchers focusing on creating frameworks that enable these systems to provide empathetic and effective inter-action (Liu et al., 2021; Kang et al., 2024). These systems aim to offer immediate, accessible support, serving as a complementary resource alongside traditional therapeutic practices. With the increasing interest in leveraging tech-nology to enhance psychological counseling (Na et al., 2025), much of the existing literature has focused on specific therapeutic tasks, such as cog-nitive reframing techniques. These techniques aim to address cognitive distortions and promote a shift from negative thought patterns to improve mental health outcomes (de Toledo Rodriguez et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023; Sharma et al., 2023). Previous studies have also presented established bench-marks to assess LLM performance in Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), examining factors such as knowledge acquisition, classification of cogni-tive distortions and beliefs, and the generation of therapeutic responses (Zhang et al., 2025). The findings indicate that while these models exhibit knowledge of cognitive processes, particularly in question-answering scenarios, they generally do not perform at the same level as mental health ex-perts in most generative tasks. Few efforts have investigated the use of LLMs specifically for deliver-ing therapy (Lee et al., 2024; Na, 2024). However, most studies rely on synthetic datasets (Lee et al., 2024; Qiu et al., 2024; De Duro et al., 2025; Man-dal et al., 2025; Abbasi et al., 2025) rather than authentic therapist dialogues (Puspo et al., 2026), leaving open questions about linguistic fidelity and therapeutic alignment.

To address this gap, our paper proposes a com-prehensive evaluation of LLMs in the context of CBT, a widely recognized and effective form of psychotherapy for a range of mental health con-ditions, including depression, anxiety, obsessive-compulsive disorder, and various forms of addic-tion (David et al., 2018). We compare two distinct methodologies for providing CBT via LLMs: one that leverages existing CBT knowledge without ex-ternal augmentation, and another that employs a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) approach. This dual approach enables us to evaluate the ex-tent to which LLMs can generate responses that resemble those of professional therapists in multi-turn dialogues. While previous studies have gen-erated synthetic CBT sessions, the multi-turn in-teractions in these sessions are generally shorter than those observed in real-life counseling (Lee et al., 2024). To address this issue, we use data from real counseling sessions conducted by pro-fessional therapists. Our goal is to evaluate how closely utterances generated by LLMs resemble those of professional CBT therapists in multi-turn dialogues. The contributions of our study include a thorough comparison of generation-only and RAG methods and a comprehensive analysis of generated re-sponses alongside responses from mental health professionals. Our analysis uses various metrics to evaluate both natural language generation (NLG) and therapeutic effectiveness. This includes as-sessments for natural language generation, natural language inference (NLI), and automated scoring for general counseling and CBT-specific skills as-sessment. The paper aims to answer the following research questions: 

RQ1 To what extent can LLMs linguistically and semantically emulate professional therapists in CBT sessions, as measured by natural language generation and inference metrics? 

RQ2 Does RAG improve the linguistic quality, se-mantic coherence, or therapeutic fidelity of LLM-generated CBT responses compared to generation-only methods? 

RQ3 How effectively do LLMs demonstrate gen-eral counseling skills (e.g., understanding, inter-personal effectiveness, and collaboration), CBT-specific competencies (e.g., guided discovery, fo-cus, and strategy) and empathy relative to human therapists? Through this exploration, we aim to provide a deeper understanding of LLMs’ capabilities in deliv-ering CBT, as many individuals increasingly rely on these models for mental health support. We aim to contribute to a clearer understanding of LLMs’ potential and limitations in mental health care.

The first approach is a generation-only method, mo-tivated by previous research showing that LLMs already possess knowledge of CBT techniques (Zhang et al., 2025). 2.3. Evaluation 

To comprehensively assess LLM performance, we employed three complementary evaluation dimen-sions: linguistic quality and similarity to the ther-apist’s original responses, semantic consistency, and therapeutic and CBT-specific skills. Each of these dimensions captures a distinct aspect of what constitutes effective therapy. 

Text generation metrics The evaluation of gen-erated responses is typically conducted using vari-ous text generation metrics (Na, 2024; Kang et al., 2024). We assess metrics that measure the lexi-cal overlap between generated utterances and the therapists’ reference responses, such as BLEU (Pa-pineni et al., 2002), ROUGE (Lin, 2004), and ME-TEOR (Banerjee and Lavie, 2005). In addition, we use BERTScore (Zhang et al.), which measures semantic similarity. We also consider Distinct-1 (Li et al., 2016), which measures the diversity in lan-guage generation tasks. However, these measures have inherent limitations, as they may allow mod-els to produce text that is contextually relevant but differs from the responses provided by therapists. Semantic consistency was assessed using nat-ural language inference techniques to evaluate con-tradictions and coherence. Building on previous research (Chim et al., 2024; Laurer et al., 2024), we employed a DeBERTa V3 large model that has been fine-tuned for NLI. 4 This model is used to determine whether the generated response is con-sistent with or contradicts the original response given by the therapist. Consistency is defined as the absence of contradiction. Since generated re-sponses may consist of multiple sentences, we adopt the approach used by Chim et al. (2024) and average the maximum contradiction probabilities of the generated sentences from each utterance. 

Therapeutic skills evaluation was conducted to assess general counseling skills, CBT-specific skills, and empathy. Recent research indicates that evaluating natural language generation using LLMs closely aligns with human evaluations and is increasingly used for this task (Chiang and Lee, 2023; Lee et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2025). For the evaluation of general and CBT skills, we adopt the methodology outlined by Lee et al. (2024). The authors adapted GPT-4o to conduct evalua-tions based on the Cognitive Therapy Rating Scale (CTRS) 5, which showed a high correlation between the model’s predictions and expert manual eval-uations. CTRS is a tool designed to assess the CBT skills of therapists, focusing on specific gen-eral and CBT skills. The evaluation described by Lee et al. (2024) measures a subset of the CTRS that can be applied to text-based settings. The gen-eral skills assessed include understanding (e.g., how accurately the therapist comprehends the pa-tient’s situation), interpersonal effectiveness (e.g., the therapist’s ability to communicate effectively and maintain a positive, professional relationship with the client), and collaboration (e.g., the thera-pist’s capacity to work effectively with the client). The CBT-specific skills evaluated include guided discovery (e.g., the therapist’s ability to explore the client’s problems and help them draw their own conclusions), focus (e.g., the therapist’s ability to concentrate on cognitions and behaviors relevant to the issue), and strategy (e.g., the therapist’s abil-ity to implement a consistent strategy for change using CBT techniques). Each of these categories is assigned a score ranging from 0 to 6, in accor-dance with the guidelines provided by the CTRS tool. We used the same evaluation methodology for CTRS categories that was previously validated by Lee et al. (2024), using GPT-4o to assess the generated responses.

The final measure in our evaluation focuses on 

empathy , which we consider a vital component of the therapeutic alliance (Feller and Cottone, 2003). The therapeutic alliance refers to the collaborative bond between the therapist and the client, char-acterized by a strong emotional connection and a mutual understanding of treatment goals and tasks. This alliance is closely linked to the effectiveness of therapy (Feller and Cottone, 2003). To evalu-ate empathy, we use the framework developed by Sharma et al. (2020), which specifically assesses empathy using a threefold approach that consid-ers emotional reactions, interpretations, and explo-rations. Emotional reactions (e.g., I am sorry you are going through... ) relate to affective empathy and help communicate emotions such as warmth and compassion, which are essential for building the therapeutic relationship. Interpretations (e.g., Iunderstand how you feel... ) are connected to cog-nitive empathy, indicating that the therapist com-prehends the client’s feelings. This understanding further enhances the therapeutic alliance. The last component, explorations (e.g., Are you feeling...? ), is used to probe the client’s feelings further. We evaluate these measures of empathy on a scale of 0 to 2.

4.1. Agreeableness Bias 

A manual inspection of the results reveals a pat-tern of agreeableness bias across several models, consistent with findings by Salecha et al. (2024) that large language models tend to produce overly affirming responses. Examples from GPT-4o-mini (“Absolutely, your feelings and decisions should be what matter most”, ”Absolutely, it’s important to rec-ognize that your relationship should be defined by what works for both of you, not by arbitrary stan-dards.”) and Qwen 7B (“Absolutely. It’s important to base our thoughts and beliefs on reality rather than just what we fear might be true”) illustrate how the models often respond with enthusiastic validation rather than critical exploration. Similarly, phrases such as “It’s completely understandable to feel that way,” frequently produced by Mistral 7B, Qwen 7B, and GPT-4o-mini variants, demonstrate a tendency toward reassurance. While these responses con-vey warmth and support, they reflect surface-level empathy that reinforces rather than explores the client’s perspective (Curry and Curry, 2023). Al-ways agreeing with clients’ views does not encour-age them to examine and reframe their thoughts, which is an important part of CBT (Sharma et al., 2023).

# 5. Conclusion and Future Work 

In this paper, we show that LLMs have the po-tential to replicate certain elements of Cognitive Behavioral Therapy, particularly in terms of lin-guistic coherence and supportive tone. How-ever, they have notable limitations in general ther-apeutic skills, CBT-specific skills, and empathy. Retrieval-augmented generation shows limited im-provement, suggesting that LLMs possess suffi-cient CBT-related knowledge to generate contextu-ally coherent, semantically aligned responses with-out the need for external information. These results indicate that while LLMs show promising linguistic and structural competence in simulating CBT and mimicking the language of a CBT therapist, they do not replicate the nuanced therapeutic skills of human therapists that are essential for achieving positive therapeutic outcomes. In future research, we aim to investigate LLMs’ ability to respond to clients and potential users from diverse cultural backgrounds. Previous studies have shown that emotional nuances can differ across populations in therapeutic settings (Abbasi et al., 2025), and that the expression of mental health distress can also vary across different cultures (Bucur et al., 2026). 

# Limitations 

This study has several limitations that should be acknowledged. First, the dataset used in our eval-uation is relatively small, consisting of a limited number of role-played CBT sessions. Although this dataset provides valuable initial insights, it does not capture the full diversity of therapeutic contexts or client presentations. In future work, we plan to expand our dataset by collecting a larger and more varied sample of authentic and simulated therapy dialogues to enhance the robustness and general-izability of our findings. Second, while we employed automated evalu-ation methods validated in prior research for as-sessing therapeutic skills (Lee et al., 2024) and empathy (Sharma et al., 2020), these approaches cannot fully replace manual evaluation by expert CBT practitioners. Expert human judgment is nec-essary to capture the subtle nuances of empathy and therapeutic reasoning that automated metrics may overlook. Therefore, future studies should in-corporate expert evaluations to supplement quanti-tative analyses and provide a more comprehensive assessment of model performance. Lastly, we evaluated the performance of small-scale language models, specifically those with 7-8B parameters, and GPT-4o-mini, due to our limited computational resources. Our findings suggest that while these models can generate contextually coherent responses that resemble CBT, they still do not match the skills demonstrated by human therapists. In future work, we plan to assess larger models to determine if the results are consistent. Despite these limitations, we believe this paper represents an important first step toward system-atically evaluating LLMs in CBT contexts. By com-bining linguistic, semantic, and therapeutic dimen-sions, our work lays the groundwork for large-scale assessments of AI-assisted psychotherapy. 

# Ethical Considerations 

All data were derived from publicly available, non-clinical role-play sessions. This research does not endorse the use of LLMs in psychotherapy. We emphasize that LLM-based counseling tools must include clear disclaimers, human oversight, and rigorous safety validation before being deployed, due to risks such as hallucination, reinforcement of cognitive distortions, and inappropriate emotional responses.
