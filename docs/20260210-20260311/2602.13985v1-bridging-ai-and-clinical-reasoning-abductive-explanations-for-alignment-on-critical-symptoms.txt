## Abstract 

Artificial intelligence (AI) has demonstrated strong potential in clinical diagnostics, often achieving accuracy comparable to or ex-ceeding that of human experts. A key challenge, however, is that AI reasoning frequently diverges from structured clinical frameworks, limiting trust, interpretability, and adoption. Critical symptoms, pivotal for rapid and accurate decision-making, may be overlooked by AI models even when predictions are correct. Existing post hoc explanation methods provide limited transparency and lack formal guarantees. To address this, we leverage formal abductive explanations, which offer consistent, guaranteed reasoning over minimal sufficient feature sets. This enables a clear understanding of AI decision-making and allows alignment with clinical reason-ing. Our approach preserves predictive accuracy while providing clinically actionable insights, establishing a robust framework for trustworthy AI in medical diagnosis.

## 1 Introduction 

The integration of artificial intelligence (AI) into clinical practice has shown considerable promise, particularly in diagnostic tasks where AI systems can achieve performance comparable to or ex-ceeding that of human experts in specific domains [ 3, 22 ]. Despite these advances, a critical challenge persists: the reasoning pro-cesses underlying AI predictions often diverge from the structured clinical reasoning frameworks used by experienced clinicians. Al-though AI systems may deliver correct diagnoses, the pathways through which these conclusions are reached frequently differ from clinically accepted practices. This disconnect raises important con-cerns regarding trust, interpretability, clinical adoption, and patient safety [4, 34].

Clinical reasoning is inherently multifaceted, combining intu-itive pattern recognition with structured analytical decision making grounded in medical knowledge and experience [ 6 , 28 ]. In contrast, many AI models rely on computational strategies that prioritize pre-dictive performance without explicitly enforcing interpretability or clinical plausibility. As a result, AI systems may generate accurate predictions while relying on reasoning processes that are difficult for clinicians to understand, validate, or justify in practice [ 22 , 41 ]. This challenge is further compounded by the limited transparency of many modern AI architectures. In particular, deep neural net-works and large language models often operate as black boxes, limiting clinicians’ ability to assess the soundness of AI reasoning even when the resulting predictions are correct [31, 34]. This lack of transparency not only obscures how AI systems ar-rive at their predictions, but also directly undermines their practical use in clinical settings. In medical decision making, understanding the rationale behind a diagnosis is as important as the diagnosis itself. Consequently, the opacity of many AI models has significant implications for clinical adoption. Clinicians are often reluctant to rely on tools whose reasoning they cannot evaluate, and limited insight into AI decision processes constrains their ability to learn from the system or identify potential errors [ 4 , 10 ]. Addressing this misalignment between AI reasoning and clinical reasoning is therefore essential for the safe and effective integration of AI into healthcare workflows. More broadly, it motivates the development of systematic evaluation frameworks, alignment strategies, and clinician-centered AI design principles that prioritize reasoning transparency alongside predictive performance [9, 43]. In response to these challenges, a growing body of work has focused on explanation techniques aimed at improving the trans-parency of AI models. Methods such as SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) and LIME (Local Interpretable Model-Agnostic Ex-planations) have been widely explored to provide post-hoc insights into model behavior [ 31 ]. By identifying features that most strongly influence individual predictions, these approaches enable clinicians to verify whether AI systems rely on clinically relevant biomark-ers, symptoms, or demographic factors [ 2, 20 , 33 ]. Consequently, such techniques have been applied in clinical settings including cardiovascular risk assessment and cancer prognosis, where they can support clinician confidence, facilitate patient communication, and improve the interpretability of model outputs [8, 21]. Despite their utility, SHAP and LIME are inherently approximation-based methods, which introduces a fidelity–interpretability trade-off [ 23 , 32 ]. These approaches may fail to capture complex feature interactions, provide formal guarantees that are crucial for system-atically evaluating diagnostic outputs [ 5, 14 , 18 , 26 , 36 ]. As a result,

there remains a need for explanation methods that can offer both interpretability and rigorous guarantees. To address these limitations, formal abductive explanations offer a promising alternative. By reasoning over minimal sufficient fea-ture sets, abductive explanations generate structured justifications for AI predictions. Importantly, their formal guarantees ensure that these explanations are consistent and reliable across similar cases, enabling a precise understanding of the AI model’s decision-making process. This, in turn, allows for the systematic mitigation of misalignment between AI reasoning and clinician reasoning. Con-sequently, abductive explanations transform AI transparency into actionable, clinically meaningful insight, effectively bridging the gap between automated predictions and human expertise [15, 38]. Building on this perspective, the contributions of this paper are fourfold. We leverage abductive explanations to: (1) Embed clinical reasoning on critical symptoms into AI sys-tem reasoning (Section 4). (2) Propose a methodology to assess misalignment between AI model reasoning and clinical reasoning on critical symptoms (Section 5). (3) Identify critical cases and critical symptoms directly from the training set (Section 6). (4) Quantify reasoning misalignment in real-world scenarios: Breast Cancer, Heart Disease and Mental Health datasets (Sections 7, 8 and 9). 

## 2 Background: Critical Symptoms and Reasoning Alignment in Medical Diagnosis 

Critical symptoms, often described as high-value diagnostic indi-cators, play a foundational role in clinical diagnosis by narrowing the diagnostic space, prioritizing life-threatening conditions, and guiding time-critical decision-making [ 19 , 30 ]. They act as cues that activate clinicians’ illness scripts and support efficient hypoth-esis generation, enabling rapid and accurate diagnostic decisions [ 11 , 40 ]. Missing or misinterpreting such symptoms is a major contributor to diagnostic errors, highlighting their importance as safeguards in clinical reasoning. Major clinical guidelines, includ-ing NICE and WHO, rely on critical symptoms to define escalation pathways and identify conditions that must not be missed [ 29 , 42 ]. Proper identification and weighting of these symptoms ensure early intervention, reduce misdiagnosis, and form the backbone of effec-tive clinical reasoning [12, 35, 45]. While AI systems have achieved predictive accuracy comparable to expert clinicians [ 1, 17 ], alignment of reasoning with critical symptoms adds an essential layer of evaluation. Beyond generating correct predictions, AI systems must consistently recognize and appropriately weigh these pivotal clinical features in ways that mirror expert clinician judgment. Achieving such alignment fosters trust, enhances interpretability, and positions AI as a collaborative partner in decision-making rather than an opaque tool [ 37 , 45 ]. In this work, we focus on evaluating and improving alignment between AI reasoning and clinician reasoning for critical symptoms, treating these high-value indicators as essential touch points for assessing clinical reliability and trustworthiness.

## 3 Preliminaries 3.1 Understanding the Diagnosis of Patients: Clinicians vs AI Model 

Broadly speaking, medical diagnosis determines whether a patient suffers from a disease (positive( 𝑑 +)) or not (negative ( 𝑑 − )). In this paper, we compare the reasoning processes underlying patient diag-nosis in a specific scenario, focusing on patients exhibiting critical symptoms that strongly indicate a positive diagnosis. We assess how clinicians ( Δ𝑐 ) and an AI system ( Δ) make these decisions, as illustrated in Figure 1. Figure 1 shows the reasoning pathways of clinicians and the AI system. Clinicians rely on established rules that are sufficient to make a positive diagnosis when critical symptoms are present, requiring no additional information. When these critical criteria are absent, clinicians gather further evidence to refine their diagnosis. In contrast, the AI system generates a diagnosis for any patient using the knowledge acquired from training data. Thus, clinicians (Δ𝑐 ) implement a partial decision function , acting only when critical information is available, whereas the AI system ( Δ) implements a 

complete decision function , capable of diagnosing any patient. While Δ𝑐 relies exclusively on clinically meaningful information, 

Δ, although often accurate, may use non-clinical correlations. This motivates the need to assess alignment between the two decision processes to improve rapid, clinically grounded diagnoses. The next section introduces the notation used to formalize this assessment.

## 3.3 Definition of Abductive Explanations 

Formal abductive explanations (AXPs) have been extensively stud-ied in prior works [ 7, 13 , 16 , 24 , 25 , 27 ]. An abductive explanation (XP) is defined as a minimal set of features of an individual that is sufficient to guarantee the same decision by a model. In other words, any individual sharing identical values on this minimal feature set will necessarily receive the same prediction. This property ensures that abductive explanations accurately capture the model’s behavior and provide a consistent characteriza-tion of its decisions. Consequently, AXPs offer stronger guarantees for evaluating a model’s consistency with respect to specific princi-ples or criteria under consideration. For completeness, we present in appendix an algorithm, which details how to compute an abduc-tive explanation, along with a step-by-step example illustrating the computation process. 

## 4 Making AI Models Reason as Clinicians 

As mentioned earlier, abductive explanations provide fundamen-tal insights into a model’s behavior. Beyond explaining individual decisions, they reveal which features the model considers impor-tant, with formal guarantees. Specifically, if 𝑋 𝑃 is an abductive explanation for an individual’s decision in an AI model, any other instance satisfying 𝑋 𝑃 will receive the same decision. Leveraging this property, prior work has assessed model behavior with re-spect to ethical principles, including bias, proxy discrimination, and unfairness [ 7 , 39 ]. Following this logic, we introduce concepts to understand reasoning in AI diagnostic models. 

## 4.1 Relevant Features in Diagnosis 

Definition 1 (Relevant Feature(s)). A feature 𝑓 , or a group of features 𝐶 , is relevant for an individual’s diagnosis if at least one abductive explanation contains 𝑓 .

Definition 1 captures the importance of individual features or feature combinations for a patient’s diagnosis. While identifying feature relevance is essential for understanding model behavior, clinicians require more: they need to know which features are particularly critical to yield positive diagnoses, often referred to as 

critical symptoms , as discussed below. 

## 4.2 Critical Symptoms in Diagnosis 

Critical symptoms play a foundational role in clinical diagnosis. These features are more than relevant—they are decisive, automati-cally leading to a positive diagnosis. We formalize this notion within the context of abductive explanations: 

Definition 2 (Critical Feature(s)). A feature 𝑓 , or a combina-tion of features 𝐶 , is critical if satisfying it automatically leads to a positive diagnosis: 

∀𝑥 ∈ 𝑋, 𝑓 ⊆ 𝑥 ∨ 𝐶 ⊆ 𝑥 ⇒ Δ𝑐 (𝑥 ) = 𝑑 +

From here on, we refer to critical features as a critical property .

Definition 3 (Critical Case). An instance (patient) is a critical case if it satisfies the critical property ( 𝐶𝑟 ). 

Definition 2 formalizes the features clinicians ( Δ𝑐 ) rely on exclu-sively to make a diagnosis. By focusing on these critical properties, we can analyze whether the AI system ( Δ) incorporates clinically meaningful reasoning in its diagnostic decisions, particularly for critical cases. 

## 4.3 Embedding Critical Symptoms in Model Reasoning 

In medical diagnosis, all features may contribute to a decision, but some are critical as they automatically trigger a positive out-come. Using abductive explanations, we can explicitly represent this property in the model’s reasoning and evaluate alignment with clinicians: 

Definition 4 (Critical Explanation). Given a critical property (𝐶𝑟 ), an abductive explanation 𝑋 𝑃 is critical if it contains 𝐶𝑟 :

𝐶𝑟 ⊆ 𝑋 𝑃 

Definition 4 allows us to distinguish explanations that justify an individual diagnosis. For a critical case, the AI system should use the critical property in its reasoning; otherwise, even a positive di-agnosis may be clinically disputable. In summary, the first criterion for an AI system Δ to make meaningful diagnoses is its ability to provide critical explanations for critical cases. The next step is to assess the alignment between AI reasoning and clinical reasoning. 

## 5 Alignment Between Clinical Reasoning and AI Systems on Critical Cases 5.1 The Basis of Alignment 

As a reminder, critical cases exhibit a critical property that is suffi-cient for clinicians to make a positive diagnosis. We have shown that an AI decision process is trustworthy if it provides critical explanations for critical cases; however, this alone is not enough to establish alignment with clinical reasoning. Indeed, critical proper-ties guarantee a positive diagnosis. Therefore, to claim alignment, we must ensure that critical explanations consistently lead to posi-tive diagnoses. 

Definition 5 (Aligned Critical Explanation). A critical ex-planation 𝑋 𝑃 is aligned with clinical reasoning if it yields a positive diagnosis ( 𝑑 +). 

Definition 5 emphasizes that alignment goes beyond simply providing critical explanations: it requires that the AI uses these ex-planations to reach the expected positive outcome. This establishes a fundamental criterion for alignment, which can then be extended to assess the AI system as a whole. 

## 5.2 From Aligned Explanations to Aligned AI Systems 

For critical cases, clinicians rely exclusively on the critical property to make a diagnosis. To align an AI system with this reasoning, the system should also base its diagnosis solely on the critical property. This requirement is formalized in the following theorems: ACSW 2026, February 09–12, 2026, Melbourne, Australia Belona Sonna and Alban Grastien 

Theorem 1 (Aligned Diagnosis). A critical case diagnosis is aligned if and only if all its abductive explanations are both critical and aligned. 

Theorem 2 (Alignment of an AI System). An AI system ( Δ) is aligned with clinical reasoning if and only if all its critical cases are diagnosed in an aligned manner. 

Theorem 1 formalizes the notion that alignment requires both AI and clinician reasoning to rely exclusively on the critical property for positive diagnoses in critical cases. This theorem provides the foundation for assessing reasoning alignment across the entire system: the AI is aligned if no critical case is diagnosed without following the aligned critical reasoning. While critical properties provide a clinical grounding for diagno-sis, identifying them can be challenging, especially when clinicians are not directly available. In the next section, we propose a method to discover critical properties within the training dataset itself, which can then serve as the basis for evaluating alignment between AI reasoning and clinical reasoning. 

## 6 Identification of Critical Properties within the Training Dataset 

As discussed earlier, alignment between an AI system and clinical reasoning depends on how the system leverages critical properties to make positive diagnoses for critical cases. Therefore, identifying these properties is essential for evaluating alignment. By definition, a critical property cannot apply to individuals with a negative diagnosis or who do not suffer from the disease. Building on this principle, this section presents a method to identify such properties from the training dataset. 

## 6.1 Identifying Critical Features 

Our approach to identifying critical features leverages the concept of background knowledge within abductive explanations. Following Liu et al. [ 44 ], background knowledge extracted from the training data can guide the computation of abductive explanations that reflect real-world constraints. Similarly, we introduce the notion of critical knowledge , defined as a set of rules used by clinicians to make the diagnosis. Such rules, when provided by domain experts, enable confident and reliable positive diagnoses. In settings where expert knowledge is unavailable, candidate rules may instead be extracted from the training data. However, these data-driven rules require careful validation to ensure their clinical relevance and trustworthiness. 

Definition 6 (Critical Knowledge). Critical knowledge ( 𝜅 )is a set of constraints that guarantees a positive diagnosis for an individual. 

From Definition 6, all critical properties that define a critical case can be derived. 

Theorem 3 (Existence of Critical Properties). If the critical knowledge 𝜅 is empty, no critical properties exist. 

Theorem 3 establishes a necessary condition for assessing mis-alignment between Δ (AI reasoning) and Δ𝑐 (clinical reasoning). If 

𝜅 is empty, there are no critical properties, and alignment cannot be evaluated. 

## 6.2 Use of Critical Features 

Critical properties serve as essential cues that clinicians rely on to make rapid and accurate diagnoses. They also provide a benchmark for assessing whether AI systems reason in ways consistent with clinical expectations. In particular, critical features can: 

• Highlight which features the AI system should prioritize for positive diagnoses in critical cases. 

• Serve as a starting point for building trust in AI recommen-dations. 

• Guide the selection of AI models that most closely match clinical reasoning. 

• Inform refinement or repair of model predictions to better align with clinical expectations. In the next section, we demonstrate how critical features can be systematically used to audit misalignment between AI and clinical reasoning, and how they can guide the improvement of AI decision-making. 

## 7 Auditing Misalignment Between Clinical and AI-System Reasoning 7.1 Causes of Misalignment 

Assuming the critical property is known, we can analyze how mis-alignment may arise. As illustrated in Figure 1, clinical reasoning will always yield a positive diagnosis for a critical case. In contrast, the AI system may produce either a positive or negative diagnosis based on its learned rules. This leads to two primary causes of misalignment: (1) The AI system predicts a negative diagnosis for a critical case, i.e., misclassification . This may indicate underfitting or insufficient training. (2) The AI system predicts a positive diagnosis but does not rely solely on the critical property, violating the conditions of Theorem 1. This reflects a reasoning misalignment rather than a simple classification error. Distinguishing these causes is crucial, as they imply different corrective actions. Misclassification must be addressed first, as it automatically constitutes misalignment. 

## 7.2 From Misclassification to Misalignment 

Formally, misclassification is defined as follows: 

Definition 7 (Model Misclassification). For a critical case 𝑥 ,the model misclassifies 𝑥 if 

Δ(𝑥 ) = 𝑑 − .

This definition establishes the first condition to verify when auditing misalignment. Misclassification serves as a necessary pre-cursor to reasoning misalignment.

## 8 Experimental Setting 8.1 Datasets 

8.1.1 Wisconsin Diagnostic Breast Cancer (WDBC) Dataset. The 

WDBC dataset comprises 569 instances, each representing a tu-mor sampled via fine-needle aspiration (FNA) of the breast. For each instance, 30 numeric features are extracted from cell nuclei in digitized images. Key predictive features include radius (mean and worst), perimeter (worst), area (mean), concavity (mean and worst), and concave points (mean), which capture tumor size, shape irregularities, and contour concavity. The target variable indicates whether the tumor is benign or malignant, forming a binary classifi-cation task. Preprocessing and binarization procedures are detailed in Appendix. 

8.1.2 Cleveland Heart Disease Dataset. The Cleveland Heart Dis-ease dataset contains 303 patient instances described by 13 clin-ical features , including demographics, chest pain type, resting blood pressure, cholesterol, ECG results, exercise-induced symp-toms, and ST-segment measurements. The target variable 𝑦 indi-cates the presence or absence of heart disease, forming a binary classification task. Clinician-informed preprocessing and binariza-tion steps are detailed in Appendix. 

8.1.3 Mental Health in Tech Survey Dataset. The Mental Health in Tech Survey (OSMI) dataset captures employees’ experiences and perceptions of mental health in the technology sector. We use a processed subset with 10 features related to demographics, workplace environment, and mental-health experiences, compris-ing 1,242 instances . The target variable indicates whether an individual sought mental health treatment, forming a binary classi-fication task. This focused subset allows analysis of the key factors influencing treatment-seeking behaviour. Preprocessing and bina-rization informed by domain knowledge are described in Appendix. 

## 8.2 AI Models 

We evaluate three classifiers: a single-layer neural network ( 𝑁 𝑁 ), lo-gistic regression ( 𝐿𝑅 ), and stochastic gradient descent ( 𝑆𝐺𝐷 ). These models were chosen for their diverse architectures to compare their behavior across the diagnostic tasks in each dataset. This compari-son provides insights into selecting decision processes that not only optimize standard metrics such as accuracy or F1 score but also maximize reasoning alignment with clinicians. Model performance metrics, including accuracy and F1 score, are provided in Appendix.

## 10 Discussion 

Our experiments provide several insights into the alignment be-tween AI models and clinician reasoning across different datasets. For the Breast Cancer dataset, the features most relevant for a positive diagnosis were 𝑥 7 for the NN and SGD models, and 𝑥 5 for the LR model. However, when we consider the dataset’s critical property, 𝐶 𝑟 : 𝑥 2 = 0 ∧ 𝑥 8 = 0, we notice that none of the features deemed most relevant by the models are part of this critical property. Consequently, in the third phase of our experiments, we observed a substantial misalignment between the models and clinician reason-ing. This finding highlights that simply inspecting which features the models consider most important can give clinicians a signal: if these features are not part of the critical property, it may indicate potential misalignment. In contrast, the Heart Disease dataset shows a different pattern. Both the LR and SGD models identified 𝑥 12 as the most relevant feature. Importantly, this feature is also part of the critical property, 

𝐶 𝑟 : 𝑥 2 = 0 ∧ 𝑥 3 = 1 ∧ 𝑥 12 = 0. As a result, the models exhibit better alignment with clinician reasoning, reinforcing the observation that alignment improves when the features critical for model predictions coincide with clinically defined critical properties. An additional factor affecting alignment appears to be the prepro-cessing of the datasets. For the Breast Cancer dataset, feature bina-rization was performed statistically (using the median), whereas for the Heart Disease dataset, thresholds were defined based on clinical knowledge. Although not exhaustively tested, this difference sug-gests that incorporating domain expertise in feature processing can improve the alignment between AI models and clinical reasoning, emphasizing the value of expert input in AI development. Finally, it is important to consider the nature of AI models versus clinical reasoning. Clinicians rely primarily on critical properties to make rapid and accurate diagnoses. AI models, however, can pro-cess multiple features simultaneously and may identify additional relevant factors beyond the critical properties. ## 11 Conclusion 

In this work, we investigated the alignment between clinical rea-soning and AI model decision-making in medical diagnostics, fo-cusing on critical cases where rapid and accurate diagnosis is es-sential. Leveraging formal abductive explanations, we identified relevant and critical features that drive AI predictions, and con-trasted them with the features clinicians rely upon to make positive diagnoses. Our experimental studies across three datasets—Breast Cancer, Heart Disease, and Mental Health in Tech—highlighted several key findings. First, the features deemed most relevant by AI models do not always correspond to the critical properties used by clinicians, as observed in the Breast Cancer dataset, resulting in substantial misalignment. Conversely, in the Heart Disease dataset, alignment was stronger when model-relevant features coincided with clinician-defined critical properties, underscoring the importance of domain-informed preprocessing and feature selection. Second, the notion of abductive explanations proved effective in formally capturing the reasoning process of AI models, allowing us to differentiate between correct predictions that are clinically aligned and those that are not. Finally, we proposed an alignment criterion (Theorem 4) that accounts for cases where AI models identify alternative reasons for a positive diagnosis, providing a flexible yet clinically meaningful framework for evaluating reasoning consistency. Our results demonstrate that reasoning alignment is a comple-mentary dimension to predictive accuracy, offering a new lens to evaluate the trustworthiness of AI systems in healthcare. By in-tegrating abductive explanations with critical property analysis, clinicians and AI developers can better identify misalignment, audit model behavior, and guide model refinement to ensure diagnostic decisions are both accurate and clinically meaningful. Future work will extend this framework to additional datasets and explore its potential for real-time clinical decision support, ultimately bridging the gap between AI reasoning and human clinical expertise.
