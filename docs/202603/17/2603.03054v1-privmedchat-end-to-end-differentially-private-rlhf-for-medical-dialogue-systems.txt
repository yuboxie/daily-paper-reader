# PrivMedChat: End-to-End Differentially Private RLHF for Medical Dialogue Systems 

Sudip Bhujel 

Abstract —Large language models are increasingly used for patient-facing medical assistance and clinical decision support, but adapting them to clinical dialogue often requires supervision derived from doctor–patient conversations that may contain sensitive information. Conventional supervised fine-tuning and reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) can am-plify memorization risks, enabling empirical membership infer-ence and extraction of rare training-set content. We present PrivMedChat, an end-to-end framework for differentially private RLHF (DP-RLHF) for medical dialogue. Our design enforces differential privacy at every training stage that directly accesses dialogue-derived supervision: (i) Differential Private Stochastic Gradient Descent (DP-SGD) for medical SFT and (ii) DP-SGD for reward model learning from preference pairs. To limit additional privacy expenditure during alignment, we apply DP-SGD to the PPO actor and critic when operating on dialogue-derived prompts, while the reward model remains fixed after DP training. We also introduce an annotation-free preference construction strategy that pairs physician responses with filtered non-expert generations to produce scalable preference data without clinician labeling. Experiments on medical dialogue benchmarks show that PrivMedChat at ε = 7 achieves the highest ROUGE-L of 0.156 among all DP models, reduces clinical hallucinations to 1.4% and harmful advice to 0.4%, and obtains the highest overall score of 2.86 in a 3-model LLM-jury evaluation, while producing membership-inference signals that are near chance (AUC 0.510–0.555). We open-source our code at https://github. com/sudip-bhujel/privmedchat. 

Index Terms —Differential privacy, Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), medical large language models, privacy-preserving fine-tuning, reward modeling, membership inference attacks, Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), Low Rank Adaptation (LoRA). 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly used for clinical decision support and patient-facing assistance, ranging from diagnosis and symptoms to care navigation and patient education. Recent medical-domain systems such as Med-PaLM [1], ChatDoctor [2], and BioMixtral [3] demonstrate that adapting general-purpose LLMs to biomedical knowledge and clinical dialogue can improve response helpfulness, do-main fluency, and adherence to medical communication norms. These capabilities make medical chatbots promising tools for scaling access to health information and reducing clinician burden, especially in resource-constrained settings. State-of-the-art alignment of such systems commonly fol-lows a Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) [4] pipeline. The process begins by adapting the base model through Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) on high-quality clinical interactions to learn domain style and conversational structure. Next, a reward model is trained on ranked preference pairs to approximate expert judgments of clinical utility and safety. Finally, the SFT model is optimized to maximize reward, typically via Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) [5] or related preference-optimization methods. This preference-based stage is often critical for improving safety behavior (e.g., appropriate escalation) and for reducing unhelpful or overly generic responses. Despite these advances, effective medical adaptation and personalization often rely on training signals derived from doctor-patient dialogues, which can contain protected health information (PHI) and other sensitive attributes governed by regulations such as the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) and the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). Conventional fine-tuning and RLHF may inadvertently memorize training records, enabling membership inference attacks (MIA) [6], attribute inference, or verbatim leakage of rare patient details [7]. Importantly, even datasets released in de-identified or “public” form can remain sensitive in an adversarial setting: rare strings, uncommon symptom

combinations, or residual identifiers may still be memorized and later recoverable. In high-stakes clinical contexts, such leakage risks patient re-identification and undermines trust in deployment. Differential privacy (DP) provides a rigorous framework for limiting the influence of any single training record on model parameters [8]. Prior work has successfully applied DP to language model pre-training and supervised fine-tuning (DP-SFT) [9], [10], [11], showing that nontrivial utility can be re-tained under meaningful privacy budgets. However, extending DP to the full RLHF pipeline remains challenging. Preference modeling and policy optimization are often highly sensitive to noise, and naive DP mechanisms can substantially degrade alignment quality—a particularly acute issue in healthcare, where correctness, safety, and calibrated uncertainty are essen-tial. As a result, existing privacy-preserving LLMs frequently exhibit pronounced performance drops at privacy levels that meaningfully reduce empirical leakage risk [12]. This paper addresses the challenge of extending differen-tial privacy (DP) beyond supervised fine-tuning to the full preference-based alignment pipeline for medical dialogue. We propose PrivMedChat, an end-to-end differentially private RLHF framework that applies DP-SGD to (i) supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on dialogue-derived supervision, (ii) reward model training on preference pairs, and (iii) PPO policy optimization. Because the PPO stage also accesses dialogue-derived prompts, we apply DP-SGD to both the PPO actor and critic, accounting for their privacy cost in εtotal . The DP-trained reward model is kept fixed during alignment so that its privacy budget is not further consumed. Although some medical dialogue datasets are publicly re-leased in de-identified form, we treat dialogue-derived super-vision as sensitive due to residual identifiers, rare symptom combinations, and adversarial linkage risks. Accordingly, the DP guarantees reported in this work are stated with respect to the training records used for SFT and reward modeling, and should be interpreted as a formal limitation on the influence of any single training example on the released model param-eters—not as a standalone claim of regulatory compliance. To avoid costly clinician annotation, we construct prefer-ence pairs using an expert-versus-non-expert strategy from medical dialogue corpora (e.g., MedDialog [13]). Specifically, we treat real physician responses as “chosen” outputs and generate “rejected” alternatives using a base LLM prompted to respond as a non-expert assistant; we apply similarity and quality filtering to ensure informative preference margins. This produces scalable, high-signal preference data for reward modeling without requiring additional human labeling. We present a scalable strategy to form preference pairs for medical dialogue alignment by pairing physician re-sponses with filtered non-expert generations, producing reward-model training data without clinician labeling. 2) End-to-end DP-RLHF. A. Dataset and Preprocessing 

We utilize the medical dialogue corpus OpenMed/MedDia-log , which has been de-identified using Presidio [14]. This pro-cess replaces sensitive information with placeholders, such as the removal of email or phone number patterns and signature-like text. The DP guarantees are defined with respect to in-dividual training examples derived from these dialogues. Raw dialogues are converted into structured input–output pairs for SFT and preference learning. In addition, we apply lightweight text normalization and perform group-aware dataset splitting based on conversation identifiers to prevent cross-split leakage. To support reward modeling without human annotation, we generate “rejected” responses using a general-purpose lan-guage model (e.g., Llama-3-8B) prompted to emulate a non-expert medical assistant. We then apply heuristic quality filter-ing to remove low-quality samples, discarding outputs shorter than 10 words, containing explicit refusal patterns (e.g., “I am an AI”), or exhibiting substantial repetition. Because reward modeling benefits from a clear margin between preferred and dispreferred outputs, we further deduplicate preference pairs by semantic similarity. Specifically, each response is embedded using a sentence transformer ( all-MiniLM-L6-v2 ), and we remove pairs whose chosen (expert) and rejected (gen-erated) responses exceed a cosine similarity threshold of 0.90 .Finally, we partition the dataset into training ( 80% ), validation (10% ), and test ( 10% ) splits using a group-aware strategy keyed by source conversation identifiers, ensuring that turns from the same multi-turn session do not appear across multiple splits. The resulting corpus yields high-quality, de-duplicated supervision and preference pairs that clearly separate expert medical advice from generic assistant responses. 

B. Preference Pair Construction 

Reward model training relies on ranked preference pairs 

(x, y w, y l) constructed via an expert-versus-non-expert strat-egy that avoids manual annotation. The chosen response yw is the original physician response provided in the dataset, while the rejected response yl is generated by prompting a general-purpose generator model (e.g., Llama-3-8B) to behave as a non-expert assistant. Concretely, we condition the generator model on the patient query and a system instruction to provide concise, high-level guidance while explicitly avoiding detailed differential diagnosis. The prompt takes the following form: 

[System]: ... Avoid detailed differential diagnosis ... [Patient]: {patient_text} [Doctor]: 

We sample with nucleus sampling ( p = 0 .95 ) at temperature 

0.7, and we constrain outputs to matched lengths (maximum 256 tokens) so that rejected responses remain plausible yet systematically less specific than the expert reference. To ensure the constructed pairs contain a meaningful pref-erence signal, we apply a multi-stage filtering pipeline. We first remove degenerate generations using heuristic criteria (minimum length, refusal-pattern detection, and repetition filtering). We then apply semantic filtering by embedding both the physician response and the generated response with sentence-transformers/all-MiniLM-L6-v2 

and discarding pairs whose cosine similarity is ≥ 0.90 . Op-tionally, we apply a heuristic judge that computes a composite score based on length penalties, medical terminology density, and safety-refusal detection, retaining only pairs where the chosen response exceeds the rejected response by at least 

0.20 . In practice, this procedure yields approximately 10 k– 

50 k preference pairs and encourages the reward model to prefer clinically grounded, expert-level responses over generic or hedged alternatives. 4

C. Supervised Fine-Tuning 

We adapt the base model to the medical dialogue domain via supervised fine-tuning on the processed conversations. Because these data may contain sensitive patient information, we perform DP-SFT using DP-SGD. Our implementation uses 

Opacus for privacy accounting and PEFT for parameter-efficient training. We freeze the backbone model parameters and train only LoRA [15] adapters inserted into the query, key, value, and output projection modules, with r=16 , α=32 , and dropout 0.1. For the reward model and PPO stages, we use adapters on the query and value projections only, with r=8 

(see Table II). 

Privacy Guarantees. We enforce per-example gradient norm bounds using DP-SGD with “ghost clipping,” constrain-ing the ℓ2 norm of each per-sample gradient to at most 1.0.We then add Gaussian noise to the aggregated gradients to achieve a strict privacy target budget, tracking privacy loss using a R´ enyi Differential Privacy (RDP) [16] accountant. This mechanism limits the influence of any single training example on the learned parameters, reducing the likelihood that the model memorizes identifiable patient content while still learning general medical dialogue patterns. 

D. Differentially Private RLHF 

Preference-based alignment is performed in two DP-protected phases within the secure environment of Zone 1 ,providing end-to-end privacy with respect to the sensitive training corpus. 

1) DP-Reward Modeling: We train a reward model by fine-tuning a separate copy of the base model on the constructed preference pairs using the same DP-SGD stack (Opacus [17] + LoRA [15]) employed in DP-SFT. The reward model learns to assign higher scalar scores to expert physician responses 

yw than to non-expert generations yl. Concretely, we optimize a standard pairwise preference loss (Bradley–Terry / logistic form): 

LRM (ϕ) = −E(x,y w ,y l) [log σ (rϕ(x, y w) − rϕ(x, y l))] ,

where rϕ(x, y ) is the scalar reward assigned to response 

y under prompt x and σ(·) is the sigmoid. The privacy expenditure for this stage, denoted εRM , is accounted for independently from SFT. 

2) Policy Optimization with Firewall: We align the DP-SFT policy using PPO [5]. We initialize the PPO actor from the DP-SFT checkpoint and use the same DP-SFT checkpoint as the reference policy πref for KL regularization. The private reward model—trained under DP in the previous phase—provides the optimization signal. We additionally regularize the policy to remain close to a reference model πref (typically the SFT model) via an explicit KL penalty:

Because the PPO prompts are derived from the same dia-logue corpus used for SFT and reward modeling, this stage also accesses dialogue-derived data. To maintain end-to-end privacy, we therefore apply DP-SGD to both the PPO actor and critic, with separate per-component privacy budgets. The total privacy cost of the system is thus the composition of all three stages: εtotal = εSFT + εRM + εPPO , with δ tracked analogously using the same RDP accountant [16]. 

E. Evaluation and Deployment 

Zone 3 (Evaluation and Deployment) operates outside the DP-protected training pipeline and accesses no private patient data or preference pairs. Evaluations are performed on held-out test prompts and generated responses, while deployment uses only user-provided inputs at inference time. 5

1) Evaluation: We evaluate PrivMedChat along three axes: utility, safety, and privacy. Automatic utility metrics (ROUGE-L, BERTScore, entity overlap, and perplexity) are computed on a larger held-out subset (5,000 prompts) to reduce variance in aggregate reporting. For safety checks and privacy attacks, we use a focused evaluation subset of n = 500 prompts and report 1,000-iteration paired bootstrap confidence intervals. All evaluation prompts are drawn from held-out test data and are not used during training. No private corpus is touched during these assessments; all prompts are non-sensitive. 

2) Deployment Considerations: In deployment, PrivMed-Chat processes only real-time user prompts, inheriting the formal (ε, δ )-DP guarantees from the DP-protected training stages. Practical safeguards include real-time monitoring for out-of-distribution inputs, human-in-the-loop escalation for high-risk queries, audit logging, and uncertainty-aware re-fusal mechanisms. The DP-SGD framework applied across all training stages provides formal privacy-by-design guarantees. We do not frame DP guarantees as a standalone compliance claim; real deployments still require institutional governance, monitoring, and clinical safety processes. III. I MPLEMENTATION 

We implement PrivMedChat in Python. The codebase sup-ports DP-SFT, DP reward modeling, and PPO-based RLHF with LoRA [15] and is publicly available 1. Differential privacy is enforced in all training stages that utilize dialogue-derived supervision through DP-SGD, implemented with Opacus [17]. We train PrivMedChat on 1–4 NVIDIA H200 GPUs. All components are initialized from the base model to lever-age its general-purpose dialogue and reasoning capabilities. We use parameter-efficient fine-tuning via LoRA [15] adapters throughout. The DP-SFT stage inserts adapters on the query, key, value, and output projection layers with r=16 , α=32 , and dropout 0.1. The reward model uses adapters on the query and value projections with r=8 , α=16 , and dropout 0.2. The PPO [5] actor and critic use adapters on the query and value projections with r=8 , α=32 , and dropout 0.1.

Training hyperparameters and DP accounting. Hyper-parameters are chosen to maintain stable optimization under per-sample clipping and noise injection. We adopt a staged learning-rate schedule, using a conservative learning rate dur-ing PPO [5] to mitigate policy collapse when combining RLHF with DP noise. Noise multipliers for DP-SGD are computed with an RDP-based accountant to ensure that cumulative privacy loss remains within the target ε budget across all three private training stages (DP-SFT, DP reward modeling, and DP-PPO). A. Experimental Setup 

We evaluate PrivMedChat on the held-out test split with em-phasis on three desiderata: clinical utility, safety, and privacy protection. Across all conditions, we use the base model as the common backbone and keep the LoRA [15] configuration fixed to control for capacity and optimization differences. This design isolates the effects of (i) the training objective (SFT vs. RLHF) and (ii) the privacy mechanism and privacy budget. 

Model variants and baselines. Our comparisons include five families of models. We first report results for the off-the-shelf base model (no adaptation), which provides a ref-erence point for zero-adaptation behavior. We then train non-private baselines using standard SFT and PPO [5]-based RLHF without differential privacy; these models serve as a practical upper bound on achievable utility under our training setup. We additionally train a non-private SFT model for 17 epochs as a deliberate Overfit SFT baseline (PPL ≈198.6), which amplifies memorization and provides a stress test for MIA sensitivity. To characterize the utility–privacy trade-off under private learning, we train DP-SFT models with privacy budgets 

ε ∈ { 1, 3, 5, 7} at fixed δ = 10 −5. Finally, we instantiate PrivMedChat using our full DP-RLHF pipeline—where a DP-SFT policy is aligned via PPO using a separately trained DP reward model—and match the cumulative privacy budget to the DP-SFT settings (same ε set, same δ), enabling a direct assessment of whether the RLHF stage improves performance at equivalent privacy cost. 

1) Evaluation Metrics: We quantify response quality using complementary lexical, semantic, and domain-specific metrics computed on the held-out test set. Unless otherwise stated, all metrics are computed by comparing generated responses to the reference physician responses. 

a) ROUGE-L: We compute ROUGE-L [22], which mea-sures longest common subsequence (LCS) overlap between the generated response and the reference. We report ROUGE-L (F1) with standard tokenization. 

b) BERTScore: We compute BERTScore (F1) [23], a semantic similarity metric that uses contextual embeddings to align tokens between candidate and reference texts and combines precision and recall into an F1 score. We report the mean F1 value across the test set. 

c) Perplexity (PPL): Perplexity is computed by con-ditioning on the prompt and evaluating only the reference response under teacher forcing, using the tokenizer of the model being assessed. 6

2) Safety Evaluation: To assess safety, we apply targeted heuristic checks designed to capture common failure modes in medical dialogue, including inappropriate diagnostic certainty, potentially harmful instructions, and missing escalation for ur-gent symptoms. These checks are not a substitute for clinician evaluation, but they provide a consistent proxy for comparing model variants under matched prompts and decoding settings. We therefore interpret safety results as comparative signals within our experimental setup, and we discuss remaining limitations and failure cases in Sec. V. 

3) Privacy Evaluation: We evaluate privacy leakage using two empirical attack protocols that capture complementary notions of memorization and membership inference. First, we conduct MIA using both loss-based signals and reference-based variants. We report the Area Under the ROC Curve (AUC) and the true positive rate at 1% false positive rate (TPR@1%,FPR), which summarizes an attacker’s ability to distinguish training members from non-members. Second, we measure verbatim memorization via canary exposure [7], where synthetic canary strings are inserted into training data and we test whether the trained model can reproduce them under targeted prompting. E. Ablation Studies 

DP noise in the reward model does not degrade downstream policy quality; the resulting PPO policies match or exceed non-private PPO on ROUGE-L and BERTScore, underscoring the robustness of pairwise preference learning. Varying ε

within {1, 3, 5, 7} produces only narrow performance bands (ROUGE-L 0.146–0.156, BERTScore 0.836–0.839), allowing practitioners to prioritize stronger privacy with minimal utility penalty. The consistency of near-chance MIA performance across six attack families confirms that observed privacy is not attacker-specific. 8

V. D ISCUSSION 

Our findings establish DP-RLHF as a viable framework for training privacy-preserving medical dialogue models. PrivMedChat maintains competitive automatic utility met-rics, delivers measurable safety improvements over both non-private and DP-SFT baselines, and exhibits near-chance perfor-mance under a comprehensive suite of membership inference attacks. Among the evaluated privacy budgets, the ε = 7 vari-ant of PrivMedChat provides the strongest overall operating point, achieving the highest ROUGE-L (0.156) and Entity F1 (0.103) among all DP models while preserving formal 

(ε, δ = 10 −5) guarantees and near-random MIA AUC values. 

Privacy–Utility Trade-off. Utility metrics remain stable across privacy budgets ( ε ∈ {1, 3, 5, 7}), with ROUGE-L varying only between 0.146–0.156 and BERTScore between 0.836–0.839 for PrivMedChat. This narrow performance band indicates that moderate DP noise, when combined with LoRA and preference alignment, acts as effective regularization with-out proportionally degrading downstream response quality. Crucially, increasing the privacy budget does not yield mono-tonic gains, suggesting that practitioners can select lower ε

values with minimal expected utility loss in this domain. 

Safety and Clinical Relevance. DP-RLHF yields consis-tent safety gains. PrivMedChat reduces hallucination rates to 1.4–3.0% (versus 1.2–3.2% for DP-SFT) and maintains low harmful advice rates across all budgets (0.2–0.8%). These improvements demonstrate that the RLHF stage successfully internalizes preference-based safety constraints even under dif-ferential privacy. Medication validation and emergency escala-tion behaviors remain stable across all variants, indicating that DP noise does not impair critical clinical reasoning capabili-ties. Such enhancements are particularly relevant for clinician-assistive tools and patient-facing applications where factual accuracy and conservative risk management are paramount. 

Privacy Guarantees. All DP models achieve AUC-ROC values between 0.510–0.555 across six distinct MIA families, statistically indistinguishable from random guessing. DP-SGD applied consistently across all training stages ensures com-prehensive privacy protection, as confirmed by identical MIA performance between DP-SFT and PrivMedChat at matched ε.Zero canary extractions across 25 inserted sequences further corroborate the absence of detectable verbatim memorization. While non-private baselines also show near-chance MIA sig-nals under our limited-epoch regime, only DP models provide the formal (ε, δ ) guarantee that bounds worst-case influence independent of attack strategy. 

Limitations. Several limitations should be noted. First, DP-SGD incurs non-trivial computational overhead from per-sample gradient clipping and noise addition, as well as multi-stage privacy accounting. Second, preference data construc-tion still relies on proxy reward models and limited expert annotation. Third, our evaluation, while extensive, remains benchmark-driven and may not fully reflect real-world clinical distribution shifts or workflow integration. Fourth, our privacy analysis, although broad, is necessarily empirical and finite-sample; theoretical guarantees hold, but adaptive or future attacks could warrant continued scrutiny. 

Ethical and Deployment Considerations. Differential pri-vacy substantially reduces memorization risk but does not con-stitute a complete solution. Responsible deployment requires complementary safeguards including human oversight, con-tinuous monitoring, explicit escalation protocols, institutional review board (IRB) approval where applicable, and governance frameworks. Formal privacy accounting should be viewed as one pillar within a broader safety, compliance, and clinical validation stack rather than a standalone certification. 

Future Work. Promising directions include stage-specific privacy budget allocation, development of adaptive or stronger membership inference attacks, extension to multimodal med-ical inputs, and prospective studies with clinician-in-the-loop evaluation. Enhancing uncertainty quantification and refusal mechanisms under DP noise remains a critical step toward safer real-world deployment of privacy-preserving medical LLMs. VI. R ELATED WORK 

We review prior work along three axes motivating PrivMed-Chat: (i) medical dialogue systems and domain adaptation of large language models, (ii) preference-based alignment meth-ods such as RLHF, and (iii) formal privacy mechanisms for language modeling. Existing medical chatbots focus on utility and safety without end-to-end privacy guarantees. Conversely, differentially private fine-tuning for LLMs rarely addresses preference optimization and reward modeling. This gap mo-tivates our end-to-end DP-RLHF formulation for medical dialogue alignment, combining scalable preference construc-tion with rigorous privacy accounting and empirical leakage evaluation. 

A. Medical Dialogue Systems and LLM Fine-Tuning 

The development of medical dialogue systems has advanced significantly with the adaptation of LLMs to clinical do-mains. Early efforts focused on task-specific models trained on doctor-patient conversation datasets, such as MedDialog [13], which provides large-scale de-identified dialogues for supervised fine-tuning. Subsequent works, including MedAl-paca [26] and ChatDoctor [2], demonstrated that domain-adaptive pre-training or instruction fine-tuning on medical corpora (e.g., PubMed abstracts, clinical notes, and online consultations) substantially improves diagnostic reasoning and response quality. More recent models, such as BioMistral [3] and OpenBioLLMs [27], leverage open-source bases (Mistral, Llama-3) fine-tuned on biomedical literature and synthetic dialogues, achieving strong performance on benchmarks like MedQA [28] and HealthSearchQA [29]. These systems emphasize personalization by conditioning responses on patient history and symptoms, but rely heavily on SFT or instruction tuning on potentially sensitive data, raising privacy concerns that remain largely unaddressed. 

B. RLHF in Healthcare 

RLHF has become a cornerstone of LLM alignment, en-abling models to produce helpful, safe, and preference-aligned 9

outputs [4], [30]. In general domains, RLHF pipelines— comprising reward modeling from human preferences and policy optimization via PPO or DPO-have powered models like InstructGPT [4] and Llama-3 [31]. In healthcare, RLHF adoption is limited due to the high cost and ethical challenges of collecting clinician preferences. Alternatives include reward modeling from synthetic preferences [32] or rule-based sig-nals, as in ClinicalCamel [33], and RLAIF approaches that use LLM-as-judge for ranking [34]. Works such as PMC-LLaMA [35] and CareBot [36] incorporate preference optimization on medical instruction data, improving safety and empathy, yet none apply formal privacy guarantees during the preference-based alignment stage. 

C. Differential Privacy in Language Models 

DP offers a principled defense against memorization and inference attacks in machine learning [8]. In LLMs, DP-SGD has been successfully applied during pre-training [9] and fine-tuning [10], with ghost clipping and parameter-efficient methods (e.g., LoRA) mitigating utility loss [37]. Recent advances explore DP at various granularities (example-level, user-level) and stages, showing viable privacy-utility trade-offs in general text tasks [38], [11]. However, extending DP to the full RLHF pipeline—particularly reward modeling and PPO/DPO—remains underexplored due to heightened noise sensitivity in preference optimization [39]. To our knowledge, no prior work has applied end-to-end DP-RLHF in the medical domain. 

D. Privacy Attacks and Defenses in Health Informatics 

LLMs trained on private data are vulnerable to MIA [40], [7], attribute inference, and training data extraction [7], with demonstrated risks in biomedical text [41]. In health infor-matics, these threats are amplified by regulatory requirements (HIPAA, GDPR) and the sensitivity of protected health in-formation [42]. Defenses beyond DP include redaction, syn-thetic data generation [43], and auditing frameworks [44], but empirical evaluations in medical dialogue systems are sparse. Recent benchmarks highlight substantial memorization in domain-adapted medical LLMs [12], underscoring the need for rigorous privacy mechanisms during both fine-tuning and alignment. Our work bridges these areas by introducing the first dif-ferentially private RLHF framework tailored for personalized medical dialogue, with scalable annotation-free preference construction and comprehensive clinical evaluation. VII. C ONCLUSION 

We introduced PrivMedChat, an end-to-end differentially private RLHF framework for privacy-preserving medical di-alogue alignment. By applying DP-SGD to all three training stages—supervised fine-tuning, reward modeling, and PPO policy optimization—PrivMedChat delivers formal (ε, δ ) guar-antees alongside competitive utility and markedly improved safety. On the held-out test set, the ε=7 variant of PrivMed-Chat achieves the strongest overall privacy–utility–safety bal-ance among all DP models, with the highest ROUGE-L and Entity F1 scores under differential privacy, reduced halluci-nation rates, near-zero harmful advice, and robust empirical privacy (near-chance performance across six membership in-ference attacks and zero canary extractions). These findings demonstrate that privacy-preserving align-ment of clinical LLMs is both practical and effective, re-covering most utility lost to DP noise while strengthening safety-critical behaviors. The framework is readily extensible to private preference optimization in other high-stakes do-mains. Future work includes stage-specific budget allocation, evaluation against adaptive attacks, multimodal extensions, and prospective clinician-in-the-loop validation. ACKNOWLEDGMENT

A. Differential Privacy 

Let D = {zi}ni=1 denote a dataset of n records. In our setting, each record zi corresponds to a training example de-rived from a doctor–patient dialogue (e.g., a single supervised input–output pair or a single preference pair). Two datasets 

D and D′ are adjacent if they differ in exactly one record. Unless stated otherwise, we consider example-level adjacency; we discuss conversation-level adjacency in Sec. A-A2. 

Definition 1 (( ε, δ)-Differential Privacy [8]) . A randomized mechanism M : D → R satisfies (ε, δ )-DP if for all adjacent datasets D, D ′ and all measurable sets S ⊆ R ,

Pr[ M(D) ∈ S] ≤ eε Pr[ M(D′) ∈ S] + δ. 

Intuitively, (ε, δ )-DP upper-bounds how much the presence of any single record can change the distribution of the mecha-nism’s output. Smaller ε implies stronger privacy; δ is typically set to a negligible value (e.g., δ = 10 −5 in our experiments). 

1) DP-SGD for neural network training: We train our models with differential privacy using DP-SGD [9]. In each iteration, DP-SGD computes per-example gradients, clips each gradient to an ℓ2 norm bound (clipping norm C) to control sensitivity, then adds Gaussian noise proportional to C (scaled by the noise multiplier σ) to the aggregated minibatch gradient before applying the update. We use Opacus [17] for the DP-SGD implementation and privacy accounting, and report (ε, δ )

using an RDP accountant [16]. 11 

2) Adjacency choices and clinical data granularity: While our default definition uses example-level adjacency, health-care deployments may demand stronger notions such as 

conversation-level or user-level DP, where adjacency corre-sponds to adding/removing all examples from a single patient or a single dialogue session. Formally, let D = Smu=1 Du be a partition of records by user/session u. User-level adjacency treats D and D′ as adjacent if one entire group Du is added or removed. This setting typically increases effective sensitivity and thus requires either larger noise or fewer training steps to achieve the same ε. In PrivMedChat, we mitigate cross-session leakage in evaluation by split construction (group-aware partitioning) and reserve user-level accounting as a direct extension when deployment requirements demand it.

C. Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning 

Large medical LLMs are expensive to fine-tune under DP because DP-SGD requires per-example gradients and introduces noise. We therefore adopt Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) [15] to reduce the number of trainable parameters while preserving expressivity. For a linear projection with weight matrix W ∈ Rdout ×din ,LoRA re-parameterizes updates as 

W ′ = W + ∆ W, ∆W = BA, 

where A ∈ Rr×din , B ∈ Rdout ×r , and r ≪ min( din , d out ) is the rank. The pretrained W is frozen and only (A, B ) are trained. 

1) LoRA under DP-SGD: Training only LoRA parameters reduces (i) memory footprint, (ii) per-step compute for per-example gradients, and (iii) the dimensionality of the pa-rameter space into which DP noise is injected. While DP guarantees are independent of parameter count, in practice LoRA can improve the privacy–utility trade-off by enabling larger effective batch sizes and more stable optimization under clipping and noise. We implement DP training using Opacus [17] with per-sample gradient computation and “ghost clipping” to effi-ciently bound ℓ2 norms at the example level. I’m happy to help...”) and often defers with follow-up questions rather than providing actionable guidance.
