Abstract. Multi-modal large language models (MM-LLMs) have shown strong performance in medical image understanding and clinical reason-ing. Recent medical agent systems extend them with tool use and multi-agent collaboration, enabling complex decision-making beyond single-pass inference. However, these systems rely almost entirely on propri-etary frontier models (e.g., GPT), whose API-based deployment incurs high cost, high latency, and privacy risks that conflict with on-premise clinical requirements. We present Meissa , a lightweight 4B-parameter medical MM-LLM that brings full agentic capability offline. Instead of imitating static answers, Meissa learns both when to engage exter-nal interaction (strategy selection) and how to execute multi-step in-teraction (strategy execution) by distilling structured trajectories from frontier agent systems. To be specific, we propose: (1) Unified tra-jectory modeling : trajectories (reasoning and action traces) are rep-resented within a single state–action–observation formalism, allowing one model to generalize across heterogeneous medical environments. (2) 

Three-tier stratified supervision : the model’s own errors trigger pro-gressive escalation from direct reasoning to tool-augmented and multi-agent interaction, explicitly learning difficulty-aware strategy selection. (3) Prospective–retrospective supervision : pairing exploratory for-ward traces with hindsight-rationalized execution traces enables stable learning of effective interaction policies. Trained on ∼40K curated tra-jectories, Meissa matches or exceeds proprietary frontier agents in 10 of 16 evaluation settings across 13 medical benchmarks spanning radiol-ogy, pathology, and clinical reasoning. Using over 25 × fewer parameters than typical frontier models like Gemini-3, Meissa operates fully offline with ∼22 × lower end-to-end latency compared to API-based deployment. Data, models, and environments are released at https://github.com/Schuture/Meissa.

## 1 Introduction 

Multi-modal large language models (MM-LLMs) can combine medical images with free-form clinical text, enabling unified visual understanding and clinical

reasoning [40,42,52]. When extended with agentic capabilities , the ability to take external actions such as invoking tools, requesting intermediate evidence, and co-ordinating specialist roles, these models can solve clinical tasks that are difficult for single-pass prediction systems [13, 25, 41]. However, today’s strongest med-ical agents are usually powered by proprietary frontier models (e.g., GPT [20], Gemini [45], Claude [3]) through cloud APIs. This deployment pattern is often incompatible with real clinical constraints: patient data must remain on-premise, repeated API calls increase cost, and multi-step interaction adds latency that can disrupt clinical workflows. This paper is motivated by a simple tension: frontier agents are capable, but hard to deploy. We ask: Can we transfer the action-taking behavior of frontier medical agents into a model that can run fully offline? Here, agentic behavior 

refers to a model’s interaction policy : deciding whether to answer directly or to take external actions, and if it acts, choosing a sequence of actions based on observed outcomes. This differs from prior works distilling single-pass reason-ing ability [15, 39, 47, 58] from larger models to smaller models, and from those learning tool-use in a single fixed pipeline [17, 21, 57], because practical medical settings involve multiple interaction modes (direct reasoning, tool chains, visual feedback loops, role-based consultation, and multi-turn clinical questioning). 

The core challenge is the lack of structured supervision for agentic behavior. Although frontier models exhibit strong agentic behaviors, there is currently no publicly available training corpus designed to teach models how to behave as medical agents. Existing benchmarks provide final answers but do not specify whether external interaction was necessary or how actions should evolve based on intermediate observations. On the other hand, existing agent systems generate agentic trajectories (reasoning and action traces) within isolated envi-ronments, each with its own format and protocol, making it difficult to construct unified supervision across heterogeneous interaction modes. To address this gap, we design a data synthesis pipeline that systematically constructs supervision for agentic behavior along three dimensions. First , we in-troduce a unified state–action–observation representation that models multiple interaction modes within a single formalism. Therefore, we avoid environment-specific formats and enable cross-environment learning within one model. Sec-ond , to teach when to act, we propose a three-tier stratification strategy driven by the model’s own errors. Queries that the model already solves become direct-reasoning trajectories (tier 1); queries solvable by a stronger frontier model with-out tools become enhanced reasoning trajectories (tier 2); and only the hardest residual samples trigger full agent environments (tier 3). This stratified supervi-sion implicitly encodes the decision boundary between direct inference and ex-ternal interaction. Third , to teach how to act within environments, we construct complementary prospective and retrospective trajectories. Prospective traces are recorded during forward interaction and capture exploratory decision-making under real observations. Retrospective traces preserve the same action sequence but provide clean hindsight explanations. Together, they provide stable execu-tion supervision across heterogeneous medical agent environments. Based on this pipeline, we train Meissa 3, a 4B-parameter multi-modal medi-cal agent initialized from Qwen3-VL-4B model and distilled from approximately 40K frontier multimodal agent’s (Gemini-3-flash) trajectories. Training requires only ∼12 hours on 8 ×A6000 GPUs. Despite its lightweight size, Meissa is com-petitive with frontier models using over 25 × fewer parameters across 13 medical benchmarks (Sec. 4.2), matches or exceeds them in 10 of 16 evaluation settings, achieves near-oracle strategy selection, and reduces end-to-end latency by ap-proximately 22 × (Sec. 4.3) compared to cloud-based agent deployment. In summary, our work makes three contributions: (1) we introduce a unified trajectory representation and targeted synthesis pipeline for constructing super-vision of medical agentic behavior across heterogeneous environments; (2) we show Meissa , a lightweight medical agentic model, can make complex agentic behaviors available for offline deployment with low cost and inference latency; (3) we provide extensive empirical evidence that Meissa learns meaningful policy and matches closed-source models on both in-distribution and out-of-distribution (OOD) benchmarks. 

## 2 Related Work 

2.1 Medical Multi-modal Large Language Models 

Medical MM-LLMs have evolved from specialized architectures [9, 28, 29, 33, 34, 48] to adaptations of general-purpose foundations [39, 46, 51]. LLaVA-Med [28] demonstrated biomedical VQA through instruction-tuned visual chat [31], CheX-agent [9] specialized in chest X-ray interpretation, and HuatuoGPT-Vision [7] injected medical visual knowledge at scale. Med-PaLM [43,44], Med-Gemini [46], and RadFM [49] further pushed performance through model scaling and propri-etary data. However, these models operate as single-pass reasoners without the ability to invoke tools or adaptively route queries based on difficulty. 

2.2 Tool-Augmented Reasoning and Its Medical Applications 

ReAct [52] established reasoning-action interleaving; FireAct [6] and AgentTun-ing [53] showed that fine-tuning on agent trajectories generalizes agentic capabil-ities across tasks. In the visual domain, DeepEyes [57] incentivized “thinking with images” via RL, and Ophiuchus [21] adapted this for medical imaging through cold-start SFT plus agentic RL. In the medical domain, MedRAX [13] inte-grated specialized tools for chest X-ray reasoning. Closely related, STeP [8] and STAR [50] train student models from teacher trajectories via trajectory-aligned SFT, and MedVistaGym [32] frames tool-augmented medical reasoning as an RL problem. Our work differs in distilling agentic behavior across four hetero-geneous environments into a single model, and in showing that SFT alone can match RL pipelines at substantially lower compute.

2.3 Multi-Agent Systems for Medicine 

MDAgents [25] dispatches queries to solo experts or multi-agent debate panels based on difficulty; MedAgentSim [2] and AgentClinic [41] created simulated clinical environments for multi-turn diagnosis. Park et al. [37] demonstrated emergent behaviors from generative agent simulations, Du et al. [12] showed debate improves factual accuracy, and Society of Mind [59] explored hierarchical organization. All require frontier LLM backends at inference. We instead distill multi-agent interaction policies into a single model. 

2.4 Learned Routing, Selective Prediction, and Early Exit 

A model that can invoke external tools must also learn when not to . RouteLLM [35] and cascade routing [11] train lightweight routers to select between a cheap and an expensive LLM; confidence-token methods [10] embed routing inside the model itself. Selective classification [14] provides theoretical grounding for risk-coverage trade-offs. Our work differs in learning routing implicitly via stratified trajectory supervision. The model’s generated reasoning trajectory determines interaction depth without an explicit router, and in treating routing as a contin-uous choice over depth ( T =0 to T > 10 ) across four heterogeneous environments, a setting not addressed by existing frameworks. 

## 3 Method 

We frame the training of Meissa as agentic behavior distillation : learning a policy over interaction depth , which is the number of external actions an agent takes before producing an answer. This single variable unifies direct reasoning (depth = 0 ), single-tool queries ( depth = 1 ), multi-step tool chains, multi-agent debate, and clinical simulation into a continuous spectrum. We first define a tra-jectory formalism that captures this spectrum (Sec. 3.1), then describe how strat-ified trajectories teach strategy selection (Sec. 3.2), the agent environments that serve as trajectory sources (Sec. 3.3), and the prospective-retrospective supervi-sion that teaches strategy execution (Sec. 3.4). Figure 1 provides an overview.

3.2 Behavior Distillation via Stratified Trajectory Supervision 

The key insight for teaching strategy selection is that not all queries require the same interaction depth. Rather than uniformly generating complex agentic trajectories for all samples, we stratify trajectory supervision using the model’s own errors as a curriculum signal [5]: 

Tier 1: Direct Reasoning Trajectories. We first run the student model 

MS (Qwen3-VL-4B [4]) directly on the training set Dtrain . Samples answered 6 Chen et al. 

correctly yield direct reasoning trajectories ( T =0 , no external interaction): 

Ddirect = {(x, τ direct ) | (x, y ) ∈ D train , MS (x) = y}. (2) These trajectories supervise the model to answer efficiently when its parametric knowledge suffices, learning when not to act .

Tier 2: Enhanced Reasoning Trajectories. The student’s incorrect samples are re-processed by a stronger teacher model MT (Gemini-3-flash [45]), again without external interaction: 

Denhanced = {(x, τ enhanced ) | (x, y ) ∈ D train \ D direct , MT (x) = y}. (3) These trajectories demonstrate stronger reasoning that the student could not produce, but remain direct reasoning ( T =0 ). 

Tier 3: Agentic Trajectories. The hardest residual samples are beyond both the student’s and teacher’s direct reasoning capacity. They are processed by the teacher within agent environments (Sec. 3.3): 

Dagentic = {(x, τ agentic ) | (x, y ) ∈ D residual , AT (x) = y}, (4) where AT denotes the teacher operating within an agent environment and resid-ual erroneous samples Dresidual = Dtrain \(Ddirect ∪D enhanced ). Only these samples produce full multi-step trajectories with external interaction. The complete data generation procedure, including retry logic, per-tier statis-tics, and discard handling, is detailed in Algorithm 1 in supplementary material. The final dataset contains ∼40K trajectories: 8.2K direct (Tier 1), 9.8K enhanced (Tier 2), and 23.9K agentic (Tier 3). The combined supervision teaches strategy selection as an emergent behavior: simple queries are associated with direct trajectories, complex queries with multi-step ones, naturally inducing a difficulty-aware routing policy. At inference, the model’s first generated action implicitly determines the strategy.

3.4 Prospective-Retrospective Supervision 

Learning when to act is addressed by stratified trajectories; here we address learning how to act effectively within agent environments. The key insight is that a single correct trajectory can be decomposed into two complementary supervision signals that teach different aspects of execution policy. 

Prospective (forward) trajectories are recorded during real-time inference: the teacher reasons forward, potentially revising strategy upon unexpected ob-servations. Only correct trajectories are retained, teaching an exploration policy .

Retrospective (backward) trajectories are generated given each prospective trajectory: a recap agent re-narrates the reasoning while preserving the original action sequence, producing a clean post-hoc narrative that teaches an optimal execution policy . The combined training set is: 

DSFT = Ddirect ∪ D enhanced ∪ { τ pro (x), τ retro (x, y ) | (x, y ) ∈ D agentic }. (5) During auditing, retrospective recaps that are over-simplified or over-complicated are discarded; approximately 8% of generated recaps are filtered. We did not ob-serve significant gains from depth- or difficulty-based weighting in preliminary experiments. A detailed comparison of the two supervision modes and trajectory validation details (structural and behavioral filtering) are in the supplementary.

4.2 Meissa Is Competitive with Frontier Models 

Table 2 presents results across all 13 benchmarks under four agent evaluation frameworks. Meissa is ranked 1st or 2nd on 10 out of 16 evaluation settings, matching frontier models. We summarize more detailed findings below: 

Trajectory supervision advantage. Meissa surpasses Qwen3-VL-4B by 17.3 points and the answer-only tuned Qwen3-VL-4B direct inference baseline by 6.1 points on average, showing that trajectory supervision conveys substantially richer learning signal than data exposure alone. 

OOD robustness. On the five strict OOD benchmarks, Meissa achieves strong results, most notably 62.8% on ChestAgentBench and 35.0% on NEJM (averaged with NEJM Ext.), matching or exceeding GPT-4o despite being 100 × smaller. Gains on ChestAgentBench are most pronounced on classification and detection 

sub-categories, where tool-generated classification/grounding information helps enhance the image understanding beyond the Qwen3-VL model itself. 

Data-rich VQA tasks. Meissa achieves the top result on PathVQA (78.2%; 12.2K training samples) and MIMIC-CXR-VQA (65.2%; 11.4K samples), rank-ing first among all methods in both cases. Notably, the base Qwen3-VL-4B already shows competitive baselines on these tasks (65.3% and 51.4%), reflect-ing that data abundance lowers the learning barrier for lightweight models. The gains attributable to Meissa thus stem primarily from learned interaction policy rather than bridging a parametric knowledge gap. 

Reference-grounded tasks. On PubMedQA (77.9%, first), Meissa outper-forms GPT-4V under MDAgents by 2.9 points. PubMedQA supplies a reference abstract alongside each question, shifting the demand from encyclopedic recall to evidence synthesis over provided context. This is precisely the execution skill that prospective-retrospective trajectory supervision optimizes, explaining Meissa ’s disproportionate gain relative to its performance on knowledge-only variants. 10 Chen et al.

4.3 Strategy Selection: Learning When to Act Counterfactual and oracle comparison. We compare Meissa ’s learned rout-ing against fixed strategies and an oracle router that selects the best strategy per query (Tab. 3) according to the samples’ testing correctness. Learned routing closes the gap to oracle, demonstrating near-optimal strat-egy selection: 72% of queries are routed to direct reasoning and 28% to agentic interaction. Notably, the always-agentic strategy degrades average accuracy rela-tive to always-direct, because overlong contexts and occasionally misleading tool outputs hurt easy queries that require no external information. The latency gap is even more striking: Gemini-3-flash averages 87.2s per query due to multiple API calls and remote tool execution, while Meissa averages 4.1s, a ∼22 × speedup. ## 5 Discussion and Conclusion 

We presented Meissa , a 4B-parameter medical agent trained through agen-tic behavior distillation. Stratified trajectory supervision teaches when to act; prospective-retrospective supervision teaches how to act; a unified trajectory for-malism enables learning across tool calling, visual reasoning, multi-agent debate, and clinical simulation. Trained on ∼40K trajectories, Meissa is competitive with frontier models across 13 benchmarks while operating fully offline. 

Why SFT over RL? Our framework is intentionally SFT-only. While RL can sharpen stopping behavior via outcome-based rewards [32], it faces two practical limitations. First, existing RL approaches are each tied to a single agent frame-work: DeepEyesV2 optimizes tool-calling via RL [17], and MARTI addresses multi-agent coordination [54]; none can jointly learn policies across the four 14 Chen et al. A.1 Algorithm: Stratified Trajectory Data Generation 

Algorithm 1 presents the complete procedure for stratified trajectory data gener-ation, including the three-tier hierarchy, retry logic for agentic trajectories ( K=8 

retries per residual sample), and discard handling for persistently failed samples. 

Key design choices: – Retry logic : Each residual sample receives up to K=8 attempts in the agent environment. Single API-call success rate on residual samples is 89%; the re-maining incomplete samples are discarded rather than included with incorrect trajectories. 

– Environment selection : Each sample is evaluated in a pre-defined environ-ment based on dataset-to-environment evaluation setting (e.g., MIMIC-CXR-VQA → tool calling, MedQA → multi-agent collaboration). 

– Validation : Valid (τ ) applies structural and behavioral filters (see Sec. A.5) before accepting a trajectory. 

– Prospective-retrospective pairing : For each accepted agentic trajectory, both the original prospective trace and a retrospective re-narration are gen-erated, doubling the agentic supervision signal.

A.5 Trajectory Validation Pipeline 

All generated trajectories undergo a two-stage validation pipeline before inclu-sion in the training set. 

Structural Filtering Structural filters verify format compliance: 

– Correctness : Only trajectories arriving at the correct answer are retained (answer (τ ) = y). 

– Turn-type integrity : Conversations must follow the human → (function_call 

→ observation )∗ → gpt pattern. 

– Tool call validity : Each function_call must reference a tool defined in the system prompt with valid JSON arguments. 

– Terminate presence : Every trajectory must end with a termination ac-tion (tool-specific: Terminate for thinking with image and clinical simulation, 

[FINAL] marker for MedRAX, synthesis turn for MDAgents). 

– Image alignment : The number of <image> tokens must match the number of entries in the images list. 

– Length bounds : Trajectories exceeding 10,000 characters total or containing 

>4 tool calls (MedRAX) / >6 rounds (thinking with image) / >12 rounds (multi-agent collaboration & clinical simulation) are discarded. 

Behavioral Filtering Behavioral filters detect semantic quality issues: 

– Hallucination detection : Scan <think> blocks for medical keywords (pathol-ogy names, anatomical terms) not present in the original question or tool outputs. If a medical term appears in reasoning but not in any input or ob-servation, the trajectory is flagged and reviewed. Meissa : Multi-modal Medical Agentic Intelligence 21

Tool-Calling Environment (MedRAX on ChestAgentBench) 

Pattern 1: Over-Invocation of Tools on Simple Questions. Example.
