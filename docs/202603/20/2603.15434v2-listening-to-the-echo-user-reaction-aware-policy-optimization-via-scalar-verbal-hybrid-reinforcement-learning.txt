Abstract 

While current emotional support dialogue sys-tems typically rely on expert-defined scalar re-wards for alignment, these signals suffer from severe information sparsity. They cannot ex-plain why a response failed or how to adapt to dynamic user states, often diverging from the actual goal of facilitating positive emotional shifts. In practice, the most direct and reli-able learning signal naturally emerges from the user’s continuous reactions during the ongoing interaction . Therefore, we propose 

Reaction Aware Policy Optimization (RAPO), a framework that optimizes over interaction consequences rather than rubric scores. RAPO treats dialogue as a reaction-driven process and utilizes simulated user responses to gen-erate dense natural-language feedback through three core components: (1) Hindsight Dia-logue Selection, which isolates pivotal turns that meaningfully alter user emotional trajecto-ries; (2) Generative Hindsight Feedback, which transforms user reactions into contrastive rank-ing signals and natural-language critiques; and (3) Scalar–Verbal Hybrid Policy Optimization, which couples scalar reward optimization for global alignment with verbal feedback dis-tillation for fine-grained semantic refinement. Extensive experiments on ESC and S OTOPIA 

demonstrate that RAPO significantly outper-forms strong RL baselines in driving positive interaction outcomes. 

1 Introduction 

Emotional Support Conversation (ESC) aims to alleviate users’ emotional distress, foster a sense of being understood, and encourage continued en-gagement (Liu et al., 2021). With the rapid ad-vancement of large language models (LLMs), re-cent work has made notable progress in generating fluent and seemingly empathetic responses through supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on expert demonstra-

> *Corresponding Author

tions (Zheng et al., 2024; Ye et al., 2025c) or rein-forcement learning (RL) (Zhou et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2025; Ma et al., 2025). However, most ex-isting approaches share a common optimization paradigm: they either imitate static expert-written responses or optimize scalar rewards derived from expert-defined rubrics, assuming that empathy qual-ity can be predefined and universally agreed upon. This paradigm exposes a critical limitation: it treats emotional support as a static, isolated scoring task rather than a dynamic, interactive process. 

First, optimization mismatch. Relying on static expert rubrics creates a fundamental mis-match with actual user experiences (Ye et al., 2026). A response that scores highly on predefined empa-thy metrics might still feel hollow to the individual seeking support, a phenomenon we term " halluci-nated empathy ". For instance, a model repeatedly outputting safe templates like “ I’m sorry to hear that. That must be really hard, ” can earn perfect scalar scores while leaving the user feeling disen-gaged. Because static rubrics cannot dynamically observe user reactions, they optimize the model to look supportive by expert standards rather than actually being supportive in practice. 

Second, signal sparsity and ambiguity. Even when scalar rewards correctly identify a poor re-sponse, compressing a complex supportive conver-sation into a single number leads to severe infor-mation loss (Luo et al., 2025). Identical low scores can mask entirely different failure modes, such as offering premature advice versus inadvertently in-validating the user’s feelings. These sparse scalar signals fail to explain why a response failed or how 

the model should adapt to dynamic shifts in the user’s emotional state. Without explicit natural-language guidance grounded in the conversational context, the policy lacks the necessary supervision for fine-grained semantic refinement. Building on this insight, we introduce Reaction

3.2 Hindsight Dialogue Selection 

Standard training paradigms typically utilize all available turns in an ESC corpus. However, a sub-stantial fraction of these turns consist of generic em-pathetic templates (e.g., " I’m sorry to hear that " or "I understand how you feel "). Repeated optimiza-tion on such high-frequency but low-information patterns can lead to hallucinated empathy , where models default to safe but vacuous responses in-stead of providing substantive support. To mitigate this issue, we introduce a Hindsight Dialogue Selection mechanism that identifies and prioritizes pivotal turns —interactions in which the agent’s response meaningfully affects the user’s emotional state or shifts the conversation’s trajec-tory. 3.4 Scalar–Verbal Hybrid Policy Optimization 

To bridge the gap between coarse scalar rewards and fine-grained verbal feedback, RAPO adopts a hybrid optimization strategy that combines scalar reinforcement learning with verbal supervision. Specifically, we use GRPO to optimize the policy for ranking-based scalar rewards, while on-policy self-distillation enables the model to internalize natural-language feedback generated by the GRM. 

3.4.1 Scalar RL 

We first apply GRPO to align the policy with the ranking rewards derived in Section 3.3. Given a group of sampled responses {ai}Gi=1 with con-trastive ranking rewards ri, the advantage ˆAi for each response by normalizing rewards within the group according to Eq. 1. The policy πθ optimized by maximizing the GRPO objective in Eq. 2, which encourages the model to assign higher probability to responses with better relative rankings. How-ever, scalar rewards provide only coarse guidance and cannot convey detailed linguistic or strategic corrections. 

3.4.2 Verbal RL 

To internalize the rich natural-language feed-back from the GRM, we introduce a verbal re-inforcement learning objective via on-policy self-distillation (Hübotter et al., 2026). The key idea is to use the policy itself as a feedback-aware teacher that corrects its own suboptimal trajectories. Rather than uniformly distilling all responses, we focus on the lowest-performing response in each group, which contains the most informative errors. By con-ditioning the policy on hindsight feedback describ-ing these errors, we construct a teacher distribution that guides the original policy toward improved behavior.

5 Related Work 

ESC. Existing work on ESC primarily relies on SFT to imitate empathic responses (Liu et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2023, 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). While such approaches improve fluency and coherence, they often produce generic expressions and exhibit limited personalization across diverse user contexts (Ye et al., 2025b). Recent RLHF-based methods (Rafailov et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2024) introduce preference optimization and structured rewards to enhance empathy modeling (Zhou et al., 2023; Jia et al., 2026; Ma et al., 2025; Ye et al., 2025a; Wang et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2025; Kim et al., 2025). However, most of them adopt static, expert-centric reward formulations, implicitly assuming that em-pathy quality can be predefined. In contrast, we model emotional support from a user-centric per-spective and optimize policies using dynamic user reactions and interaction-level feedback. 

Hindsight Learning and Verbal Feedback. 

Scalar rewards compress complex evaluation sig-nals into low-dimensional scores (Luo et al., 2025), which may be insufficient for abstract and person-alized objectives such as empathy (Zhang et al., 2025a). Recent research, therefore, explores natural-language feedback (Stephan et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2024; Hong et al., 2025) and iterative self-critique (Zhang et al., 2025b; Bensal et al., 2025; Ricciardi et al., 2025) as richer supervisory signals, as well as on-policy distillation for stable optimization (Hübotter et al., 2026; Zhao et al., 2026; Shenfeld et al., 2026). Building on these directions, we integrate verbal hindsight critiques with on-policy learning to enable fine-grained, user-driven adaptation beyond fixed scalar rewards. 

6 Conclusion 

We introduced RAPO, a framework that replaces static rubric-based supervision with dynamic feed-back driven by user reactions. Our experiments show that real-time user reactions serve as a more information-dense proxy for supportiveness than predefined expert rubrics, effectively facilitating positive interaction outcomes. Furthermore, ab-lation studies confirm that integrating scalar and verbal reinforcement learning provides a robust error-correction mechanism, enabling agents to in-fer the specific requirements for response refine-ment. By highlighting the importance of user-reaction aware feedback for adaptive social interac-tions, RAPO establishes a scalable, generalizable paradigm for building empathetic, goal-oriented AI systems aligned with complex human psychologi-cal states. 

Limitations 

While RAPO demonstrates significant improve-ments in both ESC and S OTOPIA benchmarks, sev-eral limitations remain that warrant further investi-gation: 

1. Computational Overhead. The reliance on an LLM-based user simulator to generate reaction-aware feedback introduces significant computa-tional overhead. Compared with methods that use expert-centric rewards, RAPO incurs longer train-ing time due to the iterative processes of reaction generation and conversation scoring. 

2. Dependency on Heavy Reward Models. Con-sistent with existing literature, our experimental setup utilizes advanced LLMs as reward judges across all baselines and ablation studies. While this ensures high-quality signal acquisition, it poses challenges for resource-constrained environments. A promising avenue for future work is to distill the knowledge from these large-scale judges into spe-cialized, smaller-parameter reward models, thereby improving training efficiency without sacrificing performance. 

3. Simulator Sensitivity. Given the intensive training costs, we did not perform an exhaustive ab-lation study on the impact of different user model architectures. In our experiments, we employed pre-trained simulators (ESC-Role for ESC and Qwen-3-8B for S OTOPIA ). The potential perfor-mance variations arising from different user model designs remain an open question, and we encourage future research to explore the robustness of RAPO across a wider range of simulator backbones. 

4. Domain Generalization. Our current valida-tion is confined to dialogue-based tasks. While RAPO is designed to handle reaction-driven pro-cesses, its generalizability to broader sequential decision-making domains remains to be verified. Future work will focus on scaling RAPO to diverse fields to assess its universal applicability.

A Related Work 

With the advancement of LLMs, personalized Emo-tional Support agents have attracted growing re-search interest. However, a key challenge persists: how to model effective reward signals that capture the subtle dynamics of empathy and user satisfac-tion. 

Emotional Support Conversation. Recent work on ESC has largely centered on SFT with expert-annotated demonstrations to enable imitation learn-ing (Liu et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2023, 2024; Ye et al., 2025c). Although these approaches sub-stantially enhance linguistic fluency and surface-level coherence, they frequently yield templated empathic responses and exhibit limited adaptabil-ity across heterogeneous user contexts, resulting in weak personalization. RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022; Schulman et al., 2017; Shao et al., 2024; Rafailov et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2025; Yue et al., 2025) provides a preference-driven alignment paradigm and has recently been extended to ESC. For ex-ample, Wang et al. (2025) design verifiable re-wards via emotion-consistent simulated users; Ma et al. (2025) introduces dual training signals to jointly model empathy hierarchy and semantic rel-evance; Jia et al. (2026) incorporates alternating self-reflection to strengthen empathic reasoning; and Ye et al. (2025a) mitigates reward uncertainty through group-wise comparative optimization. De-spite these methodological advances, existing ap-proaches predominantly adopt static, expert-centric reward formulations, implicitly presuming that em-pathy quality can be predefined and exhaustively specified. In contrast, we conceptualize emotional support from a user-centric perspective and opti-mize dialogue policies with dynamic user reactions and verbal hindsight critiques, enabling adaptive alignment grounded in interaction-level feedback rather than fixed supervisory signals. 

Hindsight Learning and Verbal Feedback. Con-ventional scalar reward models compress complex evaluation criteria into low-dimensional signals (Luo et al., 2025), which may be inadequate for abstract and personalized objectives such as em-pathy (Zhang et al., 2025a). To overcome this limitation, recent work explores natural language as a richer and more expressive supervisory chan-nel. One line of work explores learning directly from verbal feedback (Stephan et al., 2024). NLRL (Feng et al., 2024) reformulates RL objectives in language space. NLAC (Hong et al., 2025) fur-ther shows that generative critics that produce tex-tual evaluations provide more actionable signals in open-ended tasks. Another complementary ap-proach focuses on iterative refinement, where mod-els critique their own errors to evolve reasoning ca-pabilities (Zhang et al., 2025b; Bensal et al., 2025; Ricciardi et al., 2025). A third emerging paradigm is on-policy self-distillation. SDPO (Hübotter et al., 2026) and OPSD (Zhao et al., 2026) con-vert feedback-conditioned outputs into dense learn-ing signals without external teachers, and SDFT (Shenfeld et al., 2026) highlights the stability ben-efits of on-policy learning. Building on these in-sights, we integrate verbal hindsight critiques with on-policy distillation to enable fine-grained, user-centric adaptation beyond fixed scalar rewards.

C Discussion of User Reaction Mechanism 

In this work, we propose to leverage simulated user reactions as learning signals for Emotional Sup-port Conversation (ESC), moving away from the conventional rubric-as-reward paradigm. This shift frequently raises questions regarding the reliability of user simulation and the potential for noise in such signals. We provide our rationale and empiri-cal evidence for this design choice as follows: 

(1) From Static Rubrics to Dynamic Inter-action: Traditional LLM-as-a-judge approaches typically require a model to evaluate responses based on predefined, static expert rubrics. How-ever, these rubrics often fail to capture the nuanced, personalized, and context-dependent nature of con-versational tasks, especially for ESC. More im-portantly, requiring a model to strictly adhere to abstract expert definitions often leads to instabil-ity due to the model’s limited ability to interpret these criteria consistently, often resulting in score collapse or high sensitivity to prompt variations. In contrast, our approach treats dialogue as a reaction-driven process. The model does not need to pre-interpret an abstract rubric; instead, it generates responses that naturally elicit subsequent user reac-tions. This frames evaluation as a direct, empirical consequence of the interaction rather than a forced alignment with static norms. 

(2) Stability via Contrastive Evaluation: We do not assume that a single user reaction is perfect. Instead, we employ a groupwise contrastive eval-uation strategy, comparing reactions to multiple candidate responses within the same context. This approach, akin to generative critique reinforcement learning, provides fine-grained semantic guidance through verbal feedback, while the contrastive com-ponent counteracts noise from individual simulated reactions. Compared to pointwise scalar scoring, this method produces more stable and robust learn-ing signals, allowing the model to discern relative quality rather than rely on absolute judgments. 

(3) Self-Correction through Verbal Feedback: 

We emphasize that our framework integrates ver-bal feedback—the actual user response—as a pri-mary supervisory signal rather than a secondary score. This generative approach allows the policy to "understand" the causal relationship between a response and the user’s reaction. By distilling these feedback-informed, next-token predictions back into the policy, the model gains fine-grained guidance that a scalar value cannot convey. Empir-ical results in Table 1 and 2 and Figure 4 demon-strate that this combination of contrastive ranking and verbal feedback yields more stable training dynamics and superior performance compared to rubric-based methods, confirming that the "noise" in simulation is significantly outweighed by the rich, interactive information provided by the user model.

E Implementation Details 

E.1 Training Dataset 

Our model is trained on two complementary datasets targeting emotional support and social intelligence, respectively. These datasets are se-lected to expose the model to both psychologically grounded supportive dialogues and strategically complex social interactions. 

Emotional Support. For the emotional support task, we use the ESConv dataset (Liu et al., 2021), a manually curated corpus of supportive conversa-tions grounded in established psychological coun-seling strategies. The dialogues are constructed by trained annotators who simulate help-seeking and support-providing interactions. Compared to auto-matically collected or weakly supervised conversa-tional data, ESConv offers higher-quality, strategy-aware supervision signals. The dataset consists of 1,295 multi-turn conversations spanning diverse emotional distress scenarios. Following the stan-dard protocol, we adopt a 9:1 train–test split. While the FULL training set contains approximately 13 K

dialogue turns, our proposed HDS (Hindsight Di-alogue Selection) reduces this to a refined subset of 5,412 critical turns, focusing on high-impact strategic interactions. 

SOTOPIA . For the social intelligence task, we train on S OTOPIA -π (Wang et al., 2024), a large-scale synthetic dialogue dataset built upon the S O- 

> TOPIA

framework (Zhou et al., 2024). S OTOPIA -π

contains diverse, GPT-4-generated multi-turn so-cial interactions covering a wide range of interper-sonal settings and social objectives. The scenarios are carefully designed to be distinct from the orig-inal S OTOPIA benchmark tasks, preventing data leakage while introducing novel social challenges. The dataset emphasizes goal-driven interactions, strategic reasoning, and role-consistent behavior, providing rich supervision for modeling complex multi-agent social dynamics. 

E.2 Experiments Environment 

All training and inference tasks were executed on a cluster of 8 × NVIDIA A100 GPUs. We per-form SFT with the ms-swift framework (Zhao et al., 2025), and RL fine-tuning with the VeRL frame-work (Sheng et al., 2025), incorporating custom re-ward functions to align with our objectives. Model inference was powered by vLLM (Kwon et al., 2023). 

E.3 Hyperparameters E.3.1 Training Hyperparameters 

Due to resource constraints, we adopt parameter-efficient fine-tuning, implementing LoRA with a rank of 16 and a scaling factor α = 32 . All mod-els are trained for 3 epochs using a learning rate of 3 × 10 −6 and a batch size of 128 . The maxi-mum prompt length is set to 8,192 tokens and the maximum generation length to 2,048 tokens. In the training environment, the User Model is simulated using ESC-Role on ESC and Qwen-3-8B on S OTOPIA , while DeepSeek-R1 serves as the generative reward model that provides response-level quality assessment signals. For reinforcement learning optimization, we sample G = 4 rollouts per input prompt. Dur-ing the rollout phase, we use a temperature of 1.0

and top-p = 1 .0 to ensure unbiased stochastic ex-ploration. the KL regularization coefficient is set to β = 5 × 10 −4. Following Yu et al. (2025), we adopt Clip-Higher with asymmetric clipping thresholds ϵhigh = 0 .28 and ϵlow = 0 .2, and ap-ply Soft Overlong Punishment for length control. Specifically, when the response length exceeds a predefined maximum, a punishment interval is ac-tivated in which longer responses incur progres-sively larger penalties. The corresponding length control hyperparameters are set to Lmax = 200 

and Lcache = 80 .Within the RAPO framework, the SDPO mech-anism is specifically designed for verbal RL. The SDPO distillation term is integrated with a coeffi-cient of 1 × 10 −3, and its loss is bounded by an upper limit of 2 to stabilize training. We formulate the distillation objective using reverse KL diver-gence. To maintain a consistent and stable target distribution, we employ an Exponential Moving Average (EMA) teacher model with a mixing coef-ficient of 0.5. Furthermore, the distillation process is applied strictly to the top 256 tokens (distillation top-K = 256 ) of the teacher’s predicted distribu-tion. In terms of training efficiency, RAPO requires approximately 13 hours to train on the ESC task, compared to 9 hours for CPO, which does not in-volve user reaction simulation or self-distillation. In the S OTOPIA environment, RAPO training takes approximately 27 hours. 

E.3.2 Inference Hyperparameters 

For all evaluated models, we set the temperature to 

0.3 and the top-p value to 0.9 to balance generation diversity and stability. For reasoning-oriented mod-els, intermediate reasoning content enclosed within 

<think> and </think> tags is excluded from evaluation; we only consider the final response produced by the model, which corresponds to the explicit output shown to users. For all LLM-based judges and the ESC-Rank model, we adopt greedy decoding to ensure stable and reliable evaluation results. 

E.4 Human Evaluation 

We conducted a pairwise human evaluation to com-pare RAPO against three ablated variants (w/o URM, w/o SD, and CPO) across both the ESC-Eval and S OTOPIA benchmarks. From each bench-mark, we randomly sampled 50 simulated dialogue instances. For each instance, annotators were pre-sented with the dialogue context and two candidate responses (A and B) generated by different models. They were asked to choose among “A wins,” “B wins,” or “tie,” following the evaluation criteria de-tailed in Appendix D. To mitigate potential position and confirmation biases, we randomly shuffled the order of the responses and kept annotators blind to the model identities. Each instance was eval-uated by three independent annotators—Master’s students specialized in relevant fields. The final preference was determined via majority vote. Inter-annotator agreement was measured using Fleiss’ 

κ, yielding a score of 0.52, indicating moderate agreement. Instances without a majority consensus (i.e., three-way ties) were excluded from the final statistics.

Case Study from ESC-Eval: Qwen-2.5-RAPO 

Context. The user reports ongoing stress due to repeated conflicts with a roommate over household chores. 

Dialogue Trajectory. User: I’m feeling quite stressed out these days. I’ve been having constant conflicts with my roommate over household chores, and it’s really affecting our living situation. 

Assistant: [Empathy, Context Exploration] I can imagine that must be really tough. It sounds like there’s a lot of tension around household responsibilities between you and your roommate. That can definitely cause stress and frustration. Can you tell me more about what’s happening specifically? Maybe there are some details that could help us explore possible solutions together. 

User: [Continues elaborating on the issue.] We both agreed to take turns washing the dishes, but lately my roommate keeps forgetting and leaves them in the sink. It’s really annoying, and it feels like they’re not taking their fair share of responsibility. 

Assistant: [Empathy, Context Exploration] That does sound frustrating. It must feel like a small thing, but when it happens repeatedly, it can add up and feel pretty significant. Have you had any conversations with your roommate about how this is making you feel? Sometimes, expressing your feelings openly can help clear things up and find a solution that works for both of you. 

User: [Shares failed attempt at communication.] I tried talking to them about it, but they didn’t seem to understand the impact it has on our living situation. They always brush it off, saying they’ll do it next time. It’s like they don’t take it seriously. 

Assistant: [Collaborative Strategy] I can understand why that would feel frustrating and disappointing. It sounds like your roommate isn’t fully grasping how important it is to you. Have you considered setting up a more structured system, like using a calendar or a chore chart, to keep track of who is responsible for what? This might help make things clearer and more manageable for both of you. Additionally, maybe you could have another conversation, this time focusing on how you both can work together to solve the problem and make sure everyone feels heard and understood. 

Support Progression Learned by RAPO. Empathy → Context Exploration → Collaborative Strategy 

Analysis. The RAPO-trained model dynamically adapts its responses according to the user’s reactions throughout the conversation. It first validates the user’s emotional state, then explores contextual details of the conflict, and finally proposes a cooperative solution. This gradual support trajectory reflects effective emotional support strategies and demonstrates how RAPO encourages policies that respond to evolving user reactions.
