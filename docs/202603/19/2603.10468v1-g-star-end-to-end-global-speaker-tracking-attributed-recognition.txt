# Abstract 

We study timestamped speaker-attributed ASR for long-form, multi-party speech with overlap, where chunk-wise inference must preserve meeting-level speaker identity consistency while producing time-stamped, speaker-labeled transcripts. Previous Speech-LLM systems tend to prioritize either local diarization or global labeling, but often lack the ability to capture fine-grained temporal boundaries or robust cross-chunk identity linking. We propose G-STAR , an end-to-end system that couples a time-aware speaker-tracking module with a Speech-LLM transcrip-tion backbone. The tracker provides structured speaker cues with temporal grounding, and the LLM generates attributed text conditioned on these cues. G-STAR supports both component-wise optimization and joint end-to-end training, enabling flexi-ble learning under heterogeneous supervision and domain shift. Experiments analyze cue fusion, local versus long-context trade-offs and hierarchical objectives. 

Index Terms : speech recognition, diarization, human-computer interaction, computational paralinguistics 

# 1. Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have recently become cen-tral to speech understanding systems, largely owing to their strong semantic reasoning, discourse-level context modeling, and instruction-following capabilities[ 1, 2, 3, 4 ]. A prevail-ing paradigm, often referred to as Speech-LLM , combines a pre-trained speech encoder with an LLM through a lightweight projector that maps acoustic representations into the LLM em-bedding space[ 5, 6, 7]. This encoder–projector–LLM recipe has shown promising results across a broad range of speech tasks, especially those requiring higher-level linguistic and pragmatic understanding. However, real-world conversational audio commonly de-parts from the single-speaker assumption. In cocktail-party con-ditions and multi-party meetings, overlapping speech and rapid turn-taking demand not only accurate transcription [ 8, 9, 10 ], but also reliable attribution of who spoke what [11 ], and in-creasingly when [12 ]. This motivates speaker-attributed ASR 

(SA-ASR), which aims to produce a structured transcript con-taining both lexical content and speaker attribution[ 13 , 14 ]. In this work, we consider a timestamped SA-ASR formulation: given an input waveform x, the system outputs a sequence of attributed segments {(sn, τ st

Recent efforts begin to address parts of this problem, yet leave key gaps. SpeakerLM [ 16 ] augments the Speech LLM pipeline with an additional speaker embedding extractor, en-abling robust diarization within a local chunk; however, it does not provide an explicit mechanism to globally associate speaker identities across multiple chunks when long recordings must be processed segment-by-segment. JEDIS-LLM [ 17 ] introduces a Speaker Prompt Cache to store and index speaker represen-tations, thereby enabling the model to emit globally consistent

TagSpeech [ 18 ] further strengthens temporal grounding by in-corporating a dedicated speaker encoder and temporal anchors to obtain speaker and timestamp cues, but it similarly does not resolve the meeting-level global speaker identity linking prob-lem under chunked inference. Taken together, these advances highlight the need for an end-to-end framework that simultane-ously achieves (i) timestamped, overlap-aware attribution, and (ii) persistent, meeting-level global speaker identity consistency. Motivated by these gaps, we propose G-STAR: End-to-End Global Speaker-Tracking Attributed Recognition , an LLM-based end-to-end SA-ASR system designed explicitly for long-form, multi-speaker speech with overlapping regions. G-STAR integrates an interpretable speaker-tracking module based on Sortformer [ 19 , 20 ]-style modeling with a Speech-LLM tran-scription backbone. The speaker-tracking module provides struc-tured, time-aware speaker cues, while the LLM generates at-tributed text conditioned on these cues. This modular design enables both component-wise optimization (improving tracking and transcription separately) and joint end-to-end optimization (integrating speaker and lexical modeling in a unified objective), thereby supporting flexible training strategies under dataset im-balance and domain shifts. Most importantly, G-STAR is built to preserve global speaker identity consistency across chunk-wise inference, enabling meeting-level speaker labeling without post-hoc global clustering. Accordingly, our contributions are threefold: • We present G-STAR, an end-to-end, LLM-based SA-ASR system for multi-party meetings that produces times-tamped speaker-attributed transcripts with meeting-level global speaker identity consistency under chunk-wise stream-ing inference. We will release the model and code. • We achieve great both local and global SA-ASR performance on challenging meeting benchmarks, outperforming represen-tative Speech-LLM baselines (e.g., V IBE VOICE -ASR [ 21 ]) as well as strong conventional pipelines, demonstrating that

# 2. Related Work 

2.1. Sortformer and Streaming Sortformer for Speaker Tracking 

Sortformer [ 19 ] proposes a tracking-centric formulation that tightly couples speaker diarization with multi-speaker ASR by explicitly bridging timestamp-level speaker structure and token-level speaker attribution. Concretely, given an input waveform (or its acoustic features) segmented into frames, Sortformer maintains a slot-based speaker representation and enforces an 

arrival-time-ordered assignment of speakers, which ensures in-terpretable speaker indices and eliminates the permutation am-biguity inherent in multi-speaker modeling [ 19 ]. This design is particularly suitable for chunk-wise processing scenarios: when processing long recordings in segments, the model must pre-serve speaker identities across chunk boundaries rather than re-indexing speakers independently within each chunk. Streaming Sortformer [ 20 ] extends Sortformer to online inference by introducing an explicit memory mechanism, the 

Arrival-Order Speaker Cache (AOSC) , which stores speaker evidence and supports incremental updates as new chunks arrive. Formally, let Ct denote the cache state after processing chunk t.The streaming model updates Ct from Ct−1 using chunk-level observations, and uses the cache to condition diarization outputs for the current chunk such that the arrival-order speaker indices remain consistent over time [ 20 ]. This cache-based tracking perspective motivates our adoption of a Sortformer-style tracking module: we treat speaker identity as a compact and persistent state that can be propagated across chunks and provided to the downstream Speech-LLM generator, thereby ensuring global speaker consistency at the meeting level. 

2.2. Serialized Output Training (SOT) for Attributed Tran-scription 

Serialized Output Training (SOT) [ 22 ] addresses the label per-mutation issue in overlapped and multi-speaker transcription by converting multi-stream supervision signals into a single serial-ized target sequence. Instead of predicting K parallel speaker-dependent streams, SOT defines a deterministic serialization function that maps the set of speaker-labeled utterances (or token sequences) into a single sequence by inserting explicit speaker tags (e.g., <spk1> , <spk2> ) between lexical tokens [ 22 ]. This enables standard autoregressive or sequence-to-sequence train-ing while avoiding explicit permutation search at inference time. Crucially, SOT provides an output protocol for speaker-attributed text that is compatible with LLM-style generation: the model emits a single token stream that interleaves speaker iden-tifiers and words, and can be naturally extended with additional structural tokens (e.g., timestamps) when temporal grounding is required. However, under chunk-wise inference, SOT alone does not guarantee meeting-level identity consistency: speaker tags can still permute across chunks unless the model maintains a persistent speaker concept (e.g., via an explicit tracking state). Therefore, in our framework, SOT is adopted as the structured output interface, while global identity persistence is enforced by coupling the generator to a Sortformer-style streaming speaker tracker.

4.2. Modeling 

We have detailed the proposed architecture in Section 3.2. The audio encoder and projector are initialized from Firered-LLM[ 7]. The LLM uses Qwen2-7B-Instruct[ 28 ] with LoRA weights inher-ited from Firered-LLM. For the SD branch, the speaker diariza-tion module is initialized with SortFormer, while its projector consists of a stride-5 1D convolutional downsampling module followed by a two-layer MLP and is randomly initialized. 

4.3. Training Procedure 

We train the model in three stages: (i) meeting-style ASR pre-training, (ii) local speaker-attributed ASR (SA-ASR) training, and (iii) global (meeting-level) SA-ASR training. In both the ASR pre-training and local SA-ASR stages, we set the pack size to 12,000 tokens. The learning rate was linearly warmed up to 5e-5 and then decayed using cosine annealing. Each stage was trained for 20,000 steps. For the global SA-ASR stage, the pack size remained 12,000, while the peak learning rate was reduced to 2e-5 with the same linear warmup + cosine anneal-ing schedule, and training ran for 5,000 steps. During training, timestamps and speaker labels were treated as special tokens of the LLM. Hierarchical cross-entropy was adopted, assigning a 1.5 × loss weight to timestamp tokens and a 2 × loss weight to speaker label tokens. Before evaluation, we performed an addi-tional fine-tuning of the Sortformer for 5 epochs using default configurations to further enhance the system’s final performance. Notably, in the proposed framework, the fine-tuning of diariza-tion module can theoretically be conducted at any stage of the training process.

In this section, we evaluate G-STAR against baseline systems under the local setting, with a maximum utterance duration of 20 seconds. As shown in Table 1, G-STAR consistently outperforms the SD Sortformer baseline across all datasets, demonstrating that the LLM effectively leverages and integrates speaker diariza-tion outputs for speaker tracking. 5.3. Ablation Study 

We ablate the hierarchical cross-entropy (CE) loss and the in-terleave fusion scheme in the local setting (Table 3). Interleave fusion reduces both cpWER and DER, with a larger gain on cpWER, suggesting more reliable prediction of structure-critical tokens such as speaker and timestamp markers. In contrast, adding hierarchical CE consistently yields a clearer improve-ment on DER while leaving cpWER largely unchanged, indicat-ing stronger sensitivity to temporal boundaries and speaker turn segmentation. Together, the two components are complemen-tary: interleave fusion improves token-level attribution, whereas hierarchical CE mainly benefits diarization quality. 

# 6. Conclusion 

We presented G-STAR, an end-to-end framework for times-tamped speaker-attributed ASR on long-form multi-party au-dio under chunk-wise inference. By coupling a streaming Sortformer-style speaker tracker (with a persistent speaker cache) and a Speech-LLM transcription backbone, the model produces globally consistent speaker IDs while retaining fine-grained tem-poral grounding. Our evaluations and ablations show that the proposed cue design and training objectives improve speaker-aware transcription, and expose a practical trade-off between attribution accuracy and diarization purity under fully stream-ing, online speaker handling. Overall, G-STAR provides a new paradigm and a strong, reproducible baseline for Speech-LLM-based SA-ASR in realistic meeting scenarios. 7. References
