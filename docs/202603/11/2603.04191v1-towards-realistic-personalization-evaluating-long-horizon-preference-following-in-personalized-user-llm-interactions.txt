## Abstract 

Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly serving as personal assistants, where users share complex and diverse preferences over extended interactions. However, assessing how well LLMs can follow these preferences in realistic, long-term situations remains underexplored. This work proposes RealPref , a benchmark for evaluating re-alistic preference-following in personalized user-LLM interactions. RealPref features 100 user pro-files, 1300 personalized preferences, four types of preference expression (ranging from explicit to implicit), and long-horizon interaction histories. It includes three types of test questions (multiple-choice, true-or-false, and open-ended), with de-tailed rubrics for LLM-as-a-judge evaluation. Re-sults indicate that LLM performance significantly drops as context length grows and preference ex-pression becomes more implicit, and that gener-alizing user preference understanding to unseen scenarios poses further challenges. RealPref and these findings provide a foundation for future re-search to develop user-aware LLM assistants that better adapt to individual needs. The code is avail-able at github.com/GG14127/RealPref.

## 2. Related Work 

Instruction Following Benchmarks. Existing bench-marks for instruction following primarily fall into two cat-egories (Lou et al., 2024): Task-centric evaluation as-sesses problem-solving on predefined tasks using metrics like ROUGE (Lin, 2004) (e.g., MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020), IFEval (Zhou et al., 2023)); and Human-centric evaluation focuses on alignment with general human prefer-ences through open-ended tasks (e.g., AlpacaFarm (Dubois et al., 2023)), relying on human or LLM judges. While these works focus on adherence to standalone prompts, our work benchmarks “preference following”—a nuanced exten-sion where preference instructions are implicit and scattered across long-term interactions. 

Long-Context Understanding Benchmarks. Evaluations in this domain have evolved from synthetic retrieval tasks to realistic dialogue settings. Early benchmarks focused on direct information retrieval, exemplified by “Needle in a Haystack” tests (Nelson et al., 2024) and reading compre-hension tasks like LongBench (Bai et al., 2024). Recent efforts have shifted towards multi-turn interactions, with benchmarks such as LoCoMo (Maharana et al., 2024) and Longmemeval (Wu et al., 2024) supporting sessions exceed-ing 100k tokens. However, these benchmarks primarily prioritize factual information retrieval. Our work targets a capability beyond simple recall: it requires models to reason over implicit cues scattered across history and generalize this understanding to make personalized decisions in unseen scenarios. 

LLM Personalization Benchmarks. Benchmarks in this domain typically assess (1) Persona Adoption : role-playing (Wang et al., 2024) or mimicking styles (Salemi et al., 2023; Kumar et al., 2024); (2) User Adaptation : customizing outputs for question-answering (Zhao et al., 2025; Jiang et al., 2025) or downstream tasks like recommendation sys-tems (Lyu et al., 2024). Our work targets the latter for direct question-answering. Existing benchmarks, however, lack depth: P REF EVAL (Zhao et al., 2025) have dialogues centered on standalone preferences and coarse binary scor-ing criteria for open-ended answers, while P ERSONA MEM 

(Jiang et al., 2025) overlooks implicit preference expressions and relies on logit-based metrics inapplicable to API-based models. In contrast, RealPref provides a user-centered dia-logue dataset with preference expressions ranging from ex-plicit to implicit, supported by a granular, multi-dimensional rubric for evaluating open-ended responses.

Generalized Preferences. The term “Generalized Prefer-ences" refers to preferences that are derived from Original Preferences. People often have linked preferences across topics, driven by similar reasons. For example, someone who prefers cruelty-free, eco-friendly beauty products may also favor natural, eco-friendly cleaning supplies, reflecting a holistic lifestyle. When users state a preference with rea-sons, they often expect the LLM assistant to recognize their general tendency across topics. This scenario resembles the way recommendation systems suggest new products based on past preferences. Therefore, we designed generalized preferences that differ from the original preferences but can be rationally deduced from them with similar reasons. Only original preferences will have expression conversations in the context, while the generalized preferences will be used directly for tests. For a generalized preference example, please refer to Appendix A.2. 

3.3. Conversations 

Based on the curated user profiles, biographies, and prefer-ences, conversations are generated to simulate real-world user-LLM interactions. Here, conversations are primarily classified into three categories based on the type of user information indicated in the conversation. 

Life-Event Conversation. This category indicates user profiles and life events. Users’ life event histories com-prise distinct life stages, and users require different forms of assistance from LLMs at different stages. While unre-lated to preference testing, it helps simulate user-centered interaction data. Through dialogue, the user gradually re-veals background, circumstances, thoughts, and emotions. Typical conversation contents include task-based requests, life-choice discussions, and emotional companionship. (See conversation example in Appendix A.3.1) 

Random Conversation. This category will not reveal any user-specific information. Typically, the user begins the session with a detailed, practical question, such as factual questions, explanations, troubleshooting, tool use, small talk, or simple text generation. Given their user-free nature, random conversations are suitable for sharing among users and for managing context length for experiments. (See conversation example in Appendix A.3.2) 

Preference-Expression Conversation. This category indi-cates user preferences and serves as a key part of our work. In reality, users may express their preferences gradually, implicitly through choices, stories, or emotional cues, rather than explicit statements. Therefore, we provide four Prefer-ence Expression Types for each preference, from explicit to implicit. These four types require varying levels of capabil-ity, ranging from basic information extraction to complex comprehension and reasoning. Descriptions of each type are provided below. (See conversation examples of each type in Appendix A.3.3): 1. Explicit - Direct Statement. Preferences are stated di-rectly and explicitly in a single turn without redundant words. 2. Explicit - Contextualized Mention. In multi-round conversations, users start the dialogue with other re-lated daily topics as context instead of directly stating preferences. As the dialogue advances, preferences are naturally mentioned explicitly in the context. 3. Implicit - Stylistic Expression. The context resem-bles the second type, but users will not explicitly state preferences with words like Like, Prefer, or Dislike. In-stead, they imply preferences through stylistic devices such as rhetorical questions, metaphors, contrasts, or emotional expression. 4. Implicit - Experience Feedback. This type involves multiple sessions over time, based on the user experi-ence of the preference Initialize Event. The user won’t explicitly state their preference in one session but will reveal it gradually through experience sharing or feed-back across sessions. Preferences can only be inferred by integrating information from all sessions. For example, the early session may occur before the event, when the user is willing to try, as they haven’t formed a preference. The later sessions may occur after the event, during which users may share their experiences, give feedback, and indicate preferences. 

3.4. Contexts 

Based on the three types of conversation sessions gener-ated, we can build a complete interaction history context for each user. The context includes all user-specific ses-sions (life-event and preference-expression conversations) in chronological order, with random conversations inserted to control length. Two insertion methods are used: one randomly inserts into existing history to dilute personal in-formation, and the other appends it at the end, distancing preference cues from the query. ## 4. RealPref Evaluation 

4.1. Task Types 

Based on the generated preferences, we provide classifica-tion (multiple-choice, true-or-false) and generation (open-ended) tasks. In classification tasks, LLMs can make deci-sions by matching options to past conversations. In open-ended tasks, LLMs must proactively generate responses aligned with preferences, which challenges their memory and reasoning more than passively selecting options. Apreference query example is provided in Appendix B.1. 

Open-Ended Questions. High-quality queries should dis-tinguish between models that adhere to user preferences and those that don’t: answering without knowing preferences may violate them. Thus, the query content should not dis-close user preferences or intentions, ensuring that LLMs cannot rely solely on common-sense reasoning to provide the correct answer. 

Multiple-Choice Questions (MCQs). The queries in the MCQ setting are identical to the open-ended ones. Four options are generated, all relevant and reasonable answers to the query without logical errors, but only one best aligns with the preference. However, we noticed flaws in this setting: LLMs, given a question with four options and told only one is correct, might choose an option that stands out from the others, and answer correctly by comparing options even without knowledge of the user’s preferences. 

True-or-False Questions. Based on the above analysis, we designed this new classification task. True-or-False ques-tion combines the open-ended query and one MCQ option, asking the LLM to determine if it is suitable. In this setting, LLMs cannot compare options; they must decide based on a single option. We construct a correct one and an incorrect one for each query, forming a balanced dataset. 

4.2. Evaluation Methods 

While classification tasks adopt accuracy as the metric, gen-eration tasks require a comprehensive set of evaluation cri-teria to assess response quality. Based on prior work (Zhao et al., 2025) and an analysis of actual responses, this work provides a more detailed scoring criterion on a 1-5 scale from 3 rating dimensions: Preference Awareness , Prefer-ence Alignment , and Answer Quality . For detailed evalua-tion rubrics, please refer to Appendix B.2. 

Preference Awareness. The first dimension focuses on the extent to which the answers recognize or understand user preferences. The judgment is based on the following: If an LLM is aware of user preferences when responding, it should mention the preferences it uses in the answer. 

Preference Alignment. The second dimension focuses on how well the answer’s content aligns with the user’s prefer-ences, by checking if the answer’s actual content matches or contradicts user preferences, with criteria discussed by preference types in Table 1. 

Answer Quality. The last dimension focuses on the ex-tent to which the answer’s content is actually helpful and constructive to the user. Although not directly related to preferences, it is an essential dimension for evaluating the quality of open-ended responses.

## 6. Conclusion & Future Directions 

In this work, we proposed RealPref , a comprehensive bench-mark for evaluating LLMs’ ability to follow preferences in long-horizon, personalized user-LLM interactions. RealPref 

features a long-horizon conversation dataset constructed by a user-centric generation pipeline. Our design considers diverse preference topics and types, complex preference expressions, preference generalization scenarios, and differ-ent evaluation tasks. Experimental results show that many LLMs lack proactivity in recalling and following user pref-erences, and even capable models exhibit significant perfor-mance degradation as context length increases and prefer-ence expressions become more implicit. Besides, generaliz-ing learned preferences to unseen scenarios poses additional challenges. RealPref and these findings may provide a foun-dation for future research to develop smarter, user-aware LLM assistants tailored to individual needs. While this work proposes an innovative benchmark, there are limitations to be considered for future directions. • Complex Preference : In the real world, user prefer-ences can change over time or depend on the condition of something else. Enriching the dataset with such complex preferences would be a possible direction. • Richer Interaction Context : Besides simplified dia-logue types, real-world interactions may involve richer, multimodal contexts. Exploring how to enrich interac-tion contexts is also a promising future direction. • Complex User Feedback : Besides the current pref-erence expression types designed, other forms of in-dication, such as thumbs-up or thumbs-down to LLM responses, could be considered for possible future di-rection. • Evaluation Extension : The evaluation rubrics for open-ended responses could be expanded to include additional dimensions, such as fairness, privacy, and transparency, better aligning the benchmark with re-sponsible AI principles. 8Evaluating Long-Horizon Preference Following in Personalized User-LLM Interactions 

## Impact Statement 

This work introduces RealPref , a benchmark for evaluating realistic preference-following in long-horizon user–LLM interactions. By enabling more realistic assessment of how LLMs infer and apply user preferences over extended con-texts, this benchmark may contribute to the development of LLM assistants that are more user-aware, adaptive, and aligned with individual needs in real-world scenarios. At the same time, efforts toward personalization may raise potential risks related to user privacy or over-personalization. Our work highlights the importance of responsible, trans-parent, and interpretable practices, particularly with respect to data privacy and data quality. RealPref is designed as an evaluation benchmark, and all user profiles, preferences, and interaction histories are synthetically generated, without involving real personal data. We manually reviewed and excluded ineligible data samples throughout the generation pipeline, ensuring that the generated content contains no unethical components or privacy-sensitive information. Re-garding unavoidable user privacy concerns associated with future practical deployment of LLMs with strong preference-following abilities, we advocate for responsible, transparent model deployment that prioritizes user data privacy and security, allowing users to control their data. Regarding potential over-personalization, our personalized tasks rely solely on preference information that users actively share with the model in the dialogue and do not use detailed user data in ways that make users feel monitored. We strive to include diverse preference data in our datasets to minimize the chances of producing unfair or biased personalization data. Finally, the dataset and detailed documentation of our generation methodology, including code and prompts, will be publicly available to support reproducibility.

## D. Limitations 

Regarding the dataset, all user profiles, preferences, and dialogue histories in our work are synthetically generated. Although this approach enables controlled evaluation and protects privacy, synthetic users may not fully capture the complexity and unpredictability of human behavior. Besides, the topic pool, preference space, and preference expressions, while diverse, remain finite and may not cover all domains or cultural contexts encountered in real-world applications. Nevertheless, we make deliberate efforts through diverse topic design and varied preference expressions to make the generated data as closely approximate real-world user behavior as possible. For evaluation, our work relies on LLM-as-a-judge with predefined rubrics. While automatic judging may not perfectly align with human judgments, we carefully design detailed, structured evaluation criteria to enhance consistency and reliability. Moreover, compared with large-scale human evaluation, this approach offers a significantly more cost-efficient and scalable alternative, making large-scale systematic evaluation feasible. Regarding implicit preference expressions (e.g., experience sharing), inferring user preferences from such signals and using them to guide responses may raise concerns about over-personalization. In our work, although preferences are expressed implicitly, the data generation process is carefully designed to ensure that preference inference is grounded in clear, interpretable cues within the dialogue, rather than in speculative or unsupported assumptions. 

## E. Detailed Related Work 

E.1. LLM Personalization Tasks 

Most existing works on personalization of LLMs can be summarized into the following categories: (1) Persona Adoption :personalize the LLMs themselves to speak like given personas, with typical tasks such as mimicking user speaking or role-playing. (2) User Adaptation : adapt LLMs to generate responses aligned with individual preferences, for direct question-answering or downstream tasks like recommendation systems. In persona adoption for mimic user speaking , LLM assistants can be used as personal writing agents to write posts, emails, 19 Evaluating Long-Horizon Preference Following in Personalized User-LLM Interactions 

news, etc. LaMP benchmark (Salemi et al., 2023) provides a comprehensive evaluation framework for personalized text classification and generation in short, single-turn tasks, such as email subject generation, news headline generation, paper title generation, product rating, and movie tagging. LongLaMP benchmark (Kumar et al., 2024) proposed to evaluate personalized LLM techniques for longer-form personalized text generation across a wide variety of long-text generation tasks, such as email completion and paper abstract generation. Persona adoption for role-playing is another task scenario to test personalized LLMs. RoleLLM (Wang et al., 2024) introduces the first systematic and fine-grained character-level benchmark dataset for role-playing. In user adaptation for downstream tasks , LLMs are to generate intermediate outputs to enhance performance on personal-ization tasks such as recommendation systems. LLM-Rec (Lyu et al., 2024) employs distinct text-enrichment prompting strategies to augment the original item descriptions and concatenates them as input to the recommendation module, introducing relevant context and better aligning with user preferences. In user adaptation for direct question-answering , LLMs serve as helpful assistants, providing tailored responses to user questions. P REF EVAL (Zhao et al., 2025) is the first to provide a benchmark to evaluate LLMs’ preference-following abilities. Its dialogue dataset is generated centered on each standalone preference, rather than on user profiles to simulate complex real-world interactions. P ERSONA MEM (Jiang et al., 2025) proposes a new data generation pipeline that constructs the dialogue dataset step by step based on dynamic user profiles, covering a variety of query tasks to test whether LLMs can retain user traits, track dynamic user preferences, and generate personalized responses in new scenarios. However, its evaluation setting for generation tasks requires access to the output logits of the pre-designed answers, which are not available for proprietary LLMs, and using logits is not reliable enough for evaluating open-ended answers. Our work falls into this category. RealPref draws inspiration from P ERSONA MEM ’s generation pipeline, and takes into account the complexity of human preference expression, introducing a more comprehensive and granular evaluation method for generation tasks. 

E.2. Evaluation of LLM Personalization 

The evaluation of personalization tasks can be divided into two categories: classification tasks and generation tasks. 

Classification tasks are typically designed to evaluate performance on downstream tasks with clear ground-truth labels. For example, personalized LLMs can classify input text (such as profiles of users or items) into specific categories (Salemi et al., 2023), or choose the option that is best aligned with the context (Zhao et al., 2025). In this setting, typical classification metrics such as Accuracy, Recall, Precision, and F1 Score can be used to evaluate the performance (Zhang et al., 2024). 

Generation tasks require different evaluation methods depending on whether ground truth textual data is available. Datasets containing actual user-written ground-truth text are relatively rare, but they allow direct evaluation of generation quality. In LaMP (Salemi et al., 2023) and LongLaMP (Kumar et al., 2024), metrics like BLUE (Papineni et al., 2002), ROUGH (Lin, 2004), METEOR (Banerjee & Lavie, 2005) are used to evaluate models on personalized text generation tasks. For generation tasks that have no ground-truth text, human labor or LLMs serve as evaluators. Recent works, such as P REF EVAL (Zhao et al., 2025), have adopted an LLM-as-a-judge approach with criteria to detect defects in answers. 

E.3. Personalization Enhancing Techniques 

In general, techniques for enhancing personalization of LLMs include the following (Zhang et al., 2024): Prompt Engineering, Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), Representation Learning, and Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF). 

Prompt Engineering (Sahoo et al., 2024) is the process of designing, refining, and optimizing prompts to achieve desired outputs from language models (Zhang et al., 2024). By incorporating user information into the prompts or providing few-shot, high-quality examples for specific tasks, LLMs can leverage contextual information and generalize to perform personalization tasks without further training. 

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (Gao et al., 2023) enhances LLM performance by retrieving relevant document seg-ments from an external knowledge base using semantic similarity calculations (Zhang et al., 2024). Retrieving relevant personalization information needed for the task enables LLMs to generate customized, tailored outputs. 

Representation learning for user modeling (Li & Zhao, 2021; Tan & Jiang, 2023) aims to enhance performance by learning latent representations that accurately capture each user’s behavior. This method requires a training stage, such as fine-tuning 20 Evaluating Long-Horizon Preference Following in Personalized User-LLM Interactions 

and embedding learning, which allows LLMs to adapt to specific data formats and generate responses in a particular style as instructed (Zhang et al., 2024). This approach typically yields better results on most tasks when model parameters are available and the associated costs are acceptable. 

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022) is to learn a policy of output based on preferential human feedback. Besides general preference human feedback, there are works about Reinforcement Learning from Personalized Human Feedback (RLPHF), such as MORL tasks (Jang et al., 2023) and P-RLHF framework (Li et al., 2024).
