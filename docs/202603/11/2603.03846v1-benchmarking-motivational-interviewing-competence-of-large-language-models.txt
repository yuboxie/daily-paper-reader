# Abstract 

Introduction: Motivational interviewing (MI) promotes behavioural change in substance use 

disorders. Its fidelity is measured using the Motivational Interviewing Treatment Integrity (MITI) 

framework. While large language models (LLMs) can potentially generate MI-consistent therapist responses, their competence using MITI is not well-researched, especially in real-world clinical transcripts. We aim to benchmark MI competence of proprietary and open-source models compared to human therapists in real-world transcripts and assess distinguishability from human therapists. 

Methods: We shortlisted 3 proprietary and 7 open-source LLMs from LMArena, evaluated 

performance using MITI 4.2 framework on two datasets (96 handcrafted model transcripts, 34 

real-world clinical transcripts). We generated parallel LLM-therapist utterances iteratively for each transcript while keeping client responses static, and ranked performance using a composite ranking system with MITI components and verbosity. We conducted a distinguishability experiment with two independent psychiatrists to identify human-vs-LLM responses. 

Results: All 10 tested LLMs had fair (MITI global scores >3.5) to good (MITI global scores >4) competence across MITI measures, and three best-performing models (gemma-3-27b -it, gemini-2.5 -pro, grok-3) were tested on real-world transcripts. All showed good competence, with LLMs outperforming human-expert in Complex Reflection percentage (39% vs 96%) and Reflection -Question ratio (1.2 vs >2.8). In the distinguishability experiment, psychiatrists identified LLM responses with only 56% accuracy, with d -prime: 0.17 and 0.25 for gemini -2.5 -pro and gemma-3-27b -it respectively. 

Conclusion: LLMs can achieve good MI proficiency in real -world clinical transcripts using MITI 

framework. These findings suggest that even open -source LLMs are viable candidates for expanding MI counselling sessions in low-resource settings, warranting further clinical validation. 

Keywords: Motivational interviewing, Substance-related disorders, Large language models, 

Benchmarking, Artificial intelligence 3

# 1 Background 

Motivational Interviewing (MI) is a collaborative, client-centred counselling technique to promote behavioural change ( Miller & Rollnick, 2013 ). Briefly, in MI, the therapist tries to evoke 

clients’ reasons for change using reflections and open-ended questions. The focus is on 

affirming client strengths, emphasising their autonomy, and seeking collaboration, while avoiding behaviours that can provoke defensiveness, such as confrontation and persuasion. Since its origin in the 1980s, MI has been shown to be better than no intervention or feedback for alcohol, tobacco and cannabis use ( Schwenker et al., 2023 ). However, there have been two 

challenges in implementing and studying MI at scale. First, it is a difficult technique to imbibe and requires ongoing supervision to maintain fidelity (Hall et al., 2016 ). Rollnick et al. have 

indicated that even with training and supervision, some practitioners may not be able to practice the spirit of MI ( Rollnick & Gobat, 2016 ). Second, most effectiveness studies have not 

measured treatment fidelity , and thus, we do not know what actually worked ( Schwenker et al., 

2023 ). In this context, we discuss attempts to develop computational solutions that can deliver 

MI. 

We restrict our discussion to generative Artificial Intelligence (AI) related work , specifically Large 

Language Models (LLMs). LLMs are deep learning models which are designed to understand and generate human-like text. These models are available in two varieties: pretrained language models, which have only been trained to predict the next word in a sequence, and instruction-tuned models, which have received additional training to follow natural language instructions (Zhao et al., 2023 ). Karve et al. have reviewed the extant research on LLMs for MI ( Karve et al., 

2025 ), and the early signals are encouraging. For example, all fifteen reviewed studies reported 

good feasibility and acceptability. We must consider the presumptions made in these studies and the limitations of their study designs. 

First, most studies have tested the deployment of a chosen LLM for conducting online MI for 

various indications and measured user satisfaction or effectiveness. This design presumes substantial MI competence in LLMs. Only 6 out of 15 studies used any fidelity check (Karve et 

al., 2025 ). Even when used, ‘expert-rating’ is chosen over Motivational Interviewing Treatment 

Integrity (MITI) coding ( Moyers et al., 2016 ). MITI scoring is more informative than a global ‘expert 

rating’ as it provides technical and relational competence. It also has a standardised threshold for MI competence. A subset of studies has measured the ability of a chosen LLM to produce a 

specific component of MI , like reflections or open -ended questions. Here again, it is important to realise that MI is a gestalt of many techniques and needs holistic assessment. 

Second, none of the studies have systematically compared the competence of LLMs with that of addiction psychiatry experts. Therefore, we do not know if LLMs are as good as the current standard of care for a given target behaviour. This comparison should ideally be done in two settings. One is an artificial setting in which the expert and LLMs generate therapist utterances for a given MI session. This allows sufficient time for the expert to generate the best response, unlike in real-life situations, where a certain cadence of dialogue must be maintained. Another is a realistic setting, in which LLMs generate therapist utterances for a recorded and transcribed clinical MI session. These two settings answer distinct questions: how good are LLMs as compared to the usual performance of addiction specialists, and how good are LLMs as compared to the best performance of addiction specialists? 4

Finally, there are no studies which have examined the distinguishability of LLM-generated therapist utterances from those of addiction specialists. Distinguishability is a proxy for the human-like quality of interactions and is therefore important. 

With this background, we aim to: (i) Measure MI competence of proprietary and open-source LLMs using MITI-4.2 . (ii) Compare the performance of the top open-source and proprietary LLMs with that of an addiction specialist. (iii) Assess distinguishability between human-delivered and AI-generated MI responses using two independent addiction psychiatrists.

## 2.2 LLM-therapist response generation 

Models were to be evaluated on their ability to generate MI-consistent responses to client utterances. The model's task, therefore, was to generate one therapist utterance conditioned on the previous conversation turns. At each turn, the model only sees client and therapist utterances made by the investigators, and never its own previous utterances. The rationale for this design choice is to ensure that the text presented to it remains coherent. The algorithm is shown in Figure 2 (see code in Supplementary Box S1). 

We iteratively built a detailed prompt to optimise MI adherence by testing increasingly detailed prompts. The final prompt is shared in Supplementary Box S2 . All responses were obtained using the respective model’s default generation configuration via API calls to Open Router. 6

## 2.3 Dataset Construction 

We collected three types of datasets for this work 

Model Interview Transcripts: A set of 100 transcripts using MI training educational videos (N=55) ( psychotherapy.net, 2026 ; The Change Companies, 2023 ) and recollection of client 

interactions (N=45). These transcripts were handcrafted to provide sufficient opportunities for 

practising MI. Three independent addiction psychiatrists reviewed these and excluded four transcripts based on the feedback. Finally, an addiction psychiatrist (AJ) rewrote the therapist utterances to the best of her ability to maximise MI adherence. 

For each instance of these 96 transcripts, we have 10 copies, one for each model under study. 

The final size of this dataset is thus 96 * 11 = 1056. 

Real-world clinical interview transcripts: Adult patients seeking help for substance use were recruited with written informed consent (N = 30). MI sessions conducted by an addiction 7

psychiatrist (N=34) were audio-recorded, and recordings were transcribed (translated if not in English) and anonymised. 

We selected three LLMs (one open-source, two proprietary) that had achieved the best performance on model transcripts for this comparison. As a result, we have three copies for each instance of these 34 transcripts. The final size of this dataset is thus 34 * 4 = 136. 

MI non-adherent transcripts: We sampled 15 model transcripts and prompted Gemini-2.5 -pro to play the role of a directive-informational therapist. This was done to obtain some representation of MI non-adherent behaviours, such as confrontation, for the interrater agreement exercise. The prompt is given in Supplementary Box S3. 

## 2.4 Annotation setup 

We used the MITI 4.2 .1 coding manual for all annotations ( Moyers et al., 2014 ). The MITI 4.2 

metrics consist of Technical Global Scores, Relational Global Scores, Complex Reflection (CR) percentage, Reflection: Question (RQ) ratio, and Behavioural Counts, including MI -adherent, MI-non-adherent, and MI-neutral behaviours (Supplementary Figure S1). 

An addiction psychiatrist (AJ) and a clinical psychologist learnt MITI coding using the manual and twenty example transcripts ( https://casaa.unm.edu/tools/miti.html ). Following this, for 

three rounds, they coded 10 randomly selected transcripts, and disagreements were resolved by discussion. Finally, a dataset of 100 transcripts was constructed by combining 15 MI non-

adherent transcripts and 85 randomly selected transcripts from the pool of 1026 model 

transcripts that the annotators had not yet seen. We used the intraclass correlation coefficient 

(two-way random effects) to assess interrater agreement .

The therapist's identity was concealed from the annotators. 

## 2.5 Measuring and comparing MI competence of LLMs 

We report MI competence of LLMs using the following MITI metrics: Relational global scores (Fair=3.5, Good=4), Technical global scores (Fair=3, Good=4), CR percentage ( Fair=40%, Good=50%), and RQ ratio (Fair=1, Good=2) .

For comparison, we use a ranking procedure. Each transcript (96 model transcripts and 34 real-world transcripts) is considered a test taken by 11 therapist agents (10 LLMs, 1 human) in the case of model transcripts or 4 therapist agents (3 LLMs, 1 human) in the case of real-world transcripts. They get a composite score based on the above four MITI metrics as positively coded variables and the median number of words per therapist utterance as a negatively coded variable to penalise verbosity (Supplementary Box S4). We then rank the performance of all agents for a given transcript. Finally, we compare the rank distributions of all agents to identify those that consistently outperform others. 

## 2.6 Distinguishability experiment 

In addition to comparing model performance solely on MITI metrics, we also examined whether LLM-therapist responses were distinguishable from human-therapist responses. For this, we used the copies of 96 model transcripts – one where therapist responses are written by the addiction psychiatrist and one each where therapist responses are written by the best performing proprietary and open source LLM (total: 96 * 3 = 2 88). 8

These transcripts were presented to two independent addiction psychiatrists in random order, with the task of guessing whether therapist utterances were human or LLM. Raters were blind to each other’s decisions. They were informed that they could expect that two-thirds of instances would involve an LLM playing the role of a therapist. Thus, the baseline expectancy of AI is 67%. 

We have analysed the resulting (288 * 2 = 576) decisions using a mixed -effects , logistic regression model with random effects for instance (288) and fixed effects for the identity of 

therapist agent (3; human, LLM1, LLM2) and for the rater (2) . Further, we use the signal detection theory and compute d-prime, which is a measure of signal strength (distinctiveness of LLM responses in this case) relative to the noise ( Macmillan & Creelman, 2005 ).

# 4 Discussion 

We set out to measure the MI competence of LLMs using methods recommended for human therapists, compare their competence with addiction specialists and assess the distinguishability of LLMs playing the role of MI therapists. Before discussing our findings, we 

wish to discuss some epistemic issues inherent to such inquiries. 

First, we presume (with good evidence) that treatment fidelity is related to treatment efficacy 

and thus measuring it is the first step. For example, therapists’ MI adherent behaviours are causally linked to clients’ change talk, which is in turn a predictor of clinical improvement (Magill et al., 2014 ). We also aver that acceptability ratings from users, while important, are not 

necessarily linked to treatment fidelity or clinical improvement. 

Second, the exact methodology by which LLMs are made to take on the role of a therapist is an important design choice. A therapeutic dialogue requires two sentient agents and a mode of communication. One option is to use an LLM to simulate clients; however, earlier studies have shown that LLMs tend to be overly compliant and unrealistically agreeable ( Kiuchi et al., 2025 ;

Steenstra et al., 2024 ). Another choice is to use volunteers who interact in a chatbot interface. 

For example, the MIBot dataset ( Mahmood et al., 2025 ) has 106 conversations between MIBot 

and human smokers. In this study, we use an offline method to generate LLM responses, in which the client utterances are frozen. With these design issues in mind, we discuss the main 

findings of our study. 

We found that most LLMs exhibit “fair” or “good” competence in conducting MI in both model and real-world scenarios. Coming to specific MITI scores, the current study shows better 

performance (scoring >4 on all MITI global scores, indicating “good” competence), even in real-world transcripts, compared to previous work: Kiuchi et al.’s audit study evaluated model-generated dialogue (GPT-4-Turbo and Claude-3-Opus as counsellor models, Claude-3-Opus as the client) – the counsellor models scored between 2 -3.5 (rated by human-experts on a Likert scale of 1-5) on the MITI global scores ( Kiuchi et al., 2025 ). Oster and team evaluated GPT-4o’s 

ability to hold 45-minute MI consistent conversations with simulated patients (human actors) 

and found lower global scores (Technical global = 2.5; Relational global = 2.7) (Oster et al., 

2025 ). It is possible that the methodological differences in generating LLM -therapist responses, 

dialogue generation in Japanese ( Kiuchi et al., 2025 ), and a simple rather than structured 

prompt ( Oster et al., 2025 ), may have led to this difference in performance. 

Hu et al. employed a similar methodology to ours, rewriting therapist dialogue to make it MI-consistent while keeping client dialogue static, and coding the responses of three Chinese models using the MITI 4.2. While MITI global scores were similar to our work (~4), they found less frequent complex reflections (24 -31% vs 96 -99%) and a lower RQ ratio of 1.1 to 1.3 (Hu et 

al., 2025 ), compared to our finding of 2.8 to 8.7 across three models. 14 

Among the four performance measures, LLMs struggle most with the RQ ratio. Earlier studies have similarly found that LLMs use questions to keep the conversation going instead of 

reflections (Yang et al., 2025 ). We also see an unnaturally high percentage of complex 

reflections , which has been noted as an undesirable feature in earlier studies ( Steenstra et al., 

2024 ). But most importantly, LLMs other than frontier offerings like grok -3 and gemini-2.5 -pro 

are quite parsimonious in statements that are affirming, emphasise autonomy, or seek 

collaboration. These MI-adherent utterances are the spirit of MI and most strongly related to eliciting change talk ( Magill et al., 2014 ). 

In this evaluation, LLMs give tough competition to MI-trained addiction psychiatrists. Frontier LLMs do as well as an addiction psychiatrist who was given unlimited time to come up with the most appropriate therapist utterance. Furthermore, in a more realistic setting in which the professional responded in a live session, LLMs outperform experts, as measured by MITI scoring. However, we note that this may be a measurement artefact. For example, “yeah” can be a more natural and helpful response than a comple x reflection to a given client utterance ;

however, while “yeah” receives no code, a complex reflection counts toward MI competence. Finally, we found that LLM utterances, although verbose and containing multiple reflections, do 

not clearly stand out from human therapist utterances. We consider this a proxy for the naturalness of conversation. 

This study has limitations. First, the real-world performance evaluation was done using a small dataset of 34 clinical transcripts. Second, our method for generating LLM-therapist responses meant that individual turns could be evaluated in context, but not a full, multiturn conversation conducted by an LLM-therapist, in which both client and therapist responses are dynamic. Having methodically established the competence of LLMs, the next step is to study them with a selected clinical population. 

We identify the following strengths: We used all MITI behavioural counts and metrics, and the evaluation was as rigorous as that for a human therapist. Beyond MITI coding, we also conducted an AI-human distinguishability experiment. This is also the first study to benchmark 

LLM performance on real-world interviews specifically from an addiction medicine setting, 

moving beyond synthetic toy and non-clinical datasets – showing competence even in naturalistic situations with little control over context. 

We find that LLMs (including open -source models) can achieve better MI proficiency with only 

prompt engineering, as shown in the current benchmarking study. Beginner proficiency scores 

(technical global ≥3.5, relational global ≥3) are difficult to achieve even in human therapists, as 

demonstrated by a systematic review of MI training outcomes for SUD ( Hall et al., 2016 ). 

Moreover, a 2.5 -year-long study on MI skills in clinicians found significant between -counsellor 

variation as well as performance fluctuation in individual counsellors despite ongoing 

supervision ( Forsberg et al., 2010 ). In such contexts, LLMs are a potential candidate for 

expanding MI counselling services where human therapists are scarce. MI also lends itself to rigorous objective evaluation with measurable metrics, which makes MI a viable candidate for automation with strict oversight. 

# 5 Conclusion 

This study demonstrates that LLMs can reach a level of proficiency in MI that matches, and in some respects exceeds, that of trained human therapists, even in real-world clinical transcripts. 15 

These findings suggest that LLMs may be considered for real -world application to scale up MI counselling services in SUD contexts in low-resource settings. Future work must focus on rigorous evaluation using simulated human-client conversations before trials with real patients.

<Theoretical Grounding> 

<Terminology> 

1. **Target Behavior:** A problematic behavior that the client must change, such as substance use, treatment non-adherence, or poor lifestyle choices. 

2. **Resistance:** The client’s irrational arguments, justifications, or defensiveness against 

changing the target behavior. This is a barrier to be overcome. 

3. **Correction:** The act of rectifying the client's misunderstandings, flawed logic, or denial 

about their behavior and its consequences. 

</Terminology> 

This counseling approach is rooted in a **Directive-Informational model**. The primary goal is to impart expert knowledge and guide the client toward a predetermined, medically or socially sound objective. The core techniques include: 

**Confrontation:** Directly challenging the client's discrepancies, denial, or irrational statements. 

**Persuasion:** Using logic, facts, and persuasion to convince the client to adopt a specific 

course of action. 

**Psychoeducation:** Providing unsolicited information about the harms of the client's 

current behavior and the benefits of the recommended change. 

**Authoritative Guidance:** Taking an expert role to prescribe solutions and plans of action. 

This is **not** Motivational Interviewing. The therapist is the expert, and the focus is on correcting the client's course, not on exploring their intrinsic motivation. Concepts like collaboration, and client autonomy are subordinate to the primary goal of achieving the target behavior change. 

<Directive-Informational Principles> 

1. **Expert-Led:** The therapist assumes the expert role, defining the problem and the 

solution. 

2. **Resistance as Deficit:** Client resistance is viewed as a lack of insight or willingness, 

which must be directly addressed and broken down. 

3. **Urgency-Driven:** The interaction is conducted with a sense of urgency, prioritizing the delivery of corrective information over rapport-building. 

</Directive-Informational Principles>
