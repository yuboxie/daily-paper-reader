## Abstract 

Large Language Model (LLM) personas with explicit specifications of attributes, background, and behavioural tendencies are increas-ingly used to simulate human conversations for tasks such as user modeling, social reasoning, and behavioural analysis. Ensuring that persona-grounded simulations faithfully reflect human con-versational behaviour is therefore critical. However, current evalu-ation practices largely rely on LLM-as-a-judge approaches, offering limited grounding in observable human behavior and producing opaque scalar scores. We address this gap by proposing Eval4Sim ,an evaluation framework that measures how closely simulated con-versations align with human conversational patterns across three complementary dimensions. Adherence captures how effectively persona backgrounds are implicitly encoded in generated utter-ances, assessed via dense retrieval with speaker-aware representa-tions. Consistency evaluates whether a persona maintains a distin-guishable identity across conversations, computed through author-ship verification. Naturalness reflects whether conversations exhibit human-like flow rather than overly rigid or optimized structure, quantified through distributions derived from dialogue-focused Nat-ural Language Inference. Unlike absolute or optimization-oriented metrics, Eval4Sim uses a human conversational corpus (i.e., Per-sonaChat ) as a reference baseline and penalizes deviations in both directions, distinguishing insufficient persona encoding from over-optimized, unnatural behaviour. Although demonstrated on 

PersonaChat , the applicability of Eval4Sim extends to any con-versational corpus containing speaker-level annotations.

However, current practice often relies on “LLM-as-a-judge” paradigms, where an LLM as-signs scores for attributes such as persona consistency or overall quality [ 11 , 47 ]. Consider a system simulating an introverted persona: To maximize adherence , a model might explic-itly state “I am an introvert,” making the persona easy to infer but reducing naturalness , since humans rarely repeat trait declarations in casual dialogue [ 7]. Conversely, a highly natural model may produce fluent turns while gradually attenuating persona cues, un-dermining consistency . A strong simulator should therefore match the trade-offs observed in human conversation, rather than inde-pendently maximizing a single score. To capture this complex dynamic, Eval4Sim compares simula-tions to the human proxy using three complementary evaluations: 

(1) Adherence via Dense Retrieval: We quantify how attributable a conversation is to its persona by treating persona descriptions as queries and conversations as documents. Using ColBERT [ 21 ], we target retrieval alignment : personas should be recoverable from simulated dialogue with the same clarity (or ambiguity) as in hu-man conversations. (2) Consistency via Authorship Verification: 

We measure identity stability across conversations by framing per-sona realization as same-author/different-author verification over stylometric signals. (3) Naturalness via NLI Distribution: We measure conversational flow by comparing NLI label distributions over turn-to-turn and persona-related pairs, capturing whether simulated dialogue is overly rigid (over-entailing) or incoherent (contradictory). The main contributions of this work are: 

• We propose Eval4Sim , an evaluation framework for persona-grounded conversation simulation that penalizes deviations from human behavior in both directions, enabling evaluation of under-expression versus over-optimization. 

• We implement three alignment dimensions: adherence, consis-tency, and naturalness, in a unified, corpus-agnostic pipeline. 

• We instantiate Eval4Sim on PersonaChat and evaluate ten simulation datasets. Our results show that (i) adherence is strongest for modern LLMs, (ii) consistency follows a different ordering, and (iii) all simulations shift toward higher entailment and lower neu-trality than human dialogue, indicating overly “logically smooth” flow. Aggregating these dimensions, no simulator matches humans uniformly, while Qwen3 30B provides the best overall balance. 

## 2 Related Work 

Persona-grounded dialogue systems condition response generation on persona descriptions to produce consistent conversational be-havior. The PersonaChat dataset [ 46 ] pioneered this paradigm as an early representative benchmark, featuring crowdsourced di-alogues where speakers adopt predefined personas consisting of at least five profile sentences. Methods for improving persona con-sistency include using NLI to encourage entailing utterances [ 41 ], reinforcement learning for offline training of persona-consistent agents [ 40 ], and techniques that balance adherence to persona de-scriptions against conversational naturalness [29]. Evaluating persona-grounded dialogue is challenging because personas influence conversation in multiple ways. Traditional met-rics such as BLEU [ 33 ], Perplexity [ 14 ], and ROUGE-L [ 28 ] corre-late poorly with human judgments in open-domain dialogue [ 30 ], Eval4Sim : An Evaluation Framework for Persona Simulation Conference’17, July 2017, Washington, DC, USA 

capturing surface overlap rather than persona consistency or con-versational quality [ 42 ]. Specialized frameworks introduce persona-specific metrics (e.g., speaker probability and term salience [ 43 ]), explicit consistency labels between profiles and responses [ 45 ], and human evaluation protocols with multiple scales [ 39 ]. However, these approaches often isolate single attributes and do not explic-itly characterize trade-offs among conversational dimensions like adherence, consistency, and naturalness. For adherence, information retrieval provides a practical assess-ment: if persona cues are expressed implicitly, a persona profile should reliably retrieve the corresponding conversation among distractors. Dense retrieval embeds queries and documents in a shared semantic space to match beyond lexical overlap. Sentence-BERT [ 35 ] enabled efficient bi-encoder retrieval, while ColBERT [ 22 ]introduced late interaction for finer matching, with subsequent ef-ficiency improvements such as ColBERTv2 [ 37 ]. Adapting this to persona evaluation reverses the usual perspective: persona profiles serve as semantically rich queries and full conversations as candi-date documents, so adherence corresponds to retrieval adherence : a conversation should be most strongly associated with its persona, at a level comparable to human dialogues. For consistency, we draw on authorship verification, which asks whether two texts originate from the same author based on writing style. The PAN shared tasks at CLEF provide benchmark datasets and protocols for this setting [ 4, 20 ], and analogous methods can test whether personas exhibit distinguishable stylistic signatures across conversations. For naturalness, Natural Language Inference (NLI) offers a complementary signal [ 13 ]: NLI models label utterance pairs as entailment, contradiction, or neutral, and the resulting label distributions across turns compactly reflect dialogue flow [ 45 ]. By comparing NLI label distributions between human and generated dialogues, we can assess how closely a simulator matches human-like pragmatic flow without requiring reference responses at each turn [45]. Recent work has begun to evaluate persona-grounded LLMs via large-scale benchmarks [ 1, 36 ]. PersoBench [ 1] focuses on turn-level 

response generation (i.e., only evaluating one single response each time), scoring outputs with a mix of reference-based metrics and persona-consistency measures. PersonaGym [36 ], in contrast, tar-gets persona agents, introducing a dynamic evaluation setup where personas are tested across automatically generated environments and tasks. Its scoring is based on LLMs-as-a-judge: model outputs are graded with rubric-based judgments and aggregated into task-level persona scores. Our proposal differs from both lines of work by two main aspects: 𝑖 ) we evaluate multi-turn conversation simu-lation, and 𝑖𝑖 ) we frame persona evaluation as alignment to human dialogue rather than scoring each turn against a gold response. In summary, our work differs from prior research in three ways. First, we evaluate persona-grounded dialogue across three comple-mentary dimensions rather than treating them independently: 𝑖 )

adherence through dense retrieval that maps persona descriptions (queries) to conversations (documents), 𝑖𝑖 ) consistency through stylometric authorship verification, and 𝑖𝑖𝑖 ) naturalness through NLI distributions between consecutive turns. Second, rather than optimising a single objective, we examine trade-offs between these dimensions to understand how they interact in practice. Third, we use PersonaChat dialogues as a behavioural proxy for evalua-tion, comparing how human-authored conversations balance these dimensions against synthetic alternatives to reveal how data gener-ation choices shape the relationship between natural discourse and explicit persona expression. 

## 3 Methodology 

Eval4Sim evaluates persona-grounded conversation simulation along three complementary dimensions that capture distinct as-pects of human-like dialogue: adherence to the persona descriptions, 

consistency of speaker identity across interactions, and naturalness 

of conversational flow. We use human-to-human conversations as reference and score simulators by their alignment to them, penal-izing deviations in either direction. This allows us to distinguish between under-expression of persona information and exaggerated, unnatural persona signalling. The framework operates on two inputs: (i) a reference corpus 

of human-to-human conversations that establishes behavioural baselines, and (ii) one or more simulation corpora produced by conversational simulators to evaluate. Both corpora contain multi-turn dialogues between two speakers, and each speaker is associated with a persona description. The framework is corpus-agnostic: any persona-grounded dataset with speaker-level persona annotations can serve as the reference, with the choice of reference determining what is considered “human-like” or target style of conversations for evaluation. The expected input format is illustrated with an example below:       

> (Alex ♂) User 1 persona: I like to dance at the club. I run a dog obedience school. (Emma ♀) User 2 persona: I love to meet new people. I have a turtle named Timothy.

## 3.1 Adherence via Dense Retrieval 

We define adherence as the ability of a simulator to implicitly ex-press a persona’s traits and background such that the generated conversation is attributable to that persona [ 46 ]. To evaluate this ability, we model adherence as a retrieval task: persona descriptions as queries and generated conversations as documents . Intuitively, if a simulator consistently manifests persona cues in dialogue, the associated persona description should retrieve that conversation more reliably than unrelated conversations. For this reason, the task here is to rank conversations in which the personas participated higher than those in which they did not. Formally, let C = {𝑐 1, . . . , 𝑐 𝑛 } denote a set of conversations and let P denote the set of personas. Each persona 𝑝 ∈ P is associ-ated with a set of relevant conversations C𝑝 ⊆ C in which that persona appears. Given 𝑝 as the query, the retrieval objective is to rank conversations such that those in C𝑝 appear above the rest of conversations (i.e., C \ C 𝑝 ). Our hypothesis is that the more a simulator manifests the per-sona’s traits in its conversations, the easier it becomes to match the persona description to the actual conversation. A key challenge, however, is that adherence can be over-optimized : a simulator may maximize persona mention frequency (e.g., repeatedly stating “As a teacher, I...”), yielding high retrievability but unnaturally explicit self-disclosure. Since our goal is human-like persona expression, 

Eval4Sim does not reward maximal retrieval performance. Instead, we compare retrieval behavior against a human reference corpus and penalize deviations in either direction, distinguishing under-encoding (persona cues too weak to retrieve) from over-encoding (persona cues too explicit to be human-like). To control task difficulty, we evaluate retrieval over increasing pool sizes. For a persona query 𝑝 , each pool is formed by sampling 𝑅 

relevant conversations from C𝑝 and 𝑘 distractor conversations from 

C \ C 𝑝 , where 𝑘 increases to induce a progressively harder ranking setting. We repeat sampling and average the metric at each pool size to obtain a Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR) [ 10 ] degradation curve. In our instantiation, we set 𝑅 = 1 and report MRR, which directly measures how highly the first relevant conversation is ranked (1.0 if first, 0.5 if second, etc.), matching our attribution objective. More generally, when multiple relevant conversations per persona are included in a pool ( 𝑅 > 1), the same evaluation protocol naturally extends to multi-relevance ranking metrics such as MAP or nDCG, which account for retrieving all relevant conversations rather than only the first hit. 

3.1.1 Speaker-Aware Encoding. Conversations contain content from two participants, while persona descriptions refer to a specific speaker. To better align queries with the intended source, we pro-pose a speaker-aware scoring scheme that interpolates between two ColBERT [ 22 ] indices: (i) a full index containing utterances from both speakers, and (ii) a target-speaker index containing only the evaluated speaker’s utterances. and compute: score (𝑞, 𝑐 ) = (1 − 𝑤 ) · score full (𝑞, 𝑐 ) + 𝑤 · score tgt (𝑞, 𝑐 ). (1) When 𝛼 = 0, ranking uses only the full index; when 𝛼 = 1, it uses only the target-speaker index. The sinusoidal mapping provides a smooth interpolation between configurations.

## 3.2 Consistency via Authorship Verification 

We define consistency as the ability of a simulator to maintain a sta-ble and distinguishable speaker identity across conversations [ 3 , 17 ]: utterances produced under the same persona should exhibit coher-ent stylistic regularities, while remaining separable from other personas. We address this capability as an authorship verification problem : given a pair of text samples (𝑡 1, 𝑡 2), the verifier predicts whether they originate from the same persona (same-author) or from different personas (different-author). Intuitively, stronger per-sona consistency should make same-author pairs more reliably distinguishable from different-author pairs. As with adherence, the goal here is not to maximize distinguisha-bility metrics. While stronger consistency improves verification performance, excessive stylistic uniformity can produce repetitive dialogue that lacks the natural variation observed in human speak-ers. Eval4Sim therefore evaluates alignment to a human reference corpus: a simulator is preferred when its same vs. different separa-bility matches the reference level, rather than exceeding it through repetitive or overly stylized generation. 

3.2.1 Verification model. To evaluate consistency, we employ a character 𝑛 -gram based approach adapted from the PAN 2023 Au-thorship Verification shared task [ 4 ]. This method is a widely used and robust baseline in PAN authorship verification tasks, as charac-ter 𝑛 -grams capture stylistic cues that generalize well across topics and domains. This approach represents documents using TF-IDF weighted character n-grams and calculates cosine similarity be-tween document pairs. The resulting similarities are calibrated and projected to pseudo-probabilities through a rescaling operation that maps the similarity space to interpretable verification scores. Through grid search on calibration data, the method determines optimal rescaling parameters 𝑝 1 and 𝑝 2 that partition the similarity space into three regions: scores below 𝑝 1 map to [0, 0.49 ] (different authors), scores above 𝑝 2 map to [0.51 , 1] (same author), and scores between 𝑝 1 and 𝑝 2 map to 0.5 (non-decision). We tune (𝑝 1, 𝑝 2) on training data via grid search. 

3.2.2 Verification Pair Construction. To create authorship verifica-tion pairs, we extract each persona’s utterances from conversations and split them at the sentence level. For same-author pairs, we divide a persona’s text at the midpoint, creating two samples from the same speaker. Different-author pairs are formed by pairing sam-ples from distinct personas. We construct balanced datasets with equal proportions of same-author and different-author pairs. We train a separate verifier for each corpus, using an 80/20 train/test split. We employ character 4-gram TF-IDF with a vocabulary of 4000 features, compute cosine similarities for training pairs, and select thresholds (𝑝 1, 𝑝 2) by exhaustive search over [0.01 , 0.99 ] with step 0.01, choosing the configuration that maximizes verification performance on the training split. ## 3.3 Naturalness via NLI Distribution 

We define naturalness as the ability to generate a simulated con-versation that exhibits human-like turn-to-turn dynamics while remaining compatible with the speaker’s persona and internally self-consistent [ 24 , 31 ]. In Eval4Sim , we compute naturalness through Natural Language Inference (NLI) relations computed (i) between consecutive utterances and (ii) between a speaker’s utterances and their persona descriptions. Formally, a dialogue is a sequence of turns between two inter-locutors, {( 𝑠 𝑡 , 𝑢 𝑡 )} 𝑇 𝑡 =1, where 𝑠 𝑡 ∈ { 1, 2} denotes the speaker at turn 

𝑡 and 𝑢 𝑡 is the utterance text. Each speaker 𝑠 has a persona de-scription 𝑃 𝑠 = {𝑝 𝑠, 1, . . . , 𝑝 𝑠,𝑛 }, in a set of 𝑛 persona sentences. To probe the logical relation between any two textual units arising from this structure, we use an NLI model: Given a text pair (𝑥, 𝑦 ),an NLI model predicts the relationship of 𝑥 and 𝑦 in three labels: entailment, neutral, or contradiction, with their associated softmax confidence scores. We apply the NLI model to obtain these labels for two kinds of pairs: (i) utterance turn pairs (𝑢 𝑡 −1, 𝑢 𝑡 ) to charac-terize local dialogue flow, and (ii) persona-utterance pairs (𝑝 𝑠,𝑖 , 𝑢 𝑡 )

to assess whether an utterance conflicts with the speaker’s persona description. For the NLI model, we employ a DeBERTa-based NLI model trained on conversational data [ 45 ]. The zayn1111/deberta-v3-dnli 

model, fine-tuned on the Dialogue NLI dataset, achieves 95.36% ac-curacy on dialogue inference tasks, outperforming general-purpose NLI models trained on formal text. For each pair (𝑥, 𝑦 ), we obtain both the predicted label and its confidence. 

3.3.1 Metrics derived from NLI classification. From these NLI pre-dictions, we derive four complementary metrics: 

Coherence Score (CS). CS summarizes turn-to-turn flow over con-secutive turns. For each consecutive pair (𝑢 𝑡 −1, 𝑢 𝑡 ), we assign 1.0 if the relation is entailment, 0.5 if it is neutral (allowing topic shifts or new information), and 0.0 if it is contradiction. The final CS is the mean of these values across all consecutive turn pairs in the corpus.

Self Contradiction Rate (SCR). SCR captures within-speaker in-consistency. For each utterance 𝑢 𝑡 , we compare it against the previ-ous 𝐾 utterances from the same speaker (we use 𝐾 = 5), forming pairs (𝑢 𝑡 ′ , 𝑢 𝑡 ) where 𝑡 ′ < 𝑡 and 𝑠 𝑡 ′ = 𝑠 𝑡 . As with PCR, a contradiction is counted only when the label is in contradiction with confidence of at least 0.7. SCR is the fraction of contradictory within-speaker pairs among all such comparisons. 

Entailment Rate (ER). . ER is the proportion of consecutive turn pairs (𝑢 𝑡 −1, 𝑢 𝑡 ) labeled as entailment. ER serves as a diagnostic: unusually high entailment may indicate overly rigid, repetitive pro-gression, whereas lower values reflect greater pragmatic flexibility.

## 4 Experiments and Results 

This section describes the experimental instantiation of the Eval4Sim 

framework and reports results across the three evaluation dimen-sions. We first describe the experimental setup (reference corpus, adherence ranking configuration, and evaluated simulations), and then present adherence , consistency , and naturalness results for each simulation relative to the human baseline. 

## 4.1 Experimental Setup 

4.1.1 Reference Corpus. We instantiate the framework using Per-sonaChat [ 46 ] as the reference corpus. PersonaChat consists of persona-grounded, human-to-human dialogues crowdsourced via Amazon Mechanical Turk, where each speaker is assigned a short profile of persona descriptions that conditions the conversation. In this work, we treat PersonaChat as the behavioural proxy that defines the target distribution for human-like simulation.

## 4.2 Experiment 1: Adherence 

We test whether simulated conversations encode persona infor-mation in a way that is attributable to the intended persona, and whether this encoding matches the level of implicitness observed in human dialogue. We instantiate adherence as a retrieval task: each conversation contains two speakers, and we evaluate the target persona (User 2) by retrieving the conversation they participated in using only target persona description as the query. As the candi-date pool grows (more distractor conversations), retrieval becomes harder unless persona cues are expressed consistently across the target user’s utterances. Eval4Sim : An Evaluation Framework for Persona Simulation Conference’17, July 2017, Washington, DC, USA 

Our goal is not to maximize retrieval. Instead, we compare how retrieval degrades with difficulty and score each simulator by its alignment to the PersonaChat degradation pattern. Under-encoding typically manifests as consistently worse MRR than the human curve (persona cues are too weak or too generic). Over-encoding can manifest as systematically better MRR than humans (persona cues are unnaturally explicit or repeated), producing a curve that stays above the human baseline across pool sizes. 

4.2.1 Experimental Setting. For each dataset, we build ColBERT indices using the target-speaker only configuration ( 𝛼 = 1.0). Con-versations are chunked and aggregated via MaxSim as described in Section 3.1. We evaluate MRR at 13 pool sizes corresponding to 

𝐷 ∈ [ 1, 1000 ] distractors (i.e., one relevant conversation plus 𝐷 ran-domly sampled distractors), producing an MRR degradation curve per dataset. Finally, we compute the weighted curve-similarity score between each simulation and the PersonaChat reference using span-corrected weights (Section 3.1). 

4.2.2 Results. Figure 3 shows MRR degradation as the number of distractors increases. PersonaChat drops from near-perfect re-trieval at very small pools to roughly the mid 0.3 range at 1000 dis-tractors. Several LLM-generated corpora closely track this human degradation pattern across the full range, indicating that their per-sona cues are neither substantially weaker nor unnaturally stronger than those found in human conversations. The Generator–Critic datasets (SPC and SPC-New) deviate more clearly. SPC remains consistently below the human curve and ends as the lowest-performing dataset at large pool sizes, suggesting weaker persona attribution signals. SPC-New shows a sharp early drop (low MRR even with relatively few distractors) and then par-tially converges toward the cluster of other curves at larger pools, indicating instability in persona attribution. Overall, these trends support the view that, under this retrieval-based adherence probe, direct dialogue generation with modern open LLMs produces more human-aligned persona encoding than the older Generator–Critic synthetic corpora. Table 3 quantifies alignment using the weighted curve-similarity score (1.0 indicates a perfect match to the human reference curve). Among the evaluated simulations, Gemma 3 12B is closest to the hu-man baseline ( −1.70% deviation), followed by Qwen3 30B (−2.57% )and Gemma 3 27B (−3.00% ). Smaller LLMs are generally less aligned (e.g., Qwen3 1.7B −16 .01% , Gemma 3 1B −8.07% ), while both Generator– Critic datasets are the least aligned ( SPC −18 .93% , SPC-New −16 .30% ). Taken together, the results suggest two broad effects: (i) within each model family, increased capacity tends to improve adherence align-ment, and (ii) the generation pipeline matters, with modern LLMs direct generation producing curves markedly closer to human be-havior than the released Generator–Critic synthetic corpora.

## 4.4 Experiment 3: Naturalness 

We test whether simulated conversations exhibit human-like con-versational flow comparable to human dialogue. Following Sec-tion 3.3, we apply a dialogue-focused NLI model to three types of text pairs: (i) consecutive turns to characterize local dialogue flow, (ii) persona–utterance pairs to detect conflicts with persona descriptions, and (iii) within-speaker historical pairs to detect self-contradictions. As in the other dimensions, our objective is align-ment rather than metric maximization: human conversations are neither perfectly coherent nor strictly entailment-driven. Instead, they are dominated by neutral transitions that reflect pragmatic responses, topic drift, and elaboration, with relatively few contra-dictions. 

4.4.1 Experimental Setting. For each dataset, we apply the DeBERTa-based Dialogue NLI model to classify the pairwise relations. We compute the metrics explained in Section 3.3, Coherence Score (CS) on consecutive turns, Persona Contradiction Rate (PCR) on persona–utterance pairs, and Self-Contradiction Rate (SCR) on within-speaker history, using a contradiction confidence threshold of 0.7 and a 5-turn window for self-contradiction checks. We aggre-gate these metrics into a Naturalness score and compute Similarity to PersonaChat using the bidirectional deviation penalty, so that deviations in either direction (under or over naturalness) from the baseline reduce similarity. 

4.4.2 Results. Table 5 shows naturalness evaluation across datasets. Similarity scores reveal that all simulations deviate from PersonaChat ,with offsets ranging from −4.02% to −13 .58% . The closest alignment to the human baseline is achieved by Qwen3-30B (−4.02% ), followed by Gemma 3 4B (−4.39% ). These models also show NLI distribu-tions relatively close to PersonaChat , with entailment rates in the 0.217–0.232 range and neutral rates around 0.709–0.736, compared to 0.157/0.796 in PersonaChat .A key pattern is that many simulations are systematically over-entailing. Human dialogue is neutral-dominant ( PersonaChat :ER/NR/CR = 0.157 /0.796 /0.047 ), whereas simulated corpora move from neutral to entailment (ER = 0.217-0.458; NR = 0.519-0.736). This shift is most pronounced for the Generator–Critic datasets, which exhibit both the highest coherence scores (SPC: CS=0.682; SPC-New: CS=0.718) and the highest entailment rates (SPC: ER=0.394; SPC-New: ER=0.458). Notably, this produces an overshoot effect: 

SPC-New attains the highest Naturalness score (0.828), yet it is the least aligned with human dialogue ( −13 .58% offset), because its flow is overly coherent and entailment-heavy relative to the human baseline. 

4.4.3 External validation. To validate that PersonaChat ’s NLI distribution reflects general human conversation, we additionally analyse 1,439 casual dialogues from three external datasets [ 19 , 27 ,34 ] with the same metrics. These conversations show a similarly neutral-dominant pattern (ER/NR/CR = 0.17/0.81/0.02), consistent with PersonaChat (0.16/0.80/0.05), confirming that human dia-logue predominantly progresses through neutral transitions rather than strict entailment. In contrast, the simulated datasets (espe-cially the Generator–Critic corpora) produce an overly “logically smooth” flow that reduces topic drift. Compared to Experiments 1 (Section 4.2) and 2 (Section 4.3), this finding is complementary: some corpora can appear highly coherent under NLI while still exhibiting weak adherence, indicating that logical coherence is not a proxy for persona-grounded realism.

## 5 Conclusions 

We presented Eval4Sim , an evaluation framework for persona-grounded conversation simulation that measures alignment to hu-man conversational behavior across three complementary dimen-sions: adherence (persona attribution via dense retrieval), consis-tency (identity stability via authorship verification), and naturalness Conference’17, July 2017, Washington, DC, USA Bao et al. 

(conversational flow via dialogue NLI patterns). In contrast to LLM-as-a-judge paradigms that return opaque scalar scores, Eval4Sim 

uses a human dialogue corpus as a behavioral baseline and penalizes deviations in either direction, separating under-expression of per-sona cues from over-optimized, unnatural behavior. Experiments over ten simulated corpora reveal clear trade-offs. LLM-generated conversations achieve the strongest adherence, while Generator– Critic datasets perform worse. Consistency exhibits a different ordering, with Qwen3 14B closest to the human baseline. For natu-ralness, all simulations shift toward higher entailment compared to human dialogue, indicating an overly “logically smooth” flow. When aggregating dimensions, no simulator matches humans uniformly; 

Qwen3 30B provides the best overall balance (0.950), combining strong adherence with the closest naturalness alignment. More generally, these results highlight why persona simulation should be evaluated multi-dimensionally: optimizing a single objective can move a simulator away from the human baseline on another axis. Eval4Sim provides a practical and interpretable foundation for guiding the development of persona simulators that better match how humans balance dialogue dynamics.
