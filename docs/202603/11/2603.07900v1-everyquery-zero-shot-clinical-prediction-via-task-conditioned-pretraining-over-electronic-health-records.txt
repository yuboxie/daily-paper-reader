# Abstract 

Foundation models pretrained on electronic health records (EHR) have demon-strated zero-shot clinical prediction capabilities by generating synthetic patient futures and aggregating statistics over sampled trajectories. However, this autore-gressive inference procedure is computationally expensive, statistically noisy, and not natively promptable because users cannot directly condition predictions on specific clinical questions. In this preliminary work, we introduce EveryQuery, an EHR foundation model that achieves zero-shot inference through task-conditioned pretraining . Rather than generating future events, EveryQuery takes as input a patient’s history and a structured query specifying a clinical task, and directly estimates the likelihood of the outcome occurring in the future window via a single forward pass. EveryQuery realizes this capability by pre-training over randomly sampled combinations of query tasks and patient contexts, directly training the model to produce correct answers to arbitrary input prompts. This enables zero-shot prediction for any task in the query space without finetuning, linear probing, or trajectory generation. On MIMIC-IV, EveryQuery outperforms an autoregressive baseline on 82% of 39 randomly sampled prediction tasks, with a mean AUC improvement of +0 .16 (95% CI: [0 .10 , 0.22] ). This advantage remains consistent on tasks that were explicitly held out from the pretraining distribution. Further, EveryQuery’s performance gains are most pronounced for rare clinical events, affirming and demonstrating a solution to the fundamental limitation of autore-gressive inference for low-prevalence outcomes. However, at present, EveryQuery underperforms on tasks requiring disjunctive reasoning over multiple codes, such as 30-day readmission, exposing a concrete expressiveness limitation of the current query language. 

# 1 Introduction 

Foundation models pretrained on electronic health records (EHRs) have emerged as a promising paradigm for clinical prediction. By tokenizing patient timelines and training autoregressive trans-formers to generate future medical events, recent work has shown that a single pretrained model can predict disease incidence, mortality, and clinical outcomes without task-specific supervision [Renc et al., 2024, Waxler et al., 2025, Pang et al., 2025a, Shmatko et al., 2025]. These models capture complex longitudinal dependencies across diagnoses, procedures, and medications, and exhibit favorable scaling properties as model size and training data increase [Waxler et al., 2025, Rajamohan et al., 2025]. The technical advances delivered by the ‘pretrain once, apply broadly’ paradigm underscore the transformative potential of foundation models in clinical decision support.

Despite their promise, leading EHR foundation models employ an autoregressive approach that imposes significant practical constraints and limits clinical utility. Inference is expensive: obtaining a single prediction requires sampling many future trajectories and aggregating statistics over these samples [Renc et al., 2025]. This generation process is particularly inefficient for long-horizon predictions, which require more generation steps per trajectory. Resulting estimates are statistically noisy, with prediction variance dependent on the number of sampled trajectories, and errors compound when evaluating many patients across many tasks [Solo et al., 2026, Waxler et al., 2025]. Additionally, these models are not natively promptable: users cannot directly condition predictions on specific clinical questions without either designing custom aggregation procedures or redefining the inference pipeline, greatly limiting the interactivity of these models and their accessibility for niche clinical tasks. Together, expensive inference, high variance, and limited promptability constrain the utility of autoregressive EHR foundation models. This landscape motivates three desirable properties for EHR foundation models: 1. Zero-shot inference : making predictions for arbitrary tasks without task-specific finetuning. 2. Efficiency : inference should require minimal computation (avoiding trajectory rollouts) and provide low-variance predictions (avoiding large-sample aggregation). 3. Promptability : users should be able to condition model outputs on structured clinical queries, analogous to how text prompts specify tasks in large language models [Brown et al., 2020]. No existing EHR foundation model satisfies all three properties simultaneously. Autoregressive models achieve zero-shot inference but require expensive, high-variance sampling and lack native promptability. Multitask discriminative approaches [Steinberg et al., 2023, Bertsimas and Ma, 2024] are efficient at the expense of zero-shot generalization, requiring task-specific output heads or finetuning for each new task. In this preprint, we introduce EveryQuery , an EHR foundation model that achieves all three properties through task-conditioned pretraining over parametrized clinical queries. We leverage the observation that clinical prediction tasks exhibit a uniform parametric structure: the vast majority can be expressed as “Does outcome c occur within time window [t1, t 2]?” [Xu et al., 2024, Pang et al., 2025a]. EveryQuery operationalizes this insight by training a model to take as input both a patient’s medical history and a structured task ‘query’, and to directly estimate the answer. By pretraining over the distribution of such queries by uniformly sampling tasks at random across patient inputs, EveryQuery learns to condition predictions on task specifications in the input space. At inference, users simply provide the query parameters {c ; [ t1, t 2]} alongside the patient data, enabling zero-shot prediction for any task expressible in this structured form via a single forward pass. We evaluate EveryQuery on the MIMIC-IV dataset and demonstrate: • Zero-shot performance : EveryQuery outperforms an autoregressive baseline on 82% of 39 randomly sampled prediction tasks ( p < 10 −5, Wilcoxon signed-rank), with a mean AUC win margin of +0 .16 (95% CI: [0 .10 , 0.22] ). • Generalization to held-out codes : EveryQuery shows no degradation on tasks defined by codes explicitly withheld from the pretraining distribution, confirming that task conditioning transfers seamlessly to novel outcome targets. • Rare event advantage : EveryQuery’s advantage is most pronounced for rare clinical events (Spearman ρ = −0.32 , p < 0.05 ), consistent with a fundamental limitation of trajectory-based inference for low-prevalence outcomes. • Efficient inference : For a single prediction task, EveryQuery is approximately 3,000 ×

faster than the autoregressive baseline, requiring only a single forward pass per patient rather than 20 trajectory rollouts. • Prompt specificity : EveryQuery’s internal representations are organized by query rather than by patient. # 2 Background and Motivation 

A foundation model is characterized by broad task coverage: after pretraining on large datasets, it can address diverse downstream tasks without task-specific supervision [Wornow et al., 2023a, Bommasani et al., 2021]. In this section, we review existing EHR foundation models, then motivate task conditioning as a unifying mechanism. 

2.1 EHR Foundation Models 

Autoregressive EHR models have emerged as the dominant instantiation of the foundation model paradigm for structured clinical data [Renc et al., 2024, Waxler et al., 2025, Pang et al., 2025a, Rajamohan et al., 2025, Shmatko et al., 2025]. These models tokenize patient timelines and train transformers via next-token prediction. At inference, zero-shot predictions are obtained by sampling unconditional future trajectories and estimating the likelihood of the target event over samples [Renc et al., 2025]. Multitask and representation learning approaches take a different path. MOTOR [Steinberg et al., 2023] pretrains time-to-event predictions for thousands of events. M3H [Bertsimas and Ma, 2024] unifies multimodal multitask prediction. CLMBR [Steinberg et al., 2021] pretrains a clinical language model on structured EHR data and adapts to downstream tasks via learned representations. However, these approaches require predefined output heads or task-specific finetuning, precluding zero-shot inference. Table 1 summarizes how existing approaches relate to the three desiderata. 32.2 Task Conditioning as a Unifying Mechanism 

While typical foundation models explored to date have leveraged generative pre-training objectives, which can be repurposed at inference time to enable zero-shot prediction, here we take a different approach and directly train a model to predict the target output for a task that is passed as input to the system directly. While atypical as a direct training objective, task conditioning in general is widely used for foundation models. In LLMs, prompts serve as task specifications enabling zero-shot inference [Brown et al., 2020]. Instruction tuning showed that training over task distributions enables zero-shot generalization when tasks and labels are unified through a shared training objective [Sanh et al., 2021, Wei et al., 2021]. For EHR data, we can leverage task conditioning even more directly than is possible in NLP because clinical tasks have a natural parametric structure [Xu et al., 2024]. Most reduce to binary classification: does a specific event occur within a time window? [Pang et al., 2025a, Renc et al., 2024]. A recent benchmark defines 14 tasks within a common schema [Pang et al., 2025b]. When tasks can be expressed through structured parameters, task conditioning naturally begets promptability. Task conditioning simultaneously enables all three desiderata of EHR foundation models: zero-shot inference through full-support pretraining, efficiency via single forward passes, and promptability 

through direct task specification. However, it also imposes a constraint: the model can only answer questions expressible in the query language. In our current, preliminary work, this tension is significant, as the query language we use for this analysis is relatively limited, which we show directly in Section 4.6. However, we believe that task conditioning can generalize to much more complex query specifications, enabling greater expressivity without sacrificing the benefits of our approach, which we discuss more in Section 6. 

# 3 Methods 

3.1 Problem Formulation 

We tokenize each patient’s medical history into a sequence of discrete code tokens x =(x1, x 2, . . . , x L), where xi ∈ V and V is a shared vocabulary of medical codes following a “ev-erything is code” paradigm similar to that used in [Renc et al., 2024]. Time intervals, diagnoses, procedures, medications, and lab results are all represented as code tokens under the MEDS standard [Arnrich et al., 2024]. Let tL denote the timestamp of the final event in x.A query q = ( c, ∆t) asks: does code c ∈ V occur within ∆t time after tL? The binary label is: 

y(q, x) = 1 [∃ j > L : xj = c and tL < t j ≤ tL + ∆ t] (1) When the patient’s record ends before tL + ∆ t, the label is censored because we cannot observe whether the event would have occurred. EveryQuery handles censoring explicitly through a dedicated output head. While the query language supports arbitrary ∆t, in all experiments we fix ∆t = 30 days, effectively reducing the query to a single parameter q = ( c, 30) . This is a substantial reduction in expressivity: the model is never trained on varying prediction horizons, and its ability to generalize across ∆t is untested. Clinically, different time horizons encode fundamentally different questions (e.g., 48-hour mortality vs. 1-year mortality), so using the full query space is an important direction for future work. 

3.2 Architecture 

EveryQuery uses a single bidirectional transformer backbone with two prediction heads. The backbone follows the ModernBERT-base architecture 1, initialized with random weights. The model has 22 transformer layers, a hidden dimension of 768, 12 attention heads, and approximately 149M parameters in the backbone. 

Query prepending. The query code c is mapped to a single token using the model’s shared code embedding layer, producing q ∈ Rd. This token is prepended to the patient’s code sequence to form the input [q; x1, x 2, . . . , x L]. The concatenated sequence is processed jointly by the bidirectional 

> 1https://huggingface.co/answerdotai/ModernBERT-base

4transformer, so the query token attends to all patient tokens and vice versa. This achieves the effect of cross-attention between the query and patient context within a unified self-attention mechanism. 

Prediction heads. The final-layer hidden state at position 0 (the query token) serves as a task-conditioned bottleneck representation. Because the query token has attended to the full patient context, this single vector captures query-relevant information from the patient history. Two separate 2-layer MLPs with a hidden dimension of 128, ReLU activations, and dropout of 0.1 map this representation to scalar predictions: ˆyoccurs = P (event occurs | x, q ) and ˆycens = P (censored | x, q ). Although the censoring label depends only on data availability and not on the query in principle, both heads share the same query-conditioned input for architectural simplicity. The maximum sequence length is 256 tokens, and training uses 16-bit mixed precision. 

3.3 Pretraining Query construction. Queries are constructed by sampling from a set of 10,000 codes drawn uniformly at random from the full vocabulary of 11,467 codes produced by MEDS preprocessing [McDermott et al., 2024]. In all experiments, ∆t = 30 days. The remaining codes outside this set are held out from the query sampling distribution and serve as out-of-distribution evaluation tasks. Importantly, held-out codes still appear in patient histories; they are only excluded as query targets .This ensures that the (shared) embedding layer between the query tokens and the patient tokens can enable generalization to tasks that were not seen as queries during pre-training during inference. 

Training samples. Each training sample consists of a patient history and a randomly assigned query. Only prediction times with at least 50 preceding events are eligible, ensuring sufficient clinical context. Patient histories are subsampled using a strategy that retains the most recent events within the 256-token context window up to the prediction time. 

Censoring. A query is labeled as censored (ycens = 1 ) if the patient’s record contains fewer than 30 days of data following the prediction time. When data is observed ( ycens = 0 ), the binary occurrence label yoccurs is computed from the patient’s actual future events. 

Loss function. The model produces two predictions from the query-conditioned representation: 

ˆycens (predicted probability of censoring) and ˆyoccurs (predicted probability of the queried event). These are optimized via a multi-task objective: 

L = Lcens + λLoccurs 

The censoring loss Lcens is binary cross-entropy (BCE) computed over all training samples. The occurrence loss Loccurs is masked to ensure the model only learns from trajectories with known outcomes: 

Loccurs = (1 − ycens ) · BCE (ˆ yoccurs , y occurs )

For censored samples ( ycens = 1 ), the occurrence gradient is zeroed. We set λ = 1 .0.

Optimization. We optimize with AdamW ( lr = 10 −5, β = (0 .9, 0.999) , weight decay = 0 .05 ,

ϵ = 10 −8) using a cosine learning-rate schedule with 2,000 warmup steps over a maximum of 40,000 training steps. Weight decay is not applied to bias or layer normalization parameters. Early stopping monitors validation loss with a patience of 20 checks and a minimum improvement threshold of 

5 × 10 −5. The batch size is 160. Training is performed on a single NVIDIA L40S GPU using 16-bit mixed precision. Training terminated via early stopping at step 24,859 with a wall-clock time of 385 minutes. 

3.4 Zero-Shot Inference 

At inference, the user specifies q = ( c, ∆t) and the model produces ˆyoccurs via a single deterministic forward pass. There is neither finetuning nor trajectory sampling. The prediction is a function of the query: changing c or ∆t changes which patient history features are attended to and what is predicted. This contrasts with autoregressive inference, which requires generating K future trajectories per patient and estimating P (event ) ≈ K−1 PKk=1 1[event in trajectory k]. With K = 20 (as used in 5our experiments), rare events with prevalence p yield an expected count of only 20 p detections per patient. For events with p < 0.05 , fewer than one trajectory per patient is expected to contain the event, leading to heavily quantized and often zero-valued probability estimates. 

# 4 Experiments 

4.1 Experimental Setup Dataset. We train and evaluate on MIMIC-IV (v2.2), extracted into the MEDS format using the MIMIC_IV_MEDS ETL pipeline (v0.0.7). [Arnrich et al., 2024, McDermott et al., 2024] 2. There are 11,467 unique codes after preprocessing. The dataset comprises 200,773 patients for training, 25,059 for validation, and 15,031 for the 39 random task test set. The readmission test set contains 10,249 patients because it is restricted to patients with at least 30 days of observed data following discharge. 

Baseline. We compare against MEDS-EIC-AR [McDermott, 2025], 3 an open-source “everything-is-code” autoregressive generative model in the style of ETHOS [Renc et al., 2024]. The model has 102M parameters, tokenizes patient timelines, and performs next-token prediction. It uses a max sequence length of 1024 tokens, of which at most 256 are reserved for the input patient context and the remainder for autoregressive generation. Both models therefore operate on the same effective patient history context of 256 tokens at inference time, though MEDS-EIC-AR benefits from much longer contexts during training. At inference, the AR model generates K = 20 future trajectories per patient and estimates outcome probabilities by counting event occurrences across sampled futures. All models were trained and evaluated on identical data splits. 

Evaluation. For computational efficiency in evaluation of the autoregressive baseline, we limit our evaluation to 38 randomly sampled prediction tasks, each defined by a medical code and a 30-day prediction window. Tasks span lab values (18), diagnoses (5), medications (5), infusions (6), fluid outputs (3), and procedures (1). Of these, 21 use codes included in EveryQuery’s pretraining query distribution (in-distribution; ID) and 18 use codes explicitly held out as query targets (out-of-distribution; OOD). Note that, the autoregressive baseline has no notion of this distinction. In addition, we test on two more traditional clinical tasks: 30d mortality and on 30-day readmission, a clinically important task that does not fit our query vocabulary as it requires any of 70 admission codes to occur, and therefore requires approximate inference for EQ. We report AUROC as the primary metric. 

Sample size considerations. The autoregressive baseline generates K = 20 trajectories per patient. Many randomly sampled tasks involve rare events (prevalence < 0.5% ), meaning the number of positive cases in the evaluation cohort may be small. Individual AUC estimates for low-prevalence tasks carry substantial uncertainty. We therefore emphasize aggregate comparisons (win rates, signed-rank tests) over individual task-level claims. 

4.2 Zero-Shot Prediction Across Random Tasks 

EveryQuery outperforms the autoregressive baseline on 32 of 39 tasks ( 82% ), with a mean AUC dif-ference of +0 .160 (95% CI: [0 .104 , 0.216] ; median +0 .176 ). The improvement is highly significant (Wilcoxon signed-rank p = 5 × 10 −6). EveryQuery achieves a mean AUC of 0.843 vs. 0.683 for the autoregressive baseline. Figure 2 shows results for individual task specifications. The 7 tasks where the autoregressive model outperforms are concentrated among diagnosis codes (3/5 diagnosis tasks) and include two medications, one lab, and one procedure code. We find that EveryQuery’s loss margin is generally smaller in magnitude than its win margin (median loss = −0.08 vs. median win = +0 .21 ). Notably, the autoregressive model performs poorly for the lowest-prevalence tasks. These findings are consistent with the limitations of trajectory-based inference. 4.3 Generalization to Held-Out Query Codes 

A key question for task-conditioned models is whether they generalize to queries involving codes not seen as query targets during pretraining. Recall that approximately 10,000 codes were sampled as possible query targets during pretraining (Section 3.3); codes outside this set were held out entirely from the query distribution, though they still appeared in patient histories. We find that EveryQuery’s performance is consistent across both groups: • In-distribution (21 tasks): 15/21 won (71%), mean ∆ = +0 .167 .• Out-of-distribution (18 tasks): 17/18 won (94%), mean ∆ = +0 .152 .The mean ∆AUC is nearly identical across ID and OOD tasks ( +0 .167 vs. +0 .152 ; Mann-Whitney 

p = 0 .68 ), and the win-rate difference is not statistically significant (Fisher’s exact p = 0 .10 ). Because the AR baseline is agnostic to the ID/OOD distinction, any systematic difference in ∆AUC would reflect degradation by EveryQuery on held-out codes. Since we observe none, we conclude that EveryQuery generalizes to held-out query codes without measurable performance loss. 

4.4 Rare Event Advantage 

Figure 2 plots the AUC difference against event prevalence across all 39 tasks. There is a statistically significant negative correlation (Spearman ρ = −0.318 , p = 0 .048 ): EveryQuery’s advantage is greatest for rare events. Decomposing this effect reveals a striking asymmetry. The autoregressive model’s AUC is strongly correlated with prevalence (Spearman ρ = 0 .64 , p < 10 −4): its discriminative performance degrades markedly for rare events. EveryQuery’s AUC, by contrast, is essentially uncorrelated with prevalence (ρ = 0 .18 , p = 0 .28 ). The rare-event advantage is not driven by EveryQuery performing especially 

well on rare events, but by its performance being prevalence-invariant . Our task conditioning pretraining strategy predicts rare outcomes about as reliably as common ones. The autoregressive model cannot achieve this because trajectory-based inference is fundamentally coupled to event frequency. This pattern has a natural explanation. Autoregressive models estimate event probabilities by counting occurrences across K sampled trajectories. For rare events with true probability p ≪ 1, the expected number of trajectories containing the event is Kp , which may be very small even for large K.The resulting estimates have high variance and low sensitivity. EveryQuery circumvents this by conditioning directly on the target event code, attending to relevant signals in the patient’s history regardless of population prevalence. 

4.5 Prompt Specificity: Embedding Analysis 

We ask whether EveryQuery genuinely conditions its representations on the query or learns a fixed patient embedding with a task-specific readout. We extract the final-layer hidden state at the query token position for all (patient, query) pairs in the evaluation set and analyze the geometry of the resulting embedding space. Figure 3a shows a UMAP projection (n_neighbors = 15 , min_dist = 0 .1) of all embeddings. While embeddings from different patients are fully interleaved, distinct clusters emerge corresponding to queries. The query is the dominant organizing axis of the representation space. Figure 3b quantifies this observation via pairwise cosine similarity. Same-query pairs from different patients exhibit the highest similarity (mean = 0 .82 , median = 0 .88 ), substantially exceeding same-patient pairs from different queries (mean = 0 .72 , median = 0 .75 ). Different-patient, different-query pairs form a lower-similarity baseline (mean = 0 .56 , median = 0 .59 ). These results confirm that EveryQuery’s representations are prompt-specific : the query modulates the representation itself via cross-attention (Section 3), rather than serving as a post-hoc selector over a fixed patient summary. 4.6 Readmission: A Task Outside the Scope of the Query Language 

To evaluate EveryQuery on tasks outside the scope of our limited query language, we assess 30-day hospital readmission. Unlike the single-code tasks above, readmission is defined as the occurrence of any admission event within 30 days of discharge. Due to the data schema of MIMIC-IV, this readmission event resolves into a disjunction over 70 distinct admission codes. Because EveryQuery’s query language specifies a single code, evaluating readmission requires querying all 70 admission codes separately and aggregating the predictions. We tested two aggregation strategies: (1) taking the maximum predicted probability across all 70 queries, and (2) assuming conditional independence and computing 1−Q70

This result illustrates a concrete boundary of the current query language: tasks that require disjunctive reasoning over many codes are not naturally expressible as single queries, and naive post-hoc 8aggregation does not recover the information that a generative model captures implicitly. The autoregressive model, by generating full future trajectories, can detect any admission event without needing to enumerate specific codes. This motivates extending the query language to support disjunctions and other compositional operators (Section 6). 

4.7 Computational Efficiency 

EveryQuery requires a single forward pass per (patient, query) pair, and predictions are deterministic. The autoregressive baseline requires generating K = 20 full future trajectories per patient. On the evaluation cohort, the autoregressive baseline required approximately 25 hours (89,990 seconds) to generate all trajectories, averaging roughly 6 seconds per patient. EveryQuery evaluates a single task across all patients in approximately 30 seconds, or about 20 milliseconds per patient. For a single prediction task, this represents a speedup of approximately 3,000 ×.Because AR trajectory generation is a one-time cost amortized across tasks, while EveryQuery’s cost scales linearly with the number of tasks, the effective speedup decreases as more tasks are evaluated simultaneously. The breakeven point is approximately 3,000 concurrent tasks, far beyond typical clinical workloads. 

# 5 Related Work 

Autoregressive EHR foundation models. Several recent models tokenize patient timelines and train autoregressive transformers via next-token prediction, achieving zero-shot clinical prediction by generating synthetic future trajectories and aggregating statistics over samples [Renc et al., 2024, 2025, Waxler et al., 2025, Pang et al., 2025a, Shmatko et al., 2025, Rajamohan et al., 2025]. This trajectory sampling paradigm is computationally expensive, yields quantized and high-variance estimates for rare events, and does not allow users to directly condition predictions on specific clinical questions. Variance-reduced estimators [Solo et al., 2026] mitigate the statistical inefficiency but remain coupled to trajectory generation. 

Discriminative and multitask EHR models. An alternative line of work pretrains bidirectional or encoder-decoder representations on EHR data [Rasmy et al., 2021, Pang et al., 2021, Wornow et al., 2023b, Yang et al., 2023] and then adapts to downstream tasks via finetuning, linear probing, or task-specific output heads. Multitask models such as MOTOR [Steinberg et al., 2023] and M3H [Bertsimas and Ma, 2024] pretrain predictions for many events simultaneously but still require predefined heads for each target. These approaches achieve efficient single-pass inference but cannot generalize zero-shot to arbitrary clinical queries. 

Task specification and benchmarking. Recent benchmarking efforts have formalized the obser-vation that clinical prediction tasks share a uniform parametric structure: most reduce to whether a specific event occurs within a time window [Xu et al., 2024, Pang et al., 2025b, Wornow et al., 2023a]. The Medical Event Data Standard (MEDS) [Arnrich et al., 2024] and tools like ACES [Xu et al., 2024] codify this structure into reproducible, dataset-agnostic task definitions. This regularity is the structural foundation that our query language exploits. 

Task conditioning and zero-shot generalization. Task conditioning, supplying a task specification alongside data as model input, is a well-established mechanism for zero-shot generalization. In generative modeling, conditioning on class labels enables controllable outputs without retraining [Mirza and Osindero, 2014, Ho and Salimans, 2022]. In NLP, instruction tuning over many tasks described via natural language induces zero-shot transfer to unseen tasks [Brown et al., 2020, Sanh et al., 2021, Wei et al., 2021]. Prompt-based conditioning has also been applied to EHR generation [Wang and Sun, 2022] and multi-domain time series [Gao et al., 2024], though not to discriminative clinical prediction. 

# 6 Discussion 

EveryQuery demonstrates that task-conditioned pretraining is a viable and often superior alternative to autoregressive generation for building EHR foundation models. On 39 randomly sampled tasks, 9EveryQuery outperforms an autoregressive baseline 82% of the time, with seamless generalization to held-out codes and particular advantages for rare events. 

Why Rare Events? The negative correlation between prevalence and EveryQuery’s advantage reflects a fundamental asymmetry. Decomposing the effect reveals that AR performance is strongly coupled to prevalence ( ρ = 0 .64 , p < 10 −4) while EveryQuery’s performance is prevalence-invariant (ρ = 0 .18 , p = 0 .28 ). The AR model must generate events to detect them: its trajectory-based estimates have O(1 /K ) variance that compounds for rare codes. Since EveryQuery attends to evidence for the queried code, its estimate does not depend on generating the code itself. Increasing 

K would narrow but not eliminate the gap, as the fundamental coupling between AR performance and event frequency persists. 

The Expressiveness–Efficiency Tradeoff EveryQuery’s efficiency and promptability stem from its structured query language: by asking a specific, parametrized question, the model can answer directly. But this structure imposes restriction on composite tasks—such as readmission (any of many possible admission codes), “any cardiovascular event,” or “any medication change”—would require post-hoc error-prone aggregation over many individual queries, and the aggregation may discard information that a generative model captures implicitly. This motivates a natural extension: enriching the query language to support disjunctions (“any code in set S”), conjunctions (“code A and code 

B”), negations, and numeric predicates. Each extension trades some efficiency for expressiveness. A principled approach would define a query algebra and train the model over the extended distribution. 

Prompt specificity. The embedding analysis (Section 4.5) shows that EveryQuery’s representations cluster by query rather than by patient, indicating that task conditioning reshapes the representation itself. This provides a mechanistic explanation for the OOD generalization observed in Section 4.3: novel query codes that are nearby in embedding space activate similar attention patterns, enabling transfer without explicit training on those targets. 

6.1 Limitations Query language. The current query language is limited to single-code temporal predicates, as demonstrated by the readmission failure. 

Evaluation scale. Our evaluation uses 39 random tasks and one composite task on a single dataset with one baseline. Many tasks involve rare events (prevalence < 0.5% ), and individual AUC estimates for these tasks carry substantial uncertainty. Aggregate statistics remain robust. 

Baseline configuration. The autoregressive baseline uses K = 20 trajectories per patient, which is on par with published literature but still relatively low. More trajectories would likely improve its rare-event performance, though at proportional computational cost. 

6.2 Future Work 

Extending the query language is the most natural next step: supporting disjunctions would address the readmission limitation, while conjunctions and numeric predicates would enable richer clinical queries. Multi-dataset evaluation via the MEDS standard [Xu et al., 2024] would test generalization beyond MIMIC-IV. Hybrid approaches combining EveryQuery for efficient targeted prediction with autoregressive generation for exploratory or disjunctive tasks could offer complementary strengths. Evaluating against AR baselines with more trajectories ( K ≫ 20 ) and variance-reduction techniques [Solo et al., 2026] would provide a fairer efficiency comparison. 

6.3 Conclusion 

EveryQuery introduces task-conditioned pretraining as a superior alternative to autoregressive gen-eration for EHR foundation models. By training over parametrized clinical queries, EveryQuery achieves zero-shot inference, computational efficiency, and promptability in a single architecture. Our approach outperforms autoregressive baselines on 82% of random tasks, with seamless generalization to held-out codes and particular advantages for rare events. Nevertheless, as seen by the failure 10 to predict readmission, the model remains limited by the expressiveness of the query language. Enriching the query language to support compositional tasks is the key direction for future work.
